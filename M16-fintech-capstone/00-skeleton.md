| 16.2 | **The Ledger: double-entry, immutability, debit=credit** ★ | The heart of every money system: an *append-only, immutable* double-entry ledger where every transaction is balanced (Σ debits = Σ credits) — the source of truth from which balances are derived. ★ | ★ the double-entry ledger (entries, the debit=credit invariant, derived balances) | Modeling the ledger: accounts, immutable entries, balanced transactions, derived balances (M01/M02) |
| 16.3 | **Idempotency & exactly-once** ★ | Every money operation must be *safely retryable* — an idempotency key dedups retries so a payment happens *exactly once* in effect, no matter how many times it's submitted. ★ | ★ idempotency for money (key → first applies / retry returns prior result) | A payment API: the idempotency key that prevents the double-charge on retry (M12/12.9) |
| 16.4 | **Money movement: atomic transfers, holds, two-phase capture** ★ | A transfer is an atomic debit+credit; an *authorization* places a *hold* (reserve funds) then *captures* later (two-phase) — modeling pending vs settled money. ★ | ★ money movement: transfer (atomic) · auth-hold-capture (two-phase) | A card payment: authorize (hold) → capture (settle), and the atomic transfer (M07/M08/M11) |
| 16.5 | **Consistency for money: what to read where** | Per-operation consistency: a balance-for-authorization reads *strong* (primary); a statement reads *read-your-writes*; a report reads *eventual* (replica) — never authorize against stale data. | Per-operation consistency map (strong/RYW/eventual for money reads) | Routing each payments read to its correct consistency level (M10/M12) |
| 16.6 | **Hot account contention** ★ | A popular account (a merchant, a platform float) is a *hot row* — many concurrent transfers contend on its balance; sharding balances, batching, queueing (SKIP LOCKED), and async settlement relieve it. ★ | ★ hot-account contention + the relief patterns (shard/batch/queue/async) | A merchant account taking 10k payments/sec: the hot-balance bottleneck and its fixes (M08/M11) |
| 16.7 | **Reconciliation: internal, external & drift detection** ★ | The money-never-lies backstop: independently re-derive balances (from the immutable ledger) and match against external records (the processor/bank) to *detect and repair* any drift — via the outbox/CDC pipeline. ★ | ★ the reconciliation pipeline (re-derive → match internal/external → repair drift) | The daily reconciliation catching a half-completed Saga / a missed event (M02/M12) |
| 16.8 | **Audit trails & compliance** | Immutable history for regulators: the append-only ledger *is* the audit trail; temporal/event-sourcing patterns + retention (partitioning) satisfy "who did what when" and regulatory retention. | Audit/compliance: immutable ledger + temporal + retention | Satisfying a regulator's "show every change to this account" with the immutable ledger (M01/M13) |
| 16.9 | **Multi-currency & FX** ★ | Money is per-currency *minor units* (never FLOAT); FX needs *rate snapshots* (the rate at transaction time, immutable), careful *rounding*, and separate per-currency balances — multi-currency correctness. ★ | ★ multi-currency: minor units per currency · rate snapshots · rounding | A cross-currency transfer: USD→EUR with a snapshotted rate and correct rounding (M03) |
| 16.10 | **Scaling the platform: read/write split, sharding, HTAP** | Compose the scaling toolkit: read-replicas for reads, sharding (by tenant/account, co-locating transfers) for writes, and offloading analytics (HTAP — reporting on replicas/warehouse, not the OLTP ledger). | The scaled topology (replicas + shards + analytics offload) | Scaling from one box to a sharded, replicated, analytics-offloaded platform (M10/M11) |
| 16.11 | **Failure & DR: RPO/RTO, what zero-data-loss costs** ★ | The survivability design: RPO≈0 + fast RTO via semi-sync + automated fenced failover + tested PITR + the prevention checklist — and the honest accounting of what "zero data loss" actually costs. ★ | ★ the DR posture (RPO/RTO → semi-sync/failover/PITR/prevention + the cost) | Designing the platform's DR to survive node/region loss with no lost money (M10/M13/M15) |
| 16.12 | **The outbox/CDC integration backbone** | The reliable-propagation spine: every state change emits an outbox event (atomic), CDC streams it (no dual-write), idempotent consumers feed fraud/notifications/search/the warehouse — the platform's nervous system. | The integration backbone (outbox → CDC → idempotent consumers) | The "TransferCompleted" event reliably reaching fraud, notifications, and reporting (M12) |
| 16.13 | **Common fintech anti-patterns (the money killers)** | The catastrophic mistakes specific to money systems — FLOAT money, mutable ledgers, no idempotency, stale-replica authorization, dual-write, no reconciliation — and why each loses money. | The fintech anti-pattern catalog (each → why it loses money) | Reviewing a payments design against the money-never-lies anti-patterns (M03–M15) |
| 16.14 | **The interview playbook: designing a payments system** | How to *present* a fintech design in an interview: clarify requirements → invariants → ledger → money movement → consistency → scale → DR → tradeoffs — the structured walkthrough. | The interview structure (requirements → ledger → … → tradeoffs) | A 45-minute "design a payments platform" interview, walked through |
| 16.15 | **Beyond the core: lending, settlements, wallets, marketplaces** | How the patterns extend to adjacent fintech domains — lending (schedules/interest), settlements (batch/netting), wallets (the running domain), marketplaces (split payments/escrow) — same primitives, different challenges. | Adjacent domains → the same primitives (ledger/idempotency/reconciliation) | Applying the ledger + idempotency + reconciliation to a marketplace's split payments |
