## 8.5 · Lock granularity: row vs table (and intention locks)

**Mental model.** A lock can protect *different amounts* of data — a whole **table**, or a single **row** — and the choice is a fundamental concurrency tradeoff. **Coarse (table) locks** are simple and cheap to manage but kill concurrency (one writer freezes the whole table). **Fine (row) locks** allow huge concurrency (writers to *different* rows don't block each other) but require more lock bookkeeping. InnoDB uses **row-level locking** (the reason it crushes MyISAM's table locking for write concurrency, M04/4.12). To make row locking work efficiently alongside the occasional table-level operation, InnoDB adds **intention locks** — lightweight table-level flags that say "some rows in this table are row-locked" — so a transaction wanting a *table* lock can check one flag instead of scanning every row's locks.

**How it actually works.** InnoDB's locking is **two-level (a hierarchy)**: table-level **intention locks** above row-level **record locks**. When a transaction takes a row lock (shared or exclusive, 8.6), it *first* takes a table-level **intention lock** in the matching mode — **IS** (intention shared) before a shared row lock, **IX** (intention exclusive) before an exclusive row lock. Intention locks are cheap and mostly don't conflict with each other (many transactions can hold IX on a table simultaneously — they're just declaring "I have row locks here"). Their purpose: when a transaction wants an actual **table lock** (e.g., for some DDL, or `LOCK TABLES`), it checks the intention locks — if any conflicting intention lock exists, it knows rows are locked and waits, *without* having to inspect every row. So intention locks are a fast "is anyone row-locking this table?" signal that makes the row/table hierarchy efficient. The compatibility: IS/IX are compatible with each other (multiple transactions row-locking coexist), but conflict with a full table S/X lock.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Row-level locking is *essential* for write concurrency (transfers to different accounts must not block each other), but it creates a problem: how does a transaction wanting a *table-wide* lock know whether any rows are locked, without scanning all of them (impossibly slow on a big table)? Intention locks solve this with a cheap table-level summary. So the granularity design solves the core scaling need (fine-grained row locks for concurrency) while keeping coarse-grained operations (table locks for DDL) efficient via the intention-lock hierarchy. Without intention locks, every table-level operation would have to check every row lock — the hierarchy makes both granularities coexist efficiently.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The granularity tradeoff is **concurrency vs lock overhead**: row locks maximize concurrency but cost more memory/management (one lock structure per locked row — though InnoDB stores them compactly per page); table locks are cheap to manage but serialize all writers. InnoDB chose row locking (with intention locks to manage the hierarchy) as the right default for OLTP — the concurrency win dwarfs the overhead for transactional workloads. The alternative (table locking, MyISAM) is simpler but catastrophic for concurrent writes — which is why InnoDB won. There's no "lock escalation" to table locks in InnoDB the way some databases have (it sticks with row locks), avoiding the surprise escalation that can hurt concurrency elsewhere — a deliberate design choice favoring predictable fine-grained locking.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Lock at the finest granularity that gives concurrency, and use a hierarchy of coarse 'intention' markers to coordinate with occasional coarse operations efficiently." The granularity-vs-overhead tradeoff and the **intention-lock hierarchy** are general locking concepts (multi-granularity locking is classic database theory, and the "mark the parent to summarize children" idea appears in hierarchical locking, lock managers, and even filesystem/inode locking). The transferable insight: *fine-grained locking maximizes concurrency but needs a way for coarse operations to check the fine locks cheaply* — a summary/intention level at each granularity solves it. Choosing lock granularity is choosing your concurrency/overhead point.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB does **row-level locking** with **intention locks (IS/IX) at the table level** as the hierarchy. You rarely interact with intention locks directly — they're automatic — but they appear in lock diagnostics (`SHOW ENGINE INNODB STATUS`, `performance_schema.data_locks`) and matter for understanding why a DDL/table operation waits (it conflicts with held IX intention locks → the MDL/table-lock story, 8.13). The headline reality for our domain: **transfers to *different* accounts take exclusive row locks on *different* rows and proceed fully in parallel** (row-level locking) — only transfers to the *same* account serialize (hot-row contention, 8.15). This is the foundational win of InnoDB over MyISAM (M04/4.12) and the reason a payments system can sustain high concurrent write throughput across many accounts. The rest of the taxonomy (8.6–8.9) details *what* those row locks are.

## 8.6 · Shared vs exclusive locks (the compatibility matrix) ★

**Mental model.** At the heart of all locking are **two modes**: a **shared (S) lock** for reading ("I'm reading this; others may read too, but no one may write") and an **exclusive (X) lock** for writing ("I'm writing this; no one else may read-lock or write it"). The rule is simple and is the foundation of everything: **many transactions can hold S on the same row simultaneously; only one can hold X, and X excludes everything.** This **compatibility matrix** (S compatible with S; X compatible with nothing) is *the* primitive — every higher-level lock behavior (record, gap, next-key, intention) is built on these two modes and this rule.

**How it actually works.** The compatibility matrix:

|          | holder has **S** | holder has **X** |
|----------|:----------------:|:----------------:|
| want **S** | ✓ compatible     | ✗ conflict (wait) |
| want **X** | ✗ conflict (wait)| ✗ conflict (wait) |

So: **S + S = OK** (concurrent readers/`FOR SHARE` coexist); **S + X = conflict**, **X + S = conflict**, **X + X = conflict** (a writer excludes all others, and a reader-lock blocks a writer). When a transaction wants a lock that conflicts with one already held, it **waits** (until the holder commits/rolls back and releases, 8.10) — or times out (8.12). InnoDB takes: an **X lock** for any write (UPDATE/DELETE, and the row being inserted) and for `SELECT … FOR UPDATE`; an **S lock** for `SELECT … FOR SHARE`. (Plain `SELECT` takes *no* lock — it's an MVCC snapshot read, 8.4 — which is why reads don't appear in this matrix at all under MVCC.) The matrix governs which transactions block which: two `FOR SHARE` reads on a row coexist, but a `FOR UPDATE` on it blocks them (and they block a `FOR UPDATE`).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The S/X distinction encodes the fundamental truth that **reads don't conflict with reads, but writes conflict with everything**: multiple readers of the same data are safe (they don't change it), but a writer must exclude both other writers (to avoid lost updates / corruption) and lockers-for-read (who need the value stable). This minimal two-mode system allows maximum safe concurrency: shared access where it's safe (concurrent reads), exclusive only where required (writes). It's the smallest lock vocabulary that correctly captures read/write conflict, and every richer locking scheme is built on it.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** S/X is the classic two-mode scheme; richer schemes exist (update locks, to avoid a deadlock pattern where two transactions hold S and both try to upgrade to X — some databases have a distinct "U" lock; InnoDB doesn't, so the S-then-X upgrade deadlock is a real pattern, 8.11). The tradeoff in *using* them: `FOR SHARE` (S) allows more concurrency (other readers proceed) but if you'll later write, you risk the upgrade deadlock — so for read-then-write, `FOR UPDATE` (X up front) is often safer than `FOR SHARE`-then-update. The alternative to lock modes entirely (MVCC for reads, 8.1) is why *plain* reads don't take these locks at all — the matrix only governs *writes and locking reads*. Choosing S vs X for a locking read is choosing concurrency (S) vs upgrade-safety (X).

**Generics / first-principles.** "Reads share, writes are exclusive — the readers-writer lock is the minimal correct model of concurrent access to mutable data." The shared/exclusive (readers-writer) lock is one of the most universal concurrency primitives — `RwLock` in Rust, `ReadWriteLock` in Java, shared/exclusive file locks (`flock`), and the S/X locks in every database. The compatibility matrix (S+S ok, X excludes all) is the *same* everywhere because it reflects the *same truth*: concurrent reads are safe, any write needs exclusivity. The transferable instinct: *model concurrent access as shared-for-reads, exclusive-for-writes* — and recognize the classic hazards (the shared-to-exclusive upgrade deadlock, the writer-starvation under continuous readers) that this model brings.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB row locks are **S or X**; `FOR SHARE` takes S, `FOR UPDATE` and all writes take X, plain `SELECT` takes none (MVCC, 8.4). These combine with the *type* of lock (record/gap/next-key, 8.7) — so a lock is fully described by *mode* (S/X) × *type* (record/gap/next-key) × *granularity* (with the table-level intention lock, 8.5). You see them in `performance_schema.data_locks` (e.g., `LOCK_MODE: X,REC_NOT_GAP` = exclusive record lock). The upgrade-deadlock pattern (two transactions `FOR SHARE` then both `UPDATE`) is a real InnoDB deadlock cause (8.11) — a reason to prefer `FOR UPDATE` for read-then-write. For our domain: two reconciliation reads using `FOR SHARE` on an account coexist; a transfer's `FOR UPDATE` (or write) on that account blocks them and is blocked by them — the S/X matrix deciding exactly who waits for whom. This matrix underlies every contention and deadlock scenario in the rest of the module.

## 8.7 · Record, gap & next-key locks ★

**Mental model.** InnoDB doesn't just lock *rows* — it locks **positions in an index**, and there are three flavors: a **record lock** (locks a specific existing index record), a **gap lock** (locks the *empty space between* two index records — preventing inserts there), and a **next-key lock** (a record lock + the gap *before* it — the default at REPEATABLE READ). The gap lock is the surprising, crucial one: **it locks *nothing that exists* — it locks the *possibility* of a new row appearing in a range.** This is *exactly how InnoDB prevents phantoms* at REPEATABLE READ (M07/7.13b): by locking the gaps in a range, no other transaction can *insert* a row that would become a phantom. Record/gap/next-key locking is the mechanism behind InnoDB's "stronger-than-standard RR."

**How it actually works.**
- **Record lock** — locks a single, existing index record (e.g., the index entry for `account_id = 42`). Prevents others from modifying/locking *that row*. Mode S or X (8.6).
- **Gap lock** — locks the *interval between* index records (e.g., the gap between `account_id = 40` and `45`), preventing any **insert** into that gap. It locks no existing row — it locks the *empty range*. Gap locks don't conflict with other gap locks (multiple transactions can gap-lock the same gap) — their only job is to block inserts.
- **Next-key lock** — the **combination**: a record lock on a row *plus* a gap lock on the gap *before* it. This is InnoDB's **default lock at REPEATABLE READ** for index scans — it locks both the matched records *and* the gaps around them, so a range query locks the entire range *including the spaces*, preventing both modification of existing rows *and* insertion of new matching rows.
So when a transaction does `SELECT … WHERE account_id BETWEEN 40 AND 50 FOR UPDATE` at RR, InnoDB takes **next-key locks** across that range — locking the existing matching records *and* the gaps — so no other transaction can insert a new `account_id` in [40, 50] (which would be a phantom) until you commit. At **READ COMMITTED**, InnoDB largely *disables gap locks* (only record locks), which is why RC permits phantoms (M07/7.9) and has less locking (fewer deadlocks) but weaker isolation.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Record locks alone prevent modifying *existing* rows but can't prevent *new* rows from appearing — so they can't stop **phantoms** (a new row matching your range query, M07/7.9). Gap locks solve exactly this: by locking the *ranges* (not just the rows), InnoDB prevents the inserts that would create phantoms. This is *the* mechanism that makes InnoDB's REPEATABLE READ prevent phantoms (M07/7.13b) — stronger than the SQL standard, which permits phantoms at RR. Next-key locking (record + gap) is the default that delivers this. So this concept is the concrete answer to M07's repeated "InnoDB's RR prevents phantoms via next-key locks" — *here's how*.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Gap/next-key locks **prevent phantoms but cost concurrency**: they lock *ranges*, so they block more inserts than strictly necessary (an insert anywhere in a locked gap waits, even if it wouldn't have been a "real" conflict) — and they're a major source of **deadlocks** (8.11, gap locks interacting in surprising ways). This is the central RR-vs-RC tradeoff at the mechanism level: **RR (next-key locks) = phantom prevention but more locking/deadlocks; RC (record locks only) = phantoms possible but less locking, fewer deadlocks, more concurrency.** Some high-throughput shops run READ COMMITTED specifically to avoid gap-lock contention and deadlocks (M07/7.13b), accepting weaker isolation. The alternative — preventing phantoms without gap locks (e.g., predicate locks, or serializable via other means) — is more complex; InnoDB's gap-lock approach is efficient but has the deadlock/concurrency cost. Choosing RR vs RC is largely choosing whether you want (and can afford) gap locking.

**Generics / first-principles.** "To prevent new items from appearing in a range, you must lock the *range* (the gaps), not just the existing items." Gap locking is a specific instance of the universal **predicate/range locking** problem in concurrency control: preventing phantoms requires locking *the condition* (everything matching a predicate, including not-yet-existing rows), not just the current matches. The transferable insight: *locking existing items prevents their modification but not the insertion of new matching items — to prevent phantoms / enforce a range invariant, you must lock the range itself.* This is why "no new rows matching X" is fundamentally harder to enforce than "this existing row doesn't change," and why phantom prevention is the hardest isolation guarantee. Recognizing "I need to lock a range, not just rows" is the key concurrency insight here.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB's three lock types — **record**, **gap**, **next-key** (the RR default) — appear in `performance_schema.data_locks` (`LOCK_MODE: X,REC_NOT_GAP` = record only; `X,GAP` = gap only; `X` = next-key). At **REPEATABLE READ**, locking reads/writes use **next-key locks** (→ phantom prevention, M07/7.13b); at **READ COMMITTED**, gap locks are mostly disabled (record locks only → phantoms possible, less locking). A subtlety: a unique-index equality lookup that finds a row takes just a *record* lock (no gap needed — a unique key can't have a phantom for that exact value), while a range or non-unique scan takes next-key locks. Gap locks are a top cause of "surprising" deadlocks (8.11). For our domain: a query locking a *range* of the ledger (e.g., `FOR UPDATE` over an account's recent entries) at RR takes next-key locks, blocking concurrent inserts of new entries in that range — which is exactly the phantom prevention you want for a consistent range operation, but also a contention/deadlock source to be aware of. ## 8.8 · Locks are on index records (which index matters)

**Mental model.** A subtle, high-impact reality: **InnoDB locks *index records*, not "rows" in the abstract** — so the locks a query takes depend on **which index it uses** and **how many index records it scans**, not just on how many rows it ultimately *matches*. The dangerous consequence: a query with **no usable index** scans (and *locks*) every record it examines — potentially the *entire table* — even if it matches one row. The index the optimizer chooses (M05, M06) directly determines the **lock footprint**, and a missing index turns a small update into a table-wide lock and a contention/deadlock disaster.

**How it actually works.** When InnoDB executes a locking statement (a write or `FOR UPDATE`), it locks the **index records it accesses** while finding the rows. If there's a good index on the `WHERE` column, it locks only the few matching index records (and their gaps at RR, 8.7) — a tight lock footprint. But if there's **no index** for the predicate, InnoDB must **scan the table** to find matching rows — and it takes locks on **every record it scans** (because at the storage-engine level, it locks each row it examines to evaluate the condition), not just the ones that match. So `UPDATE account SET … WHERE non_indexed_column = 'x'` might **lock every row in the table** even though it updates one — because it scanned (and locked) them all to find the match. (The optimizer's index-condition pushdown and InnoDB's "unlock non-matching rows" optimizations mitigate this somewhat at some isolation levels, but the core hazard stands: locks follow the *access path*, M06/4.8, not the *result set*.) This means the *same logical update* can have a tiny or enormous lock footprint depending purely on indexing.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** This isn't a feature to "solve" something — it's a *consequence* of how row locking works (locks are placed as rows are accessed via the index), and understanding it solves a whole class of mysterious contention and deadlock problems. It connects **indexing (M05)** directly to **concurrency**: indexes aren't just for read speed — they're for *minimizing lock footprint*. A query that's slow due to a missing index (M06) is *also* a concurrency hazard (it locks everything it scans). So this concept reveals that "add an index" often fixes contention as much as it fixes latency. It's the bridge between the performance arc (M04–M06) and the concurrency mechanism.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** There's no real tradeoff *in the behavior* — it's how InnoDB works — but it has design implications: **index your locking-statement predicates** (writes, `FOR UPDATE`) so they lock only the matching records, not the whole table. The "alternative" (a locking statement on an unindexed column) is simply a bug waiting to cause contention: it works on small tables (scanning few rows) and becomes a table-wide lock disaster as the table grows (scanning/locking everything). The cost of the index (M05/5.15, write amplification) is almost always worth it for a column used in locking predicates, precisely because the *concurrency* cost of the missing index (table-wide locking) is so severe. So the guidance is unambiguous: predicates in `UPDATE`/`DELETE`/`FOR UPDATE` should be indexed.

**Generics / first-principles.** "The cost (and lock footprint) of an operation follows its *access path*, not its *result* — touching more data to find your answer means locking (and reading) more data." This connects to the universal access-path principle (M04/4.8, M06): the work a query does is proportional to what it *examines*, not what it *returns* — and that includes locking. The transferable insight: *locks (and I/O, and CPU) are placed on what you traverse, so an inefficient access path is a concurrency problem as well as a performance problem*. A full scan doesn't just read everything slowly — under a locking statement, it *locks* everything. Recognizing "this unindexed locking query locks far more than it matches" is a key diagnostic for contention.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB locks the **index records accessed via the chosen index** (visible in `performance_schema.data_locks` — you can see *which* index and *which* records are locked). A locking statement on an **unindexed column** scans and locks every examined record (potentially the whole table) — a notorious contention/deadlock cause. The fix is M05: index the predicate columns of writes and `FOR UPDATE` reads. Connection to M06: `EXPLAIN` the locking statement — if it shows `type: ALL` (full scan), it's *also* locking everything it scans. A subtlety: even matching the *right* index, at RR the next-key locks (8.7) extend to gaps, so the locked footprint is "the index range scanned" — keep the scanned range tight. For our domain: a transfer's `UPDATE account SET balance = … WHERE account_id = 42` uses the PK (a tight record lock on one row — minimal footprint, good); but a poorly-written `UPDATE ledger_entry SET … WHERE some_unindexed_flag = 1` would scan and lock huge swaths of the ledger — a contention bomb. Indexing your locking predicates is as much a concurrency requirement as a performance one.

## 8.9 · Insert-intention & auto-increment locks

**Mental model.** Two special-purpose locks for **inserts**. An **insert-intention lock** is a *special gap lock* that signals "I intend to insert here" — and crucially, **multiple transactions can hold insert-intention locks on the *same* gap simultaneously** as long as they're inserting at *different* positions, so concurrent inserts into the same range don't needlessly block each other. An **auto-increment lock** serializes the assignment of `AUTO_INCREMENT` values (so two concurrent inserts get distinct, ordered IDs) — and its *mode* (how long it's held) determines how much concurrent inserts contend on it. Both are niche but matter for **insert-heavy workloads** like the ever-growing ledger.

**How it actually works.**
- **Insert-intention lock** — before inserting a row, a transaction takes an insert-intention lock on the gap where the new row will go (8.7). Unlike a regular gap lock, **insert-intention locks don't conflict with each other** (two transactions inserting *different* values into the same gap both proceed) — they only conflict with a *gap lock* held by another transaction (which is blocking inserts into that gap, e.g., for phantom prevention, 8.7). So insert-intention locks let concurrent inserts into a range coexist, *unless* someone is gap-locking that range. This is why a transaction doing a range `FOR UPDATE` (taking next-key/gap locks) *blocks* concurrent inserts into that range (the inserts' insert-intention locks conflict with the gap lock) — the mechanism behind "range lock blocks new rows" (8.7).
- **Auto-increment lock** — to give each insert a unique, increasing auto-inc value, InnoDB serializes value assignment. The **lock mode** (`innodb_autoinc_lock_mode`) controls how: mode 0 (traditional) holds a table-level auto-inc lock for the whole statement (most serialization, least concurrency); mode 1 (consecutive) holds it more briefly for simple inserts; **mode 2 (interleaved, the default in 8.0)** doesn't hold a table-level lock for individual inserts (maximum concurrency, but auto-inc values may be non-consecutive across concurrent inserts — fine unless you depend on gapless IDs). Higher modes = more insert concurrency, at the cost of consecutiveness guarantees.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** **Insert-intention locks** solve "let concurrent inserts into the same range proceed without blocking each other, while still respecting gap locks that prevent phantoms" — without them, every insert into a gap would block every other insert into that gap (terrible for insert-heavy tables). **Auto-increment locks** solve "give each concurrent insert a unique, monotonic ID" — necessary for auto-inc PKs, but a potential *serialization point* (all inserts to a table contend on its auto-inc), which the lock modes exist to relax. Both target **insert concurrency**, which is exactly what an append-heavy ledger needs (M03/3.12 — monotonic keys, high insert rate).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The auto-inc lock is a real **contention point for insert-heavy tables**: every insert to a table needs an auto-inc value, so they serialize on that assignment. The lock-mode tradeoff is **concurrency vs consecutiveness**: interleaved mode (default 8.0) maximizes concurrency but allows gaps/non-consecutive IDs across concurrent inserts (and behaves differently with statement-based replication — a reason it interacts with binlog format, M10). If you need gapless or strictly-consecutive IDs (you usually shouldn't — gaps are fine for surrogate keys, M01/1.4), you pay concurrency. The alternative to auto-inc entirely — application-generated IDs (UUIDv7/ULID, Snowflake, M03/3.12) — *avoids the auto-inc lock contention* (no central sequence) at the cost of larger keys, which is one reason high-insert-rate distributed systems prefer them. So the auto-inc lock is a (usually minor) contention cost of the convenience of database-assigned monotonic IDs.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Allow concurrent appends that don't truly conflict (different positions) to proceed, and minimize the serialization of any shared counter (the sequence)." Insert-intention locks embody "don't block operations that don't actually conflict" (concurrent inserts at different points are independent); auto-inc lock modes embody "a shared monotonic counter is a serialization point — relax it as much as correctness allows." Both are instances of the universal *minimize the critical section around shared resources* principle. The transferable insight: *a centralized sequence/counter is a concurrency bottleneck under high append rates* — which is exactly why distributed systems move to coordination-free IDs (timestamp-based, partitioned counters). Recognizing the auto-inc lock as "a shared counter all inserts contend on" explains a class of insert-throughput ceilings.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB: **insert-intention locks** (special gap locks that permit concurrent inserts but conflict with gap locks); **`innodb_autoinc_lock_mode`** (0 traditional / 1 consecutive / **2 interleaved, the 8.0 default**). The auto-inc lock is a real contention point on very-high-insert-rate single tables. Mitigations: interleaved mode (default), or coordination-free keys (ULID/UUIDv7/Snowflake, M03/3.12) to eliminate the central sequence entirely — a reason the *ledger* (highest insert rate) might use a time-ordered key instead of a single auto-inc sequence at extreme scale, or be sharded so each shard has its own sequence (M11/M16). The interleaved mode's interaction with **statement-based binlog** (M10) is a known gotcha (non-deterministic auto-inc order) — row-based binlog avoids it. For our domain: the ever-growing `ledger_entry` is insert-heavy, so auto-inc lock behavior (or choosing a coordination-free key) is a real throughput consideration at scale — connecting M03/3.12's key-choice to M08's insert-concurrency and M11/M16's sharding.

## 8.10 · Two-phase locking & lock lifetime

**Mental model.** InnoDB follows **strict two-phase locking (2PL)**: a transaction **acquires** locks as it needs them (the "growing" phase) and **releases them all at once at commit/rollback** (no early release) — it *never* releases a lock and then acquires another. The load-bearing consequence: **a lock's lifetime = the transaction's lifetime.** Every row a transaction locks stays locked until that transaction *ends*. This is *why* transaction duration matters so much (M07/7.15): a long transaction holds its locks for a long time, blocking everyone who needs those rows for the *entire* duration. "Locks are held to commit" is the rule that makes short transactions a concurrency necessity, not just a nicety.

**How it actually works.** **Two-phase locking** has two phases: a **growing phase** (acquire locks, never release) and a **shrinking phase** (release locks, never acquire). *Strict* 2PL (what InnoDB uses) makes the shrinking phase *atomic at commit*: all locks are held until the transaction commits or rolls back, then released together. So InnoDB acquires locks lazily *as statements execute* (a row lock when you update a row, a next-key lock when you `FOR UPDATE` a range, 8.7) and holds *every* acquired lock until the final `COMMIT`/`ROLLBACK`. There's no way to release a lock early (no "I'm done with this row, unlock it mid-transaction"). This guarantees **serializability of the lock order** (strict 2PL is what makes lock-based concurrency correct — it prevents a transaction from releasing a lock that another could grab to create an inconsistent interleaving) and ensures **recoverability** (locks held to commit means no one reads your uncommitted writes). The cost is that lock duration is bounded only by transaction duration — hence the M07/7.15 imperative to keep transactions short.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Strict 2PL is *the* classic mechanism that makes lock-based concurrency **correct** — holding locks until commit guarantees that the concurrent execution is equivalent to some serial order (serializable, for the locked accesses) and that no transaction depends on another's uncommitted state (recoverable, cascadeless). Releasing locks early would allow another transaction to act on data you might still roll back, or create non-serializable interleavings. So 2PL solves "how do locks guarantee correct isolation?" The "held to commit" property is non-negotiable for correctness — which means the *only* lever for reducing lock-holding time is **reducing transaction duration** (M07/7.15). This concept is the mechanism behind "keep transactions short."

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Strict 2PL's correctness comes at a **concurrency cost**: locks held to commit means longer transactions block more, for longer. The tension is fundamental — you can't release locks early without risking correctness, so the *only* mitigation is short transactions (M07/7.15) and fine-grained locks (8.5, lock less). Alternatives to 2PL exist (optimistic concurrency control — no locks during execution, validate at commit, M07/7.12; or MVCC for reads, 8.1, which avoids read locks entirely) — and InnoDB *uses* MVCC precisely to keep *reads* out of 2PL (reads don't take locks, so they don't suffer the held-to-commit cost). So InnoDB's design is "2PL for writes (correct, held to commit) + MVCC for reads (no locks)" — getting 2PL's write correctness without paying its cost on reads. The remaining lever is transaction length.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Hold locks until commit to guarantee serializability — so the duration you hold the critical section is bounded only by how long your transaction runs." Two-phase locking is foundational concurrency theory (the proof that 2PL → serializable is a cornerstone of database theory), and "strict 2PL holds locks to commit" is *the* reason critical-section duration matters. The transferable insight: *in any lock-based system, correctness often requires holding locks until your unit of work completes — so the way to reduce contention is to make units of work short, not to release locks early (which breaks correctness)*. This is why "keep your critical section / transaction / locked region short" is universal advice — the lock duration is dictated by correctness, so the only knob is the duration of the work.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB uses **strict 2PL for locks** (acquired as needed, all released at commit/rollback) — combined with **MVCC for non-locking reads** (8.1, so reads don't participate in 2PL). The practical consequences are everywhere in this resource: **locks held to commit** → long transactions block others for their whole duration (M07/7.15) and pin undo (8.2/M09) → the imperative to keep transactions short and do no slow/external work inside them (M07/7.15). You can't unlock a row mid-transaction. Detection: a transaction holding a lock that others wait on appears in `performance_schema.data_lock_waits` and `INNODB_TRX` (M07/7.15). For our domain: a transfer takes an X lock on the hot account's balance row when it updates it, and **holds that lock until commit** — so every *other* transfer to that account waits until this one commits (hot-row contention, 8.15). This is precisely why the transfer transaction must be short (M07/7.16) and why hot-account contention is *the* fintech concurrency problem (8.15) — the 2PL "held to commit" rule turns transaction duration directly into contention.
