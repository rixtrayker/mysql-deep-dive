## 9.1 · The fast-and-durable problem (why WAL exists) ★

**Mental model.** Here's the tension every durable database must resolve: **durability says "a committed change must be on disk," but writing the changed *data pages* to their final disk locations on every commit is far too slow** — those pages are scattered all over the disk (random I/O, the slowest thing storage does), and a single transaction might dirty many of them. The resolution is **write-ahead logging (WAL)**: instead of writing the scattered pages, write a compact description of *the change* **sequentially** to a **redo log** and **fsync just that** (one fast sequential write), then update the page *in memory* (the buffer pool) and flush it to its real home **lazily, later, in the background.** A commit becomes durable in ~one sequential log write instead of many random page writes — fast *and* crash-safe, because the log can always reconstruct the pages.

**How it actually works.** The naive approach — "on COMMIT, write every dirty page to disk" — fails on two counts: (1) data pages are at *random* locations (the clustered index, secondary indexes, all over the tablespace), so it's random I/O; (2) you'd write the *whole 16KB page* even for a tiny change. WAL inverts this: at commit, InnoDB appends a small **redo log record** ("page P, offset O, changed to value V") to the **redo log** — which is a *sequential, append-only* file (fast — sequential I/O is orders of magnitude faster than random) — and **fsyncs the redo log** so it's on stable storage. The actual data page is updated *in the buffer pool* (RAM) and marked **dirty**; it's written to its real disk location *later*, asynchronously, batched with other dirty pages (turning many random writes into fewer, smoother, background ones). **Durability is satisfied the moment the redo is fsync'd** — because if the server crashes before the dirty page reaches disk, **crash recovery replays the redo log** to re-apply the change. So: log-first (durable), pages-later (lazy). This is the foundational trick the whole module elaborates.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** WAL resolves the apparent contradiction between **durability** (must be on disk at commit) and **performance** (can't afford random page writes per commit). It turns the per-commit cost from "many random writes" into "one sequential log write + fsync," and it lets page writes be *deferred and batched* (absorbing many in-memory updates to a hot page into a single eventual disk write — write *combining*). Without WAL, a transactional database would be unusably slow (every commit = a storm of random I/O) or unsafe (skip the writes, lose data on crash). WAL is *the* mechanism that makes a database both fast and crash-safe — it's the single most important idea in this module, and the reason a payments system can commit thousands of durable transfers per second.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** WAL's cost is **the fsync on commit** (forcing the redo log to stable storage is still the biggest per-commit expense, 9.8 — but it's *one sequential* fsync, vastly cheaper than random page writes) and **the need for crash recovery** (replay the log on restart, 9.14). The redo log also consumes space and must be sized (9.6/9.13). The "alternatives": *no logging* (flush pages on commit — too slow, or skip flushing — not durable); *shadow paging* (write new pages, atomically switch — used by some systems, avoids logging but has its own costs). Log-structured approaches (LSM-trees) take WAL further (everything is a log). WAL (redo log + lazy page flush) is the dominant design for B+Tree databases precisely because it gives sequential-write durability + deferred/batched page writes — the best of both. The remaining tradeoff is the durability/throughput dial on *how strictly* to fsync (9.10).

**Generics / first-principles.** "To make a change durable cheaply, append a description of it to a sequential log and sync *that*, then apply it to the real (scattered) data lazily — the log can always replay." Write-ahead logging is one of the most important and universal ideas in systems: **filesystem journals** (ext4/XFS journal the metadata change before applying it), **Kafka** (the log *is* the data), **Raft/Paxos** (a replicated log of operations), **event sourcing** (M01/1.17 — the event log is truth, state is derived). The deep principle: **sequential writes are cheap and a log is replayable, so log-then-apply gives durability without paying random-write cost per operation.** The transferable instinct: *when you need durable-but-fast writes to scattered state, log the intent sequentially and apply lazily* — you've seen this pattern in M01/1.17 (append-only ledger), M08/8.2 (undo log), and it recurs everywhere.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB's WAL is the **redo log** (9.4) + the **buffer pool** (9.2, where pages are updated and held dirty) + lazy **flushing** (9.6/9.13) + **crash recovery** (9.14). A commit fsyncs the redo log (governed by `innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`, 9.10) and returns; the dirty data pages are flushed in the background. This is why InnoDB is both durable and fast, and it's the mechanism behind M07/7.5's "COMMIT means durable." For our domain: a transfer's COMMIT writes its redo records sequentially, fsyncs them, and returns "durable" — the `account`/`ledger_entry` pages it dirtied in the buffer pool are written to their real homes later (and if the server crashes in between, recovery replays the redo). Everything else in M09 is the detail of *how each piece works* — and *where it can go wrong* (9.7–9.10).

## 9.2 · The buffer pool: caching pages in RAM ★

**Mental model.** The **buffer pool** is a large region of RAM where InnoDB caches **pages** (data and index, 16KB each, 9.3) — and it's the single most important performance structure in the engine. The principle: **most reads and writes should hit RAM, never disk.** When a query needs a page, InnoDB checks the buffer pool first; if it's there (a *hit*), no disk I/O. Writes go to the buffer-pool copy (marked *dirty*) and are flushed to disk later (lazily, 9.1). Disk (even SSD) is orders of magnitude slower than RAM, so the fraction of accesses served from the buffer pool — the **hit rate** — largely *is* your database's performance. A correctly-sized buffer pool that holds your *working set* (the hot data) means the database runs at memory speed; one too small means constant disk I/O.

**How it actually works.** The buffer pool holds pages and manages them with an **LRU (least-recently-used) list** — but a *modified* LRU split into a **young (new) sublist** and an **old sublist** to resist *pollution*. A newly-read page enters the *old* sublist (not the front of young); only if it's accessed *again* (after a short delay) does it get promoted to the *young* sublist. This protects the hot working set from being evicted by a one-time large scan (a full table scan reads many pages once — they enter "old" and get evicted without displacing the genuinely-hot "young" pages). **Dirty pages** (modified but not yet flushed) are tracked on a **flush list** (ordered by their oldest modification, for checkpointing, 9.6); a background thread flushes them. The **change buffer** (9.13) defers/batches modifications to *secondary index* pages not currently in the pool. Reads check the pool (hit → RAM; miss → read the page from disk into the pool, evicting an old-sublist page if full). The buffer pool also caches the *adaptive hash index* (M05/5.14) and other structures. Its size (`innodb_buffer_pool_size`) is the dominant memory-tuning knob — typically set to a large fraction (e.g., 50–75%) of system RAM on a dedicated DB server.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The buffer pool solves the **storage-speed gap**: disk I/O is the bottleneck (RAM is ~100,000× faster than disk seeks, and even SSDs are far slower than RAM), so caching the working set in memory is what makes a database usable at scale. It turns "every query reads from disk" (catastrophic) into "most queries read from RAM" (fast). It also enables WAL's lazy-flush (9.1) — writes accumulate in the pool and are batched to disk — and absorbs *repeated* writes to a hot page into one eventual flush (write combining). Without a buffer pool (or with one too small for the working set), the database is disk-bound and slow no matter how good the indexes are. It's *the* reason "keep rows/keys compact" (M03/3.2) matters — smaller data → more of the working set fits in the buffer pool → higher hit rate.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The buffer pool trades **RAM for I/O avoidance** — bigger pool = higher hit rate = less disk I/O, bounded by available RAM (and you must leave RAM for the OS, connections, other buffers). Too small → working set spills → disk-bound; too big → starves the OS/other processes (or swapping, which is catastrophic). The young/old LRU split trades a little complexity for **scan resistance** (the alternative — plain LRU — lets a big scan evict the hot set, a classic problem). Dirty pages held in the pool trade *crash-recovery time* (more unflushed changes = more redo to replay, 9.14) for *write smoothing* — managed by checkpointing/adaptive flushing (9.6/9.13). The alternative to a buffer pool (relying on the OS page cache, as MyISAM partly did) is worse — InnoDB manages its own cache with database-aware policies (dirty tracking, the LRU split, the change buffer) that the generic OS cache can't.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Cache the working set in fast memory; serve most accesses from it; the hit rate determines performance." The buffer pool is the database instance of the **universal caching / memory-hierarchy principle**: every layer of the storage hierarchy (CPU cache → RAM → SSD → disk) caches the hotter data from the slower layer below, and performance is dominated by how often you hit the faster layer. The young/old LRU split is the universal *scan-resistant cache* idea (protect frequently-reused items from one-time bulk accesses — the same as MySQL's old query cache lessons, OS page cache heuristics, CDN cache policies). The transferable instinct: *identify your working set, size your cache to hold it, and protect the hot set from cache pollution* — the buffer pool, a CPU cache, and a Redis layer all live or die by this.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB's buffer pool: `innodb_buffer_pool_size` (the key knob — size it to hold the working set, often 50–75% of RAM on a dedicated server), `innodb_buffer_pool_instances` (split into instances to reduce mutex contention), the young/old LRU split (`innodb_old_blocks_pct`/`_time` tune scan resistance). Monitor the **hit rate** and dirty-page ratio via `SHOW ENGINE INNODB STATUS` and `information_schema.INNODB_BUFFER_POOL_STATS` (M13). The buffer pool caches data pages, index pages, the adaptive hash index, the change buffer, and more. Crucially, it's the cache the **removed query cache** never was (M04/4.14) — it caches *pages* (reused by many queries), not results. For our domain: a hot account's `account` and `ledger_entry` pages stay resident in the young sublist (frequently accessed), so balance reads and statement queries run at memory speed; a nightly full-ledger reconciliation scan reads many pages *once* — they enter the old sublist and are evicted without polluting the hot set (scan resistance). The buffer-pool hit rate is the #1 health metric of an InnoDB database, and the ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the LRU young/old structure + change buffer + dirty pages.

## 9.3 · Pages, tablespaces & the on-disk layout

**Mental model.** Zooming out to the physical organization: **rows live in 16KB pages** (M05/5.3), pages are grouped into **tablespaces** (the actual files on disk), the **buffer pool caches** those pages in RAM (9.2), and the **logs describe changes** to them (9.4/9.5). This is the layered physical model: tablespace (file) → pages (16KB units) → rows. Knowing it grounds everything else — the buffer pool caches *these* pages, the redo log records changes to *these* pages, crash recovery re-applies redo to *these* pages.

**How it actually works.** A **page** (16KB default) is InnoDB's atomic unit of storage and I/O (9.1/9.2/M05·5.3) — it holds rows (in a clustered-index leaf), index entries, or engine metadata, with a header (page number, type, LSN — 9.6), the records, and a page directory. Pages are grouped into **extents** (1MB = 64 pages) and **segments**, which live in **tablespaces** — files on disk. InnoDB's tablespace organization: **file-per-table** (`innodb_file_per_table=ON`, the modern default) gives each table its own `.ibd` file (easier to manage, reclaim space, move tables); a **system/shared tablespace** (`ibdata1`) historically held everything and still holds some internal structures (the doublewrite buffer historically, undo logs depending on version, the data dictionary). There are also **undo tablespaces** (holding undo logs, 9.5) and the **redo log** files (a separate, fixed set of files, 9.4 — *not* a tablespace, a circular log). So on disk: per-table `.ibd` files (the data), undo tablespaces (old versions), the redo log files (the WAL), and system files — and the buffer pool caches pages from the data files while the logs sit separately.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The page-and-tablespace organization is what makes storage **manageable and efficient**: fixed-size pages enable uniform I/O and caching (9.1/9.2); grouping into extents/segments enables efficient sequential allocation and read-ahead (reading a whole extent at once); file-per-table enables per-table management (drop a table → reclaim its file; move/backup a single table; per-table compression/encryption). Separating the *data* files from the *log* files (redo, undo) is essential to WAL (9.1) — the log is sequential and small-and-fast, the data is large and scattered, and they have different access patterns and durability roles. This physical layout is the substrate the whole engine operates on.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** File-per-table (modern default) vs shared tablespace: file-per-table eases management and space reclamation but uses more file handles and can have slightly more overhead for many small tables; the shared tablespace pools everything (fewer files) but never shrinks (deleted data leaves holes) and is harder to manage. Page size (16KB default, configurable to 4/8/32/64KB) trades fanout/sequential-throughput vs random-I/O efficiency (M05/5.3) — 16KB is the general default. Larger extents enable better read-ahead but coarser allocation. These are mostly set-and-forget defaults; the main practical choice is file-per-table (keep it on — the default) and *not* over-thinking page size.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Organize storage in fixed-size blocks, grouped into larger allocation units, in files — separating data from logs by access pattern." This is the universal physical-storage organization (filesystems use blocks → extents → files; every block store has this hierarchy). The separation of *sequential logs* from *scattered data files* by access pattern is a recurring storage-engine design (logs are append-only and synced; data is random-access and cached). The transferable instinct: *fixed blocks for uniform I/O/caching, larger units for efficient allocation/prefetch, and separate your sequential append-only logs from your random-access data* — the same shape in databases, filesystems, and log-structured stores.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB: 16KB pages (`innodb_page_size`), extents (1MB), segments; **file-per-table** (`.ibd` files, default on) vs the system tablespace (`ibdata1`); undo tablespaces; the redo log files (`#innodb_redo` in 8.0.30+, previously `ib_logfile*`). `information_schema.INNODB_TABLESPACES`/`FILES` expose the layout. For our domain, `ledger_entry` lives in its own `ledger_entry.ibd` (file-per-table) as a tree of 16KB pages (the clustered index, M01/1.3, M05/5.4); the buffer pool caches its hot pages (9.2); the redo log (separate files) records changes to those pages (9.4); undo tablespaces hold the old versions MVCC reads (9.5, M08/8.2). This physical picture is the ground truth under everything — the buffer pool caches *these* pages, the logs change *these* pages, recovery restores *these* pages.

## 9.4 · The redo log (WAL): how commits become durable ★

**Mental model.** The **redo log** is InnoDB's write-ahead log: a **sequential, circular, fsync'd record of physical changes to pages.** It's the heart of durability (9.1): **a commit is durable the instant its redo records are on stable storage** — even though the actual data pages it changed are still only in the buffer pool (dirty, not yet on disk). The redo log's job is to make commits durable *cheaply* (one sequential write + fsync, not random page writes) and to enable *recovery* (replay the log to re-apply any committed change that didn't reach its data page before a crash). It records *redo* — "how to re-do the change" — so recovery can reconstruct the post-commit state.

**How it actually works.** As transactions modify pages (in the buffer pool), InnoDB appends **redo log records** describing each physical change ("page P, this byte range became this") to an in-memory **log buffer**, then to the **redo log files** on disk. The redo log is **circular** (a fixed set of files reused in a loop) and **append-only/sequential** (fast I/O). Each change gets an **LSN** (Log Sequence Number, 9.6 — a monotonic offset into the log). At **commit**, InnoDB ensures the transaction's redo records (up to its commit LSN) are **flushed to the log files and fsync'd** to stable storage (governed by `innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`, 9.10) — *then* the commit returns. The data pages remain dirty in the buffer pool and are flushed lazily (9.1/9.6/9.13). Because the redo log is circular and fixed-size, InnoDB can only reuse a log region once the corresponding dirty pages have been **checkpointed** (flushed to disk) — so the redo log size and checkpointing bound how much un-flushed work can accumulate (9.6/9.13). On **recovery** (9.14), InnoDB replays the redo log from the last checkpoint forward, re-applying every change to bring data pages up to the last committed state.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The redo log is *the* durability mechanism — it's what makes "COMMIT means durable" (M07/7.5) true *and* fast. It solves the WAL problem (9.1): commit durability via a single sequential fsync rather than random page writes. It also bounds crash-recovery work (only changes since the last checkpoint must be replayed) and enables write-combining/deferral (pages flushed lazily and batched). Without it, durability would require flushing every dirty page on commit (too slow) and crash recovery would be impossible (no record of un-flushed committed changes). It's the single structure that reconciles "fast commits" with "nothing committed is ever lost."

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The redo log's central tradeoff is the **fsync-on-commit** (durability vs throughput, the flush matrix, 9.10) — and its **size** (`innodb_redo_log_capacity`): a *larger* redo log lets more dirty pages accumulate before checkpointing forces a flush, smoothing writes and improving throughput under bursts — but means *longer crash recovery* (more redo to replay) and more disk space; a *smaller* redo log recovers faster but forces more frequent checkpointing (more aggressive flushing, possible stalls when it fills, 9.13). The modern default leans toward a generously-sized redo log for write-heavy workloads. The redo log is *physical* (records page-level byte changes) — vs the *binlog* (M10), which is *logical/statement-or-row* level and serves replication, not crash recovery; the two are different logs with different jobs (and must be kept consistent, 9.11). Alternatives to physical redo (logical undo/redo, shadow paging) exist but InnoDB's physical redo is efficient for recovery.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Make durability cheap by appending the change to a sequential log and syncing *that*; reconstruct the real (lazily-written) state by replaying the log." The redo log is the canonical **write-ahead log** (9.1's principle made concrete) — the same structure as a filesystem journal, a database transaction log, or a replicated consensus log: *log the operation durably before (or at) commit, apply it to the slower scattered state lazily, replay on recovery.* The **circular/checkpointed** design (a fixed log reused once its changes are applied) is the universal bounded-log pattern (you can't keep an infinite log; you reclaim space once the data has caught up). The transferable instinct: *a sequential, synced, replayable log is how you get cheap durability over expensive-to-write state* — and you bound it with checkpoints.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB's redo log: circular files (`#innodb_redo` directory in 8.0.30+, sized by `innodb_redo_log_capacity`; previously `ib_logfile0/1` sized by `innodb_log_file_size` × `innodb_log_files_in_group`), an in-memory `innodb_log_buffer` before the files, LSN-ordered. **`innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`** governs the commit fsync (9.10): `1` = flush+fsync every commit (durable — the money default); `2` = flush to OS cache every commit, fsync ~once/sec (faster, OS-crash-safe but power-loss can lose ~1s); `0` = flush+fsync ~once/sec (fastest, a crash loses ~1s). Recovery replays redo automatically on startup (9.14). For our domain: a transfer's COMMIT flushes+fsyncs its redo records (at `=1`) before returning "durable" — so even if power is lost a microsecond later, recovery replays the redo and the transfer survives (M07/7.5 realized). Sizing the redo log generously (so write bursts don't cause checkpoint stalls, 9.13) and keeping `flush_log_at_trx_commit=1` (durability) are core to a payments-system durability posture (9.16). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the circular redo log + LSN + the commit fsync.

## 9.5 · The undo log: old versions for rollback & MVCC

**Mental model.** The **undo log** is the mirror image of the redo log: where redo records "how to *re-do* a change" (for durability/recovery), undo records "how to *un-do* a change" (the *previous* value). And here's the elegant part — that single structure serves **two** purposes at once: **atomicity/rollback** (M07/7.2 — to roll back a transaction, apply its undo records in reverse to restore the prior state) *and* **MVCC** (M08/8.2 — the old row versions a reader walks to see its snapshot *are* the undo records). The undo log is where "old versions of rows" physically live — it's the same machinery powering both "undo a failed transfer" and "let a concurrent reader see the pre-transfer balance."

**How it actually works.** When a transaction modifies a row, InnoDB writes an **undo log record** capturing the row's *previous* version (enough to reconstruct it), and the row's hidden `DB_ROLL_PTR` (M08/8.2) points at it — building the **version chain**. This undo serves both roles: **(1) Rollback (atomicity):** if the transaction rolls back (explicitly, or on error/crash before commit), InnoDB applies its undo records in reverse, restoring every modified row to its pre-transaction state (M07/7.2) — and crash recovery rolls back in-flight transactions this way (9.14). **(2) MVCC (isolation):** a concurrent reader whose snapshot predates the modification walks the `DB_ROLL_PTR` chain into the undo log to find the *old* version visible to its read view (M08/8.2/8.3) — so the undo records *are* the old versions MVCC serves. Undo lives in **undo tablespaces** (9.3). Crucially, undo records can only be removed (by the **purge thread**, 9.12) once **no active transaction's snapshot might still need them** *and* no rollback could need them — which is why a long transaction (holding an old snapshot) *pins* undo and causes history-list bloat (9.12, M07/7.15, M08/8.2).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The undo log solves *two* problems with *one* structure: **atomicity** (you can't roll back without knowing the prior state — undo is that knowledge) and **MVCC** (non-blocking consistent reads need old versions to read — undo is those versions). It's a beautiful unification: the "previous value" you need to undo a change is *exactly* the "old version" a snapshot reader needs. This is why InnoDB's MVCC (M08) and rollback (M07/7.2) share machinery, and why the same "long transaction pins old data" problem (9.12) affects both. Without undo, there'd be no rollback (no atomicity) and no MVCC (reads would have to lock). It's the structural foundation of two ACID properties (A and the I-via-MVCC).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Undo's cost is **storage and maintenance**: old versions accumulate and must be purged (9.12); a long transaction pinning undo causes **history-list bloat** (undo storage grows, version chains lengthen, reads slow — M07/7.15) — the central undo-related performance problem. The tradeoff is implicit in MVCC (M08/8.1): you keep old versions (undo) to get non-blocking reads + rollback, at the cost of version storage and purge. The "alternative" to undo-based MVCC (lock-based reads, no versions) avoids the storage but destroys read concurrency. Undo storage configuration (undo tablespaces, truncation) is mostly automatic in modern InnoDB. The key operational tradeoff is *transaction length* (M07/7.15): short transactions let undo be purged promptly; long ones bloat it.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Record the previous state so you can both undo (roll back) and time-travel (read old versions) — one log of prior values serves both atomicity and isolation." The undo log embodies the insight that *rollback information and historical-version information are the same thing* — a unification that appears in any system needing both transactional rollback and snapshot reads (MVCC databases generally; even Git's reflog/history serves both "undo" and "view past" roles). The deep principle: **keeping the prior state of mutations gives you both reversibility and point-in-time reads.** The transferable instinct: *if you keep enough to undo a change, you also keep enough to show the state before it* — recognizing this unification (one structure, two uses) is the elegant core of MVCC + rollback.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB undo logs live in **undo tablespaces** (separate, automatically managed in modern versions, truncatable). The `DB_ROLL_PTR` per row threads the version chain (M08/8.2); undo serves rollback (M07/7.2) and MVCC (M08/8.2/8.3). The **purge thread** (9.12) removes undo no longer needed; **history list length** (`SHOW ENGINE INNODB STATUS`) is the key metric — it bloats when a long transaction pins old versions (M07/7.15, M08/8.2 — the mechanism M08 deferred to here). For our domain: when a transfer updates a balance, the *old* balance goes to undo — and that *same* undo record both rolls back the transfer if it fails (atomicity) and lets a concurrent reconciliation read see the pre-transfer balance (MVCC, M08/8.2). A forgotten long reconciliation transaction would pin undo (its snapshot needs the old versions), blocking purge → history-list blowup → undo bloat → slower reads (9.12). This is the storage-level mechanism behind M07/7.15 ("keep transactions short") and M08/8.2 ("long txn pins versions"). Undo management/purge is concept 9.12.

## 9.6 · The LSN, checkpointing & flushing

**Mental model.** The **LSN (Log Sequence Number)** is a monotonically-increasing number that **orders every change in the system** — it's an offset into the redo log, stamped on every redo record and on every page (the page's LSN says "this page reflects changes up to here"). **Checkpointing** is the process of tracking how far the redo log has been *applied to data pages* (flushed to disk): the **checkpoint LSN** marks "all changes up to here are safely in the data files, so this redo can be reused." **Flushing** is writing dirty pages from the buffer pool to disk, which advances the checkpoint. Together, LSN + checkpoint govern two critical things: **crash-recovery time** (recovery replays redo from the checkpoint LSN forward — so the further behind the checkpoint, the longer recovery) and **redo-log reuse** (the circular redo log can only overwrite regions whose changes are checkpointed). The gap between the newest LSN and the checkpoint LSN — the **checkpoint age** — is the amount of un-flushed work, and managing it is what keeps writes smooth (9.13).

**How it actually works.** Every redo record and every page modification advances the **LSN**. A page's header stores the LSN of the last change applied to it. The **checkpoint** mechanism periodically (and continuously, "fuzzy checkpointing") flushes dirty pages and records a **checkpoint LSN** = the oldest LSN of any still-dirty page (everything older has been flushed). The **flush list** orders dirty pages by their oldest modification LSN, so InnoDB flushes the oldest-modified pages first to advance the checkpoint. **Checkpoint age** = current LSN − checkpoint LSN = how much redo would need replaying on crash / how full the circular redo log is. InnoDB watches this: as checkpoint age grows (the redo log fills with un-checkpointed changes), it **flushes more aggressively** (adaptive flushing, 9.13) to advance the checkpoint and free redo space — and if it gets dangerously full, it can force a hard flush (a **stall**, 9.13). On **recovery** (9.14): InnoDB reads the checkpoint LSN, then replays redo from there forward (re-applying changes to pages whose LSN is behind the redo) — pages already flushed past a redo record's LSN are skipped (idempotent replay via LSN comparison).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** LSN + checkpointing solve two problems: **(1)** they make crash recovery *bounded and correct* — recovery only replays redo since the last checkpoint (not the entire log history), and the per-page LSN makes replay *idempotent* (a change is only re-applied if the page's LSN shows it wasn't yet applied); **(2)** they enable the **circular redo log** (9.4) — redo space can be reused once checkpointed, so the log stays a fixed size. The checkpoint age is the *control variable* for the write path: keeping it bounded (via flushing) keeps recovery fast and the redo log from filling. Without checkpointing, recovery would have to replay all history (impossible) and the redo log couldn't be bounded.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The core tradeoff is **checkpoint age (recovery time + redo fullness) vs flush aggressiveness (write I/O)**: a *higher* checkpoint age (lazier flushing, larger redo log) means smoother writes and better burst absorption but longer recovery and risk of the redo filling; a *lower* checkpoint age (aggressive flushing) means fast recovery but more constant write I/O. InnoDB's **adaptive flushing** (9.13) tries to flush *just enough* to keep checkpoint age in a safe band without over-flushing — the balance. Redo log size (9.4) sets the headroom. The alternative (sharp/non-fuzzy checkpoints — flush everything periodically) causes write spikes; InnoDB's continuous fuzzy checkpointing smooths them. The tuning surface (redo size, IO capacity, flush settings) is about keeping this balance for your workload.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Stamp every change with a monotonic sequence number; track how far the durable log has been applied to the real data (the checkpoint); replay only the gap on recovery, idempotently via the sequence number." LSN + checkpointing is the universal **log-replay-with-checkpoints** pattern: a monotonic sequence orders operations, a checkpoint marks "applied up to here," and recovery replays only the un-applied tail — *idempotently* by comparing sequence numbers (re-applying a change is a no-op if the target already reflects it). This appears in every WAL system, in replication (M10 — replicas track an applied position), in event-sourcing replays, in distributed logs (Kafka offsets, Raft log indices). The transferable instinct: *a monotonic sequence + a checkpoint position makes a log replayable, bounded, and idempotent* — the foundation of crash recovery and log-based replication alike.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB: the **LSN** (visible in `SHOW ENGINE INNODB STATUS` — "Log sequence number" vs "Last checkpoint at"; their difference is checkpoint age), per-page LSN, the flush list, fuzzy checkpointing. **Checkpoint age** relative to redo log capacity drives adaptive flushing (9.13) — if it approaches the redo log size, InnoDB flushes hard (a stall). `innodb_io_capacity`/`innodb_io_capacity_max` tell InnoDB how much flush I/O the storage can sustain (guiding adaptive flushing). For our domain: under a burst of transfers, the checkpoint age grows (many dirty `account`/`ledger_entry` pages, much un-checkpointed redo); InnoDB flushes the oldest dirty pages to advance the checkpoint and keep the redo log from filling — and if the redo log is too small for the burst, a checkpoint-age stall (9.13) briefly throttles writes. Sizing the redo log and IO capacity for the payments write rate (9.15) is what prevents these stalls. LSN/checkpointing is the connective tissue between the redo log (9.4), flushing (9.13), and crash recovery (9.14).
