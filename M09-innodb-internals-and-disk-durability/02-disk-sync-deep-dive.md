## 9.7 · The durability chain (disk sync, in depth) ★

**Mental model.** "Written to disk" is not one step — it's a **chain of layers**, and data can sit (volatile, lose-able) at *any* of them: **application (InnoDB's log buffer in RAM) → OS page cache (kernel RAM) → disk-controller/drive write cache (volatile RAM *on the device*) → physical platter/NAND (the only truly non-volatile layer).** A normal `write()` only gets data to the OS cache (still volatile — a power loss loses it). **`fsync` is supposed to force data all the way down to the physical medium** — but here's the dangerous part: **each layer can *lie*.** The OS might return from `write()` before the data is anywhere durable; the drive might acknowledge `fsync` while the data still sits in its *volatile on-board cache* (the "lying disk" problem). So "I called fsync, it returned, therefore it's durable" can be *false* — and that's how committed data silently vanishes on power loss. Understanding this chain is understanding what "durable" actually requires.

**How it actually works.** Trace a write down the chain:
1. **Application (InnoDB log buffer):** InnoDB writes redo records to its in-process **log buffer** (RAM) — purely volatile; a crash loses anything not yet pushed down.
2. **`write()` → OS page cache:** writing the log buffer to the file (a `write()` syscall) copies it into the **kernel page cache** — *still RAM, still volatile.* `write()` returning means "the kernel has it," **not** "it's on disk." A power loss here loses the data.
3. **`fsync()` → device:** `fsync` (or `fdatasync`, 9.8) tells the kernel "flush this file's dirty cache to the device and don't return until the device confirms." This pushes data out of the OS cache to the **disk controller / drive.**
4. **Drive write cache → platter/NAND:** the drive may have its *own* volatile **write cache** (DRAM on the SSD/HDD). A correctly-behaving drive, on receiving fsync's flush command, writes through its cache to the **physical medium** (platter or NAND) — the only non-volatile layer — and *then* acknowledges. **A "lying" drive acknowledges fsync while the data is still in its volatile cache** (to look faster) — so a power loss *after* fsync returned still loses the "durable" data. This is the **lying-disk / volatile-write-cache problem.**
So true durability requires: fsync that actually flushes the OS cache *and* a drive that honors the cache-flush (writes through to the medium before acking). When either lies, "committed" can be lost on power loss — the silent, catastrophic data-loss mode.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The layered chain exists for **performance** — each cache layer (OS page cache, drive write cache) lets writes return fast (to volatile RAM) and be flushed to slow physical media in the background. That's great for throughput but creates the durability hazard: *fast acknowledgment ≠ durable.* Understanding the chain is what lets you reason about *real* durability: it's not "did I call fsync?" but "did fsync actually reach non-volatile storage, through every honest layer?" This concept exists to demolish the dangerous assumption that "commit returned = data is safe" — it's safe only if every layer in the chain honored the sync. For money, where a lost committed payment is catastrophic, knowing the chain (and its lying layers) is essential to a defensible durability posture (9.16) and to understanding the rare data-loss modes (M15).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Every cache layer is a **performance-vs-durability** tradeoff: keeping data in the OS cache / drive cache is faster but volatile; forcing it through (fsync + honored cache-flush) is durable but slower (the fsync cost, 9.8). You can move the durability/throughput point: relax InnoDB's flush (don't fsync every commit, 9.10) for speed at the cost of a loss window; or ensure the chain is *honest* (disable the drive's volatile write cache, or use a drive with a battery/capacitor-backed cache that's effectively non-volatile, or enterprise storage with proper flush semantics) for durability. **Cloud/network storage** (EBS and friends) adds *more* layers and its own fsync semantics — "fast" cloud storage isn't automatically durable (it may ack before replication/persistence completes; you must verify its durability guarantees). The fundamental tradeoff is unavoidable: the more volatile caching between your fsync and the physical medium, the faster *and* the more lose-able.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Durability is a chain of layers, and an acknowledgment is only as trustworthy as the *weakest, most-volatile layer that lied* — 'I synced' means nothing if a layer below cached it volatilely and acked early." This is a universal and frequently-violated truth about durability in *any* layered storage system: the OS page cache, drive caches, RAID controller caches, network-storage caches, and even replication acks all sit between "I wrote it" and "it survives power loss," and *any* of them acking before true persistence breaks durability. The transferable instinct: **trace the full path from your write to non-volatile storage, and trust the durability only as far as the last layer that honestly confirmed persistence** — "the disk lied" / "the cache acked early" is one of the most insidious data-loss causes, in databases, filesystems, and distributed systems alike. (This is the M15 data-loss territory: durability that *looked* committed but wasn't.)

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB's chain: the **log buffer** (`innodb_log_buffer_size`) → file write → OS page cache → `fsync`/`fdatasync` (9.8, controlled by `innodb_flush_method`) → drive. `innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit=1` (9.10) makes InnoDB fsync the redo log every commit — but that only guarantees durability *if the OS and drive honor the fsync* (don't ack from volatile cache). Practical durability requires: a drive whose write cache is either disabled or non-volatile (battery/capacitor-backed), honest fsync semantics, and on cloud storage, verified durability guarantees. The doublewrite buffer (9.9) handles a *different* hazard (torn pages), not the lying-disk one. For our domain: a transfer's COMMIT fsyncs the redo log — but the payment is *truly* durable only if that fsync reached non-volatile storage through an honest chain; a lying disk or a relaxed flush setting (9.10) is exactly how a "confirmed" payment could be lost on power loss. This is *the* disk-sync reality, the foundation for the flush matrix (9.10) and the catastrophic data-loss cases (M15). ## 9.8 · fsync, fdatasync, O_DIRECT & write barriers

**Mental model.** These are the **actual system-level primitives** that force data through the durability chain (9.7) — the tools InnoDB uses to make "durable" real. **`fsync`** forces a file's data *and* metadata to the device; **`fdatasync`** forces the data (and only essential metadata) — slightly cheaper, often sufficient. **`O_DIRECT`** bypasses the OS page cache entirely (write goes straight toward the device, avoiding double-buffering between InnoDB's buffer pool and the kernel cache). **Write barriers** are lower-level ordering guarantees ensuring writes hit the medium in order. The practical point: **the fsync is the single biggest per-commit cost** (forcing physical I/O is slow), which is *why* durability has a throughput cost and *why* the flush matrix (9.10) exists — and `innodb_flush_method` controls which of these primitives InnoDB uses.

**How it actually works.**
- **`fsync(fd)`** — flush the file's dirty pages from the OS cache to the device *and* the device's metadata, then (on an honest device) force the device to write through its cache to the medium; return only when done. The strong, complete sync — but it also flushes metadata (file size, mtime), which can be extra work.
- **`fdatasync(fd)`** — flush the file's *data* and only the metadata *needed to read it back* (skips e.g. mtime). Slightly cheaper than fsync; safe for InnoDB's pre-allocated log files (size doesn't change, so metadata sync is unnecessary). InnoDB can use this for the redo log.
- **`O_DIRECT`** — open the file bypassing the OS page cache, so InnoDB's writes go (more) directly to the device. This avoids **double buffering** (InnoDB already caches pages in the buffer pool; caching them *again* in the OS page cache wastes RAM and adds a copy). With `O_DIRECT`, InnoDB still must fsync to force the device cache, but it skips the OS-cache layer. `innodb_flush_method=O_DIRECT` is a common production setting for the *data files* (the redo log handling differs).
- **Write barriers / FUA** — ordering primitives (and "force unit access" flags) ensuring writes reach the medium in the right order / bypass the drive cache. Mostly handled below MySQL, but relevant to whether the chain (9.7) is honest.
The cost: every fsync is a *synchronous wait for physical I/O* — milliseconds on spinning disk, microseconds-to-sub-millisecond on good SSDs, but always *much* slower than a memory write. This is why group commit (9.11) batches many commits' fsyncs into one, and why the flush matrix (9.10) exists.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** These primitives are how software *requests* durability from the OS and hardware — without `fsync`/`fdatasync`, a `write()` only reaches the volatile OS cache (9.7), so nothing would be crash-safe. `O_DIRECT` solves the double-buffering waste (InnoDB caching pages the OS *also* caches). They're the concrete tools that turn "I want this durable" into "force it down the chain." Understanding their *cost* (the synchronous physical-I/O wait) explains the entire durability/throughput tradeoff (9.10) and the optimizations built around it (group commit, 9.11). They're the bottom of the durability stack — everything above (redo log, commit, the flush matrix) ultimately calls these.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** `fsync` vs `fdatasync`: fdatasync is cheaper (skips non-essential metadata) but you must be sure the skipped metadata isn't needed (safe for fixed-size log files). `O_DIRECT` vs buffered: O_DIRECT avoids double-buffering (saves RAM, one copy) and gives InnoDB more control, but loses OS read-ahead/caching benefits for the data files (InnoDB's buffer pool replaces them) — generally a win for InnoDB's data files. The big tradeoff is **fsync-per-commit vs throughput** (9.10): you can fsync every commit (durable, slow) or batch/skip fsyncs (fast, loss window). `innodb_flush_method` (O_DIRECT, fsync, O_DIRECT_NO_FSYNC, etc.) picks the combination. The alternative — *not* syncing — is just non-durable. There's no free durability: forcing physical I/O costs latency, and the only levers are *how often* you sync (9.10), *how much* you batch (group commit, 9.11), and *how fast your storage is*.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Durability requires a synchronous force-to-physical-medium primitive (fsync), which is expensive — so amortize it (batch) and avoid redundant buffering (direct I/O), but you cannot eliminate its cost." `fsync`-and-friends are the universal **durability syscalls** — every database, message queue, and filesystem-backed durable store depends on them, and the "fsync is the expensive part" reality drives the same optimizations everywhere (group commit / batched fsync in databases and Kafka; the durability/latency tradeoff in every WAL system). The double-buffering problem `O_DIRECT` solves is the universal "don't cache the same data twice across layers" principle. The transferable instinct: *durability bottoms out in a slow synchronous physical-write primitive — so batch it, don't double-buffer, and recognize that durability latency is fundamental, not a bug to optimize away.*

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB: **`innodb_flush_method`** selects the I/O method — `O_DIRECT` (bypass OS cache for data files, common in production), `fsync` (buffered + fsync), `O_DIRECT_NO_FSYNC` (O_DIRECT and skip fsync where the storage guarantees durability without it — careful), etc. The redo log can use `fdatasync` semantics (fixed-size files). **`innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`** (9.10) decides *when* the redo log is fsync'd. The fsync cost is the dominant per-commit latency — mitigated by **group commit** (9.11, batching many commits' fsyncs). For our domain: a payments workload typically runs `innodb_flush_method=O_DIRECT` (avoid double-buffering the large data files) with `innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit=1` (fsync the redo every commit for durability) — accepting the fsync latency as the cost of never losing a committed payment, and relying on group commit (9.11) + fast (honest, ideally capacitor-backed) SSD storage to keep throughput high. These primitives are what make 9.7's chain durable in practice, and their cost is what the flush matrix (9.10) trades against.

## 9.9 · Torn / partial page writes & the doublewrite buffer ★

**Mental model.** A **16KB page write is not atomic at the hardware level** — the storage device writes in smaller units (e.g., 512-byte or 4KB sectors), so a crash *mid-write* can leave a page **torn**: part of it is the new version, part is the old, and the page is now *corrupt* (a garbage mix that neither the redo log nor anything else can fix — redo records *changes to* a valid page, not how to reconstruct a half-written one). InnoDB's defense is the **doublewrite buffer**: before writing any data page to its real location, InnoDB **first writes the page to a separate doublewrite area** (and fsyncs it), *then* writes it to its real home. If a crash tears the *real* write, recovery finds the *intact* copy in the doublewrite buffer and restores it. It's "write it twice so a torn write is always recoverable" — protecting against the one corruption WAL alone can't.

**How it actually works.** The hazard: InnoDB flushes a dirty 16KB page (9.1/9.6) to its location in the tablespace. If the OS/device writes that 16KB as several smaller sector writes and the server crashes after *some* sectors are written but not others, the on-disk page is **torn** — half new, half old, internally inconsistent (a broken checksum, a corrupt structure). Crash recovery can't fix this: the redo log says "apply change X to page P," but page P is now garbage, not the valid pre-change page the redo expects. The **doublewrite buffer** prevents it: InnoDB first writes the page (along with others, batched) to a dedicated, sequential **doublewrite area**, fsyncs *that*, and only *then* writes each page to its real (scattered) location. On recovery, InnoDB checks each page's **checksum**; if a page is found torn (bad checksum), it reads the good copy from the doublewrite buffer and rewrites it. So every page is written twice (once to the doublewrite area, once to its home), and the doublewrite copy is the safety net for a torn home-write. The cost is the extra write (doubled write I/O for data pages — though the doublewrite area is sequential, so it's cheaper than 2× random). **When it's safe to disable:** if the storage provides **atomic 16KB writes** (some enterprise SSDs / filesystems guarantee a 16KB write is all-or-nothing), torn pages can't happen, so the doublewrite buffer is redundant and can be disabled for the write-I/O savings (`innodb_doublewrite=OFF`) — but *only* with verified atomic-write hardware, or you've removed your torn-page protection.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The doublewrite buffer solves a corruption mode that **WAL (redo log) cannot**: a *torn page write*. Redo logging gives you durability (re-apply committed changes, 9.4) but assumes pages are written atomically — it has no way to reconstruct a *partially-written* page (it records deltas to a valid page, not the full page content). The doublewrite buffer fills exactly that gap: it guarantees that for every page, an *intact* copy exists somewhere, so a torn home-write is always recoverable. Without it, a crash during a page flush could leave a permanently-corrupt page that recovery couldn't repair — silent data corruption (a far worse failure than data *loss*, because it can go undetected). It's the protection that makes InnoDB's crash-safety *complete* (durability + no-torn-page-corruption).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The doublewrite buffer trades **extra write I/O** (every data page written twice) for **torn-page protection**. The overhead is less than 2× because the doublewrite area is *sequential* (cheap) and pages are batched — but it's real write amplification (9.13). The alternatives: **atomic-write storage** (hardware/filesystem that guarantees 16KB atomic writes — then doublewrite is redundant, disable it for the I/O savings); **a smaller page size matching the device's atomic-write unit** (if pages = atomic sector size, no tearing — but tiny pages hurt fanout, M05/5.3). The decision: keep the doublewrite buffer ON (the safe default) unless you've *verified* atomic-write hardware. For money, the I/O cost is cheap insurance against corruption — keep it on. (Note: the doublewrite protects *data pages*; the redo log itself is written differently and protected by checksums/LSN, not the doublewrite.)

**Generics / first-principles.** "If a write unit isn't atomic at the hardware level, a crash mid-write corrupts it — so write a recoverable copy first, then the real write, so the copy can repair a torn write." The doublewrite buffer is an instance of the universal **write-twice-for-atomicity / shadow-write** pattern for non-atomic writes: when you can't make a large write atomic, you write a durable copy elsewhere first so a torn primary write is always recoverable (the same idea as a filesystem journaling *full data blocks*, or copy-on-write filesystems that never overwrite in place). The deeper principle: **durability (replay a log) and atomicity-of-large-writes (no torn pages) are *different* guarantees needing *different* mechanisms** — WAL gives the former, write-twice gives the latter. The transferable instinct: *a log re-applies changes but can't fix a half-written block; protecting against torn writes needs a separate "intact copy exists" mechanism.* Many people conflate "I have a WAL" with "I'm safe from all crash corruption" — the doublewrite buffer is the reminder that torn pages are a distinct hazard.

**MySQL-specific reality.** InnoDB's **doublewrite buffer** (`innodb_doublewrite`, ON by default; in 8.0.20+ it lives in separate `#ib_*dblwr` files): before flushing data pages to their tablespace locations, InnoDB writes them (batched, sequentially) to the doublewrite area + fsync, then to their real homes; recovery uses page **checksums** (`innodb_checksum_algorithm`) to detect torn pages and restores intact copies from the doublewrite area. Safe to disable *only* with atomic-write storage (some NVMe SSDs / filesystems like ZFS with appropriate settings guarantee it) — verify before disabling. For our domain: when InnoDB flushes a dirty `ledger_entry` page and the server crashes mid-write (tearing it), recovery detects the bad checksum and restores the intact page from the doublewrite buffer — so a crash during a page flush can't corrupt the ledger. Keeping `innodb_doublewrite=ON` is part of a money-system durability posture (9.16) — the modest write-I/O cost is worth guaranteed torn-page protection. The doublewrite buffer is *the* answer to "what about a crash *during* a page write?" — a question redo logging alone doesn't address. ## 9.10 · The durability tradeoff matrix (flush_log_at_trx_commit × sync_binlog) ★

**Mental model.** Durability is a **tunable dial**, and the two main knobs — **`innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`** (how strictly the *redo log* is synced on commit) and **`sync_binlog`** (how strictly the *binary log* is synced, M10) — form a **matrix**, where each combination buys a different **throughput** at the cost of a specific **loss window** (*exactly what you lose on a crash*). This is *the* central, designed-in durability decision: at the strictest setting (1, 1), nothing committed is lost (but every commit pays two fsyncs); relax either, and you trade fsync cost for a window of potentially-lost recent commits. Knowing *exactly what each cell loses* is what lets you choose deliberately — and for money, the answer is almost always the strict corner.

**How it actually works.**
**`innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`** (the redo log, 9.4):
- **`1`** (default, fully durable): at every commit, the redo log is **written to the log files AND fsync'd** to stable storage before the commit returns. A crash (even power loss) loses **nothing committed**. Cost: an fsync per commit (mitigated by group commit, 9.11).
- **`2`** (write every commit, fsync ~1×/sec): at commit, redo is **written to the OS cache** (a `write()`, not fsync) and returns; fsync happens about once per second. A *MySQL process crash* loses nothing (the OS still has it and will flush it); a ***power loss / OS crash* can lose ~1 second of committed transactions** (what was in the OS cache, un-fsync'd). Faster (no per-commit fsync).
- **`0`** (fsync ~1×/sec): redo is written+fsync'd about once per second, *not* at commit. A crash of *any* kind (even just MySQL) can lose ~1 second of commits. Fastest, least durable.

**`sync_binlog`** (the binary log, for replication/PITR, M10):
- **`1`** (durable): fsync the binlog at every commit — the binlog won't lose committed transactions on crash (critical for replication consistency and point-in-time recovery, M10/M13).
- **`0`** (or N): fsync the binlog rarely (rely on the OS) — faster, but a crash can lose recent binlog entries (replicas/PITR could miss transactions the primary committed).

The **matrix** combines them: **(1, 1)** = fully durable (redo + binlog both fsync'd per commit) — nothing lost, two fsyncs/commit (group commit amortizes); **(2, 1)** or **(1, 0)** etc. = various relaxations with specific loss windows. The key insight: **the loss window is precisely defined** for each cell — you're not gambling, you're choosing a known tradeoff.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The matrix exists because **durability has a real, unavoidable cost (the fsync, 9.8), and different workloads have different tolerances.** A payments system *cannot* lose a committed transfer → strict (1, 1). A high-volume analytics ingest or a cache-like workload *can* tolerate losing the last second of writes on a rare crash → relax to (2, 0) for a big throughput win. Exposing this as a tunable matrix lets each system pick its point on the durability/throughput curve *knowingly* — and the per-cell loss window (exactly what's lost) makes it a *deliberate engineering decision*, not a hope. It's the practical realization of "durability vs throughput" as a controllable dial.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** This concept *is* the tradeoff: **strict (1,1)** = maximum durability, ~two fsyncs/commit (slowest, but group commit, 9.11, amortizes heavily under concurrency); **relaxed** = fewer/no per-commit fsyncs (faster) at a known loss window (~1s of recent commits on power loss). The decision is per-system (and sometimes per-table-via-separate-instances): money/orders/anything-you-can't-lose → (1,1); ephemeral/reconstructible data → relax for speed. Beyond these two knobs, the durability posture also depends on the *honest chain* (9.7 — a (1,1) setting is undermined by a lying disk) and *replication* (M10 — a replica is another durability copy, so semi-sync replication, M10, can provide durability even if the primary's local fsync is relaxed). So the full durability decision is: flush settings (this matrix) + honest storage (9.7/9.9) + replication (M10). The alternative to choosing deliberately is the bug: running relaxed settings on money data and silently losing committed payments on a crash (an M15 incident).

**Generics / first-principles.** "Durability is a spectrum from 'every commit synced' to 'sync periodically,' and each point has a precisely-defined loss window — choose the point your data's value justifies." The flush matrix is the universal **durability/throughput knob** present in every durable system (Postgres `synchronous_commit`, Kafka `acks`, Redis `appendfsync`, every WAL store): you can sync-per-operation (durable, slow), sync-periodically (fast, bounded loss), or never (fastest, lossy) — and the engineering discipline is *matching the durability level to the cost of loss.* The transferable instinct: **durability is not binary; it's a dial with a known loss window at each setting, and you set it by asking "what can I afford to lose on a crash?"** For data you can't afford to lose (money), pay the fsync; for reconstructible data, relax. The matrix makes the tradeoff explicit and quantified — the hallmark of a deliberate durability decision (and the subject, at its catastrophic extreme, of M15).

**MySQL-specific reality.** The two knobs: **`innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`** (1 / 2 / 0, redo log, 9.4) and **`sync_binlog`** (1 / 0 / N, binlog, M10). For money/fintech: **(1, 1) is the standard, mandatory setting** — every committed transfer is durable in *both* the redo log (crash recovery) and the binlog (replication/PITR consistency, M10/M13), so nothing is lost on a crash and replicas/backups stay consistent. The fsync cost is absorbed by **group commit** (9.11 — under concurrency, many commits share one fsync, so the per-commit cost is far less than "two fsyncs each"). Relaxed settings (2, 0) are for workloads that tolerate ~1s loss (and the savings are largest at low concurrency where group commit can't amortize). The honest-chain caveat (9.7) applies: (1,1) only delivers durability if the OS/drive honor fsync (no lying cache). For our domain: a payments platform runs `flush_log_at_trx_commit=1` + `sync_binlog=1` + doublewrite ON (9.9) + honest storage (9.7) — so a committed payment survives a crash, a power loss, *and* a torn page, and replicas/backups never miss it. This (1,1) durable posture is the spine of 9.16 and the line that separates a money-safe configuration from an M15 data-loss waiting to happen.
