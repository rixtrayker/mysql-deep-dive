# M12 · Distributed Data Concerns — Pass A (Skeleton)

> **Pass A goal:** lock the ordered concept list, a one-line mental model per concept, and the planned diagram + bespoke-SVG inventory + worked example per concept. No prose yet — that's Pass B.
>
> **Module mental model:** *M10 (replication) and M11 (sharding) forced the same realization from two directions: once data lives on more than one node, the single-node guarantees you took for granted — instant consistency, atomic multi-key transactions, "the database is one place" — stop holding, and you must* choose *what to give up. This module is the* theory and the patterns *of that choice. **CAP** and **PACELC** name the fundamental tradeoff: under a network partition you can't have both consistency and availability (CAP), and even when the network is fine you trade latency against consistency (PACELC) — there is no distributed system that escapes this, only systems that pick a point. The **consistency spectrum** (strong → linearizable → causal → read-your-writes → eventual) is the menu of points. Then the patterns for the hard problem M11 surfaced — **operations that span nodes**: **distributed transactions** (2PC/XA — atomic but blocking, and why MySQL XA is avoided), the **Saga** (local transactions + compensations — the scalable alternative), **idempotency** (make every operation safely retryable — the load-bearing primitive of all distributed money movement), the **dual-write problem** (the trap of writing to two systems without atomicity) and its fix the **outbox pattern**, and **Change Data Capture** (read the binlog — M10 — as a stream, via Debezium/Kafka, to integrate systems reliably without dual-writes). This is the module where "distributed" stops being a deployment detail and becomes a set of named, reasoned-about correctness decisions — and for money, it's where you learn the patterns (idempotency, outbox, Saga, reconciliation) that keep a transfer correct when it crosses the boundary of a single ACID transaction.*
>
> **Threads carried in this module:**
> - **Durability** — distributed durability is *agreement* durability: a write is "durable" once enough nodes agree (quorum/consensus, M10/10.4), and the outbox pattern makes an event durable *in the same transaction* as the state change (so it can't be lost). The question shifts from "survived a crash on one node?" (M09) to "survived, and *agreed-upon*, across nodes?"
> - **Money-never-lies** — *the* money module for distribution: **idempotency** (a retried payment must not double-charge — the single most important fintech primitive), the **dual-write problem** (state saved but the event lost, or vice-versa → money/notifications inconsistent), **Saga** correctness (a multi-step transfer that fails partway must compensate, never leave money in limbo), and **reconciliation** (M02/2.17 — the backstop that *detects* any distributed inconsistency). Eventual consistency is acceptable for money *only* when paired with idempotency + reconciliation.
> - **Generics-first** — CAP/PACELC, the consistency spectrum, 2PC, Saga, idempotency, outbox, and CDC are *the* canonical distributed-systems concepts — they transfer to every distributed datastore, every microservices architecture, every event-driven system; MySQL is one substrate (its binlog powers CDC; its XA implements 2PC).
> - **Tradeoff** — this module *is* the tradeoff thread made explicit: consistency vs availability vs latency (CAP/PACELC), atomicity vs scalability (2PC vs Saga), strong vs eventual consistency, synchronous coordination vs asynchronous compensation. Nothing distributed is free; this module names every cost.
>
> **Prereqs:** M11 (sharding — the cross-shard-transaction problem 11.11 that this module *solves* with 2PC/Saga/outbox; the co-location principle 11.9 that *avoids* it), M10 (replication — the async/semi-sync/sync dial 10.4 that *is* the CAP/PACELC knob; the binlog 10.14 that *is* the CDC source; eventual consistency/lag 10.5 made concrete), M07 (transactions/ACID — what single-node atomicity gives, that distribution loses), M09 (single-node durability — extended here to agreement durability), M02/2.17 (normalization + the reconciliation/derived-data pattern — the backstop that detects distributed drift, and the outbox is a derived-event table), M01/1.17 (event sourcing / the log as source of truth — CDC and outbox are this). **Leads into:** M13 (Ops — operating CDC pipelines, monitoring distributed consistency), **M15 (Failure Modes — the catastrophic distributed failures: split-brain, dual-write data loss, Saga stuck mid-flight, idempotency-key gaps, the dual-write/outbox failures in depth)**, M16 (the fintech capstone — *every* pattern here is load-bearing: idempotency keys, the outbox/CDC reconciliation pipeline, Sagas for cross-shard transfers, the consistency choices per money flow).

## Concept list (ordered)

| # | Concept | One-line mental model | Diagram | Worked example |
|---|---------|----------------------|---------|----------------|
| 12.1 | **Why distributed data is hard (the shift)** | The moment data spans nodes, single-node guarantees (instant consistency, atomic multi-key transactions, one place to look) stop holding — and you must consciously choose what to trade. ★ | What single-node gave vs what distribution takes away | The payments platform after M10+M11: where its single-node guarantees broke, and what it now must decide |
| 12.2 | **CAP theorem** ★ | Under a network **partition**, a distributed system must choose **consistency** (reject/wait) or **availability** (answer with possibly-stale data) — you cannot have both; it's a forced choice, not a free lunch. ★ custom SVG | ★ CAP: partition → choose C (reject) or A (stale) | A partition splits the payments cluster: does a balance read fail (CP) or return stale (AP)? — and which money operations need which |
| 12.3 | **PACELC: the fuller picture** | CAP only speaks to partitions; **PACELC** adds the common case — *even when the network is fine* (Else), you trade **Latency** vs **Consistency** (wait for more nodes = consistent + slow, or fewer = fast + stale). | PACELC: if-Partition (C/A) else (L/C) — the everyday dial | Semi-sync (M10/10.4) as the PACELC "else" knob: every commit trades latency for replica-confirmed consistency |
| 12.4 | **The consistency spectrum** ★ | Consistency isn't binary — it's a ladder: **strong/linearizable** (acts like one copy) → **causal** → **read-your-writes / monotonic** → **eventual** (converges someday) — each weaker, cheaper, more available. ★ | ★ the consistency ladder (strong→causal→RYW→eventual) with cost/availability | Which level each payments read needs: a balance-for-a-decision (strong) vs a transaction list (RYW) vs a report (eventual) |
| 12.5 | **Eventual consistency & convergence** | Replicas/copies that are temporarily different but **converge** to the same state once updates propagate — acceptable when staleness is tolerable and conflicts are resolvable, dangerous when read for a money decision. | Eventual consistency: diverge → propagate → converge (+ conflict resolution) | A balance eventually-consistent across replicas: fine for display, a double-spend bug for an authorization decision |
| 12.6 | **Distributed transactions & 2PC** ★ | To make an operation atomic across nodes, a **coordinator** runs **two-phase commit** (prepare → vote → commit/abort) — it works, but holds locks across the network and **blocks** if the coordinator dies mid-commit. ★ | ★ 2PC: prepare/vote/commit + the coordinator-crash in-doubt window | A cross-shard transfer via 2PC: both shards prepare, then the coordinator crashes — both stuck in-doubt, locks held |
| 12.7 | **XA in MySQL (and why it's avoided)** | MySQL implements 2PC as **XA transactions** — but they're slow, lock-heavy, historically fragile on recovery, and block on coordinator failure, so high-scale fintech **avoids** them in favor of Sagas. | XA states (START→END→PREPARE→COMMIT) + the avoidance reasons | Why a payments platform doesn't use XA for cross-shard transfers — the in-doubt/throughput problems |
| 12.8 | **The Saga pattern** ★ | A long/cross-node operation as a **sequence of local transactions**, each with a **compensating** transaction to undo it — non-blocking and scalable, but **eventually consistent** and you must design correct compensations. ★ | ★ Saga: forward steps + compensations (orchestration vs choreography) | A cross-shard transfer as a Saga: debit → credit → (on failure) compensate the debit; the in-flight state |
| 12.9 | **Idempotency: the load-bearing primitive** ★ | An operation you can safely **repeat** with no extra effect — achieved with an **idempotency key** that dedups retries — *the* primitive that makes unreliable distributed money movement safe (no double-charge). ★ | ★ idempotency: key → first-time applies / retry returns the same result (dedup) | A payment request retried after a timeout: the idempotency key ensures it charges **once**, not twice |
| 12.10 | **The dual-write problem** ★ | Writing to **two systems** (DB + queue, DB + cache, two services) without a shared transaction is a trap: a crash between the writes leaves them **inconsistent** — money saved but event lost, or vice-versa. ★ | ★ dual-write: write A ✓ → crash → write B ✗ → inconsistent state | "Save the transfer to the DB, then publish to Kafka" — the publish fails after commit → downstream never sees it |
| 12.11 | **The outbox pattern** ★ | Fix the dual-write by writing the **event into the same database transaction** as the state change (an **outbox** table) — then a separate process reliably publishes it; the event can't be lost because it's part of the atomic commit. ★ | ★ outbox: one txn writes state + outbox row → relay/CDC publishes | The transfer and its "TransferCompleted" event committed atomically to the outbox, then published — no lost event |
| 12.12 | **Change Data Capture (CDC)** ★ | Read the **binary log** (M10) as a **stream of changes** (via Debezium → Kafka) to propagate data to other systems reliably — the database's own change log becomes the integration backbone, no dual-writes. ★ | ★ CDC: binlog → Debezium → Kafka → consumers (search, cache, analytics, outbox relay) | The ledger's binlog streamed via CDC to fraud detection, the search index, and the reporting warehouse |
| 12.13 | **Idempotent consumers & exactly-once (the truth)** | "Exactly-once delivery" is a myth in distributed messaging; you get **at-least-once delivery + idempotent processing** = exactly-once *effect* — consumers must dedup (idempotency again, 12.9). | at-least-once + idempotent consumer = exactly-once EFFECT | A CDC/Kafka consumer that may see a "credit" event twice → idempotency key makes the second a no-op |
| 12.14 | **Reconciliation as the distributed backstop** | No distributed system is perfectly consistent, so you **reconcile** — independently re-derive and compare (internal vs external ledgers, state vs events) — to *detect and repair* any drift; the money-never-lies safety net. | Reconciliation: independent re-derivation → compare → alert/repair on drift | A daily job comparing the ledger to the payment processor's records, catching a Saga that silently half-completed |
| 12.15 | **Choosing consistency per operation (the decision)** | Don't pick one consistency level for the whole system — pick **per operation** by its cost-of-being-wrong: strong + atomic for money authorization, eventual + idempotent + reconciled for propagation/reporting. | Decision: per-operation → (strong/atomic) vs (eventual/idempotent/reconciled) | Mapping each payments operation to its consistency + pattern: authorize (strong), notify (outbox/CDC), report (eventual) |
| 12.16 | **Fintech capstone: the consistent-enough payments platform** ★ | The complete distributed design — single-shard ACID where possible (M11), Sagas + idempotency for cross-shard, outbox/CDC for propagation, reconciliation as backstop, consistency chosen per operation — so money is correct despite distribution. ★ | ★ the distributed payments architecture (annotated: per-op consistency, outbox/CDC, Saga, reconciliation) | End-to-end: how every distributed pattern composes so a payment is never lost/duplicated across the whole platform (caps Track D, sets up M15/M16) |
