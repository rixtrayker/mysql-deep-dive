## 12.1 · Why distributed data is hard (the shift)

**Mental model.** On a single server, a set of guarantees came *for free* and you never thought about them: a read sees the latest write (instant consistency); a transaction over many rows is atomic and isolated (ACID, M07–M09); there's *one place* to look for the truth. The instant data lives on **more than one node** — replicas (M10) or shards (M11) — *every one of those free guarantees stops holding*: a replica can be stale (M10/10.5), a transaction spanning shards isn't atomic (M11/11.11), and "the truth" is now spread across nodes that can disagree. Distributed data is hard because you can no longer *assume* consistency, atomicity, or a single source of truth — you must **consciously choose** which to keep, which to weaken, and how to compensate. This module is the theory (CAP/PACELC, the consistency spectrum) and the patterns (2PC, Saga, idempotency, outbox, CDC, reconciliation) of making that choice deliberately.

**How it actually works.** The shift has three concrete faces, each from earlier modules: **(1) Consistency breaks** — replication is asynchronous (M10/10.4), so a replica reflects a *past* state (lag, 10.5); two nodes can momentarily disagree about a balance. **(2) Atomicity breaks across nodes** — a single redo log and commit give atomicity *within one node* (M09); there is no commit spanning two independent servers, so a cross-shard transfer (M11/11.11) has no native all-or-nothing (a crash between legs loses money). **(3) The single source of truth fragments** — data is replicated and sharded, integrated with other systems (queues, caches, services), so "what is the balance?" depends on *which copy you ask and when*. Each face forces a decision: how stale a read may be, how to make a cross-node operation safe, how to keep copies/systems agreeing. The rest of the module names and solves each.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** This concept exists to *frame the whole module*: distribution isn't a deployment detail you can ignore — it fundamentally changes what's *true* about your data, and pretending otherwise is how distributed systems silently lose or duplicate money. Naming the shift ("here's exactly what you lost when you went multi-node, and here's the menu of what to trade") turns a vague unease into a set of explicit, reasoned decisions. It solves the meta-problem of *knowing you now have choices to make* — and that the right choice differs per operation (a balance-for-a-decision needs strong consistency; a report tolerates staleness). Without this framing, teams apply single-node intuitions to distributed data and get burned (stale-read double-spends, dual-write data loss, Sagas stuck in limbo).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The overarching tradeoff this module elaborates: **consistency/atomicity vs availability/latency/scale** — you went multi-node *for* availability, scale, and locality (M10/M11), and the cost is the single-node guarantees. The "alternative" is to *not* distribute (stay single-node — simplest, all guarantees, but a capacity/availability ceiling, M11/11.15) — strongly preferred when you can (which is why M11 says "shard last"). Once distributed, you can't escape the tradeoffs (CAP/PACELC, 12.2/12.3); you can only choose *where* on the surface to sit, *per operation* (12.15). The discipline: distribute only what must be (keep money operations single-node/single-shard where possible, M11/11.9), and for the genuinely-distributed parts, choose consistency consciously and add the safety patterns (idempotency, outbox, reconciliation).

**Generics / first-principles.** "Distribution converts implicit single-node guarantees (consistency, atomicity, single source of truth) into explicit, per-operation tradeoffs — you must now *choose* consistency rather than *assume* it." This is the foundational realization of all distributed systems (the motivation behind CAP, the whole field of distributed databases, the design of every microservices architecture). The transferable insight: **a single node is a powerful abstraction precisely because it gives consistency and atomicity for free; the moment you cross node boundaries — for replication, partitioning, or integrating services — those become things you design, pay for, and sometimes give up.** Every distributed-data decision downstream (which consistency level, 2PC vs Saga, how to integrate systems) is a consequence of this one shift. Recognizing "this operation now spans nodes, so its guarantees are no longer free" is the first move in all distributed design.

**MySQL-specific reality.** The shift is exactly what M10 and M11 introduced in MySQL terms: **replication** (M10) makes reads potentially stale (async lag, 10.5; semi-sync trades latency for consistency, 10.4); **sharding** (M11) makes cross-shard transactions non-atomic (11.11) and cross-shard queries scatter-gather (11.10). MySQL gives you *strong* guarantees *within* a single InnoDB node (M07–M09) and *tools* for the distributed parts: **semi-sync/group replication** (M10 — the consistency/latency dial), **XA** (12.7 — 2PC), and the **binlog** (12.12 — the CDC source). The MySQL-specific discipline this module teaches: **keep money operations within a single node/shard** (where InnoDB's ACID is free, M11/11.9), and for what genuinely crosses nodes, apply the patterns (Saga, idempotency, outbox, CDC, reconciliation) — because MySQL (like every database) can't give you free cross-node atomicity or consistency. The whole module is "what to do at the boundaries MySQL's single-node guarantees stop at."

## 12.2 · CAP theorem ★

**Mental model.** The **CAP theorem** states the unavoidable: when a **network partition** happens (nodes can't talk to each other — a real, inevitable event in any distributed system), a system must choose between **Consistency** (every read sees the latest write, or an error — so during a partition, nodes that can't confirm the latest state must *reject* requests) and **Availability** (every request gets a non-error response — so partitioned nodes *answer anyway*, with possibly-**stale** data). You **cannot have both during a partition** — it's a forced choice. The famous "pick two of C/A/P" is misleading: **P (partition tolerance) isn't optional** (partitions *will* happen; you don't choose to tolerate them, you must), so the real choice is **C vs A** *when a partition occurs*. A **CP** system stays consistent by sacrificing availability (rejects/waits during partitions); an **AP** system stays available by sacrificing consistency (serves stale during partitions).

**How it actually works.** Picture a cluster split by a network partition into two sides that can't communicate. A write arrives on side 1; a read for the same data arrives on side 2 (which hasn't seen the write). The system faces an impossible bind: **(CP choice)** side 2 *refuses to answer* (or blocks) because it can't guarantee it has the latest write — **consistency preserved, availability sacrificed** (that read fails until the partition heals). **(AP choice)** side 2 *answers with its (stale) data* — **availability preserved, consistency sacrificed** (the read returns an old value; the two sides may even accept conflicting writes that must be reconciled later). There is no third option that's both consistent and available *during the partition* — that's the theorem. When the partition heals, an AP system must *reconcile* any divergence (conflict resolution); a CP system simply resumes (it never diverged, because it refused to serve the minority). The choice is made by the system's design (and sometimes tunably, per operation).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** CAP exists to *name a fundamental, unavoidable constraint* so designers stop chasing the impossible (a partition-proof, always-consistent, always-available distributed system) and instead make a *conscious* C-vs-A choice appropriate to their data. It solves the problem of *false expectations*: before CAP, people assumed distributed databases could "just" be consistent and available; CAP proves they can't be both under partition, forcing the honest question "for *this* data, during a partition, do I prefer to be *wrong* (stale, AP) or *unavailable* (reject, CP)?" For money, this question is sharp and the answer differs by operation: a **balance-for-an-authorization** must be CP (better to reject than to authorize against stale data → overdraft/double-spend), while a **display of recent activity** can be AP (better to show slightly-stale than to error). CAP gives the vocabulary to make and defend that split.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The CAP tradeoff *is* C vs A under partition: **CP** (consistency-first) sacrifices availability during partitions (the system rejects/blocks — safe but unavailable; e.g., a strongly-consistent store, MySQL group replication's minority side, Spanner, ZooKeeper); **AP** (availability-first) sacrifices consistency during partitions (the system serves stale/divergent and reconciles later — available but possibly-wrong; e.g., Dynamo/Cassandra default, DNS). The nuance CAP *omits* (and PACELC, 12.3, adds): CAP only describes the *partition* case (rare); the *common* case (network fine) has its own latency-vs-consistency tradeoff. Also, CAP is not all-or-nothing per system — modern systems are **tunable per operation** (Cassandra's per-query consistency level, MySQL's per-deployment semi-sync vs async) — so "CP or AP" is often a *per-operation* choice, not a whole-system label. The practical stance: choose C-vs-A *per operation* by cost-of-being-wrong (12.15).

**Generics / first-principles.** "Under a network partition, you must choose consistency *or* availability — partitions are inevitable, so the real choice is C-vs-A-when-partitioned, and it's a per-data decision, not a free lunch." This is *the* foundational distributed-systems theorem (Brewer's conjecture, proven by Gilbert & Lynch), underlying the design of every distributed datastore. The transferable insight: **a distributed system cannot be both consistent and available when nodes can't communicate — so every distributed design has a (often tunable) C-vs-A stance, and the engineering question is "during a partition, for this operation, would I rather return stale data or no data?"** It transfers everywhere: Dynamo/Cassandra (AP), Spanner/etcd/ZooKeeper (CP), the per-operation tuning in between. The deeper principle: **you can't wish away partitions; you can only decide how to behave during them** — and pretending a system is "CA" (consistent + available, no P) only means it hasn't hit a partition *yet*.

**MySQL-specific reality.** A MySQL deployment's CAP stance depends on its replication mode (M10): **async replication** leans **AP-ish** (replicas stay available and serve, but stale — and a partition can lose un-replicated writes on failover, 10.10); **semi-sync** tightens consistency at a latency/availability cost (10.4); **Group Replication / InnoDB Cluster** is **CP** — it requires a **majority quorum** to commit, so a partitioned *minority* side becomes *unavailable for writes* (it refuses rather than diverge — preventing split-brain, 10.11) while the majority side stays consistent and available. So MySQL gives you a CAP *dial* via the replication/HA choice. For our domain: a payments cluster typically wants **CP for money-critical operations** (a balance-for-authorization, a transfer commit — better to reject during a partition than to authorize against stale/divergent data; this is why group replication's quorum and the fencing of M10/10.11 matter — a forked ledger is catastrophic) and can tolerate **AP for non-critical reads** (display, recent activity — better stale than erroring). The CAP choice is made concrete by the M10 replication mode + the per-operation routing (12.15). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the partition → C-vs-A forced choice.

## 12.3 · PACELC: the fuller picture

**Mental model.** CAP only describes behavior *during a partition* — but partitions are *rare*, so CAP says nothing about the **common case** (the network is fine). **PACELC** completes the picture: **if Partition (P), choose Availability or Consistency (A/C)** — that's CAP — **Else (E), choose Latency or Consistency (L/C)**. The "else" is the everyday reality: even with a healthy network, to be *more consistent* you must wait for *more nodes* to acknowledge (higher **latency**), and to be *faster* you accept *less consistency* (a replica might be stale). So a distributed system has *two* tradeoff knobs: the rare partition-time C-vs-A, and the constant operation-time **L-vs-C**. PACELC classifies systems as e.g. **PC/EC** (consistent always — Spanner), **PA/EL** (available + fast, eventually consistent — Dynamo/Cassandra), etc. The "else" knob is the one you actually tune every day.

**How it actually works.** The **else** branch is the latency-vs-consistency dial you already met as semi-sync (M10/10.4): when a write commits, *how many nodes must acknowledge before you return "done"?* **Wait for more** (e.g., a quorum or all replicas to confirm) → the write is durable/consistent across nodes (a subsequent read anywhere sees it) but each commit pays the **latency** of those round-trips. **Wait for fewer** (e.g., just the local node — async) → commits are **fast**, but replicas lag (a read elsewhere may be stale). The **partition** branch is CAP (12.2): when nodes *can't* communicate, choose A (serve stale) or C (reject). PACELC's contribution is making the *else* branch explicit and equal in importance — because you spend ~all your time there. A system's PACELC class names *both* choices: e.g. **PA/EL** = available under partition *and* low-latency (eventually consistent) normally; **PC/EC** = consistent under partition *and* consistent (higher-latency) normally.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** PACELC exists because **CAP is incomplete and over-focused on the rare case**, leading to a common misconception that the only distributed tradeoff is partition-time C-vs-A. In reality, the *latency-vs-consistency* tradeoff (the "else") dominates day-to-day design — every replicated write chooses how synchronous to be (M10/10.4), and that choice is *constant*, not partition-conditional. PACELC solves the *under-emphasis* of the everyday tradeoff: it forces you to recognize that "how consistent" is something you pay latency for *all the time*, not just during partitions. For money: the "else" choice is exactly "do I wait for a replica to confirm this transfer (semi-sync — durable beyond a node, but slower) or return immediately (async — fast, but a node loss could lose it)?" — a latency-vs-consistency/durability decision made on *every* commit, which CAP alone wouldn't surface.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** PACELC names four corner stances: **PC/EC** (always consistent — sacrifice availability under partition *and* latency normally; Spanner, BigTable-ish, MySQL group replication leans here); **PA/EL** (always available + fast — sacrifice consistency both times; Dynamo, Cassandra default); **PC/EL** and **PA/EC** (mixed). The everyday tradeoff (the "EL vs EC" knob): **EC** (wait for consistency — higher latency, fresher reads everywhere) vs **EL** (don't wait — lower latency, possibly-stale reads). For fintech, money-critical writes lean **EC** (semi-sync — confirm durability/consistency, accept the latency, M10/10.4) while non-critical reads lean **EL** (async replicas — fast, stale-tolerant). The alternative framings (just CAP) under-serve design by hiding the constant latency-vs-consistency choice. PACELC's value is completeness: it's the better mental model for actually *tuning* a distributed system.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Two tradeoffs, not one: under partition, availability-vs-consistency (CAP); the rest of the time, latency-vs-consistency — because stronger consistency always means waiting for more nodes." This is the more-complete distributed-tradeoff framework (Abadi's PACELC), refining CAP. The transferable insight: **consistency costs *latency* even when the network is healthy (you wait for more nodes to agree) and costs *availability* when it isn't (you reject rather than diverge) — so 'how consistent' is a dial you pay for constantly, with two different currencies (latency normally, availability under partition).** It transfers to every replicated/quorum system: Kafka `acks`, Cassandra/Dynamo consistency levels, Postgres `synchronous_commit`, MySQL semi-sync — *all* are "EL vs EC" knobs. The deep point: **the everyday tradeoff (latency-vs-consistency) matters more than the rare one (CAP), because you're almost always in the "else" branch** — design for the common case.

**MySQL-specific reality.** MySQL's PACELC "else" knob *is* the replication sync mode (M10/10.4): **async = EL** (commit returns immediately, fast, replicas stale — low latency, weak consistency); **semi-sync = toward EC** (commit waits for a replica's ack — higher latency, replica-confirmed consistency/durability); **group replication = PC/EC** (majority must certify each transaction — consistent, but coordination latency, and the partitioned minority is unavailable, the PC part). So choosing async vs semi-sync vs group replication *is* choosing MySQL's PACELC class. For our domain: money-critical commits use **semi-sync (toward EC)** — pay the latency to guarantee the transfer is durable/consistent beyond one node (M10/10.4/9.16) — while read replicas serve non-critical reads with **async (EL)** — fast, stale-tolerant (display, reports). The per-operation split (12.15) is exactly choosing EL vs EC per operation. PACELC is the framework that makes M10's sync-mode dial a *principled* per-operation decision rather than a single global setting. The Pass C Mermaid draws the if-P(A/C)-else(L/C) structure.

## 12.4 · The consistency spectrum ★

**Mental model.** Consistency isn't a binary (consistent / not) — it's a **ladder** of named levels, each weaker, cheaper, faster, and more available than the one above. From strongest to weakest: **Linearizable / strong** (the system behaves as if there's *one* copy — every read sees the latest write, in real-time order; the gold standard, most expensive); **Sequential / causal** (operations respect causal order — if A caused B, everyone sees A before B — but unrelated operations may be seen in different orders); **Read-your-writes / monotonic reads** (*session* guarantees — you always see your *own* writes and never go backwards in time, even if others' writes lag); **Eventual** (replicas converge to the same state *eventually*, with *no* ordering or recency guarantee in the meantime — weakest, cheapest, most available). The art is picking the **weakest level that's still correct** for each operation — strong where being wrong loses money, eventual where staleness is harmless.

**How it actually works.** Each level is a *guarantee about what reads can observe*:
- **Linearizable/strong:** there's a single, real-time total order of operations; once a write completes, *every* subsequent read (anywhere) sees it. Requires coordination/consensus on every operation (quorum reads+writes, or a single leader that all reads go through) → highest latency, lowest availability (CP, 12.2). The behavior of a single-node database, recreated across nodes.
- **Causal:** preserves cause→effect order (if you read X then write Y based on it, anyone who sees Y also sees X), but concurrent (unrelated) operations can be observed in different orders on different nodes. Cheaper than strong (no global total order needed), stronger than eventual.
- **Read-your-writes / monotonic:** *per-session* guarantees — after *you* write, *your* reads see it (RYW, M10/10.6); your reads never move backward in time (monotonic). Achieved by routing your reads to a node that has your writes (sticky routing or GTID-wait, M10/10.6). Doesn't constrain what *other* sessions see.
- **Eventual:** the only promise is *convergence* — if writes stop, all replicas eventually agree. In the meantime any read may be arbitrarily stale or out-of-order. Cheapest, most available (AP), needs conflict resolution if concurrent writes diverge.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The spectrum exists because **different data has different consistency needs, and always using the strongest level is wasteful (slow, less available) while always using the weakest is wrong (loses money).** It solves the "one consistency level for everything" mistake by giving a *menu*: match each operation to the weakest level that keeps it correct. A balance read driving an authorization decision needs **strong/linearizable** (any staleness → overdraft/double-spend → money lost); a user viewing their *own* transaction list needs **read-your-writes** (they must see the payment they just made, M10/10.6 — but slight staleness of *others'* data is fine); a public dashboard or analytics report tolerates **eventual** (a few seconds stale is harmless). The spectrum lets you *pay for consistency only where it's worth it* — the foundation of cost-effective distributed design (12.15).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The spectrum *is* a tradeoff ladder: **strong** (correct everywhere, but slow + low-availability + expensive coordination) → **causal** (preserves causality, cheaper, but concurrent ops unordered) → **RYW/monotonic** (cheap session guarantees, but only about *your* view) → **eventual** (fastest + most available, but no recency/order guarantee, needs conflict resolution). Stronger = more coordination = more latency + less availability (PACELC's EC; CAP's CP). Weaker = less coordination = faster + more available (EL; AP), but you must *tolerate or repair* staleness/conflicts. There's no universally right level — only the right level *per operation*. The alternative to choosing per operation (one global level) is either wasteful (everything strong) or unsafe (everything eventual); the spectrum's whole point is *granular* choice (12.15). For money, the rule: **strong + atomic for the money-moving/authorizing operations; eventual + idempotent + reconciled for propagation and reporting.**

**Generics / first-principles.** "Consistency is a spectrum from linearizable (acts like one copy, expensive) to eventual (converges someday, cheap) — pick the *weakest level that's still correct* for each operation, because stronger always costs more coordination/latency/availability." This is the universal consistency-models hierarchy (linearizable → sequential → causal → eventual, plus session guarantees like RYW/monotonic) studied across all distributed systems. The transferable insight: **consistency is not all-or-nothing — it's a graded resource you spend per operation, and good distributed design uses the cheapest sufficient level everywhere, reserving strong consistency for the operations whose incorrectness is unacceptable.** It transfers to every system offering tunable consistency (Cassandra/Dynamo levels, Cosmos DB's five explicit levels, Spanner's strong-by-default-but-stale-reads-available). The deep principle: **most data doesn't need strong consistency — and recognizing which data *does* (the money-moving operations) vs which doesn't (everything else) is the core skill** of distributed data design.

**MySQL-specific reality.** MySQL realizes the spectrum through *where you read and how synchronously you write*: **strong/linearizable-ish** = read from the primary (or group-replication majority) so you see the latest committed state (the money-decision path); **read-your-writes** = route a user's post-write reads to the primary or a GTID-caught-up replica (M10/10.6 — `WAIT_FOR_EXECUTED_GTID_SET`); **eventual** = read from async replicas (M10/10.5 — fast, stale-tolerant, for reports/display). The write side's consistency is the sync mode (M10/10.4 — semi-sync/group replication for stronger, async for eventual). MySQL doesn't have a single "consistency level" knob like Cassandra; you *compose* the level from read-routing + sync-mode choices (12.15). For our domain: a **balance-for-authorization** reads from the **primary** (strong — never authorize against a stale balance); a user's **own transaction history** uses **read-your-writes** (M10/10.6 — they must see their just-made payment); **reporting/reconciliation** reads **async replicas** (eventual — staleness harmless, offloads the primary, M02/2.17). The consistency spectrum is the framework for the M10 read-routing decisions, made per-operation. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the ladder with cost/availability axes and the per-operation money mapping.

## 12.5 · Eventual consistency & convergence

**Mental model.** **Eventual consistency** is the weakest useful level (bottom of the spectrum, 12.4): replicas/copies are *temporarily different* but **converge** to the same state once all updates propagate and no new writes arrive. It's the natural state of asynchronous replication (M10/10.5 — a replica lags, then catches up) and of any system that prioritizes availability/latency over instant agreement (AP, EL). It's *acceptable* when staleness is tolerable and any concurrent-write conflicts are resolvable — and *dangerous* when a stale read drives an irreversible decision (a money authorization). The key skills: knowing *when* eventual is safe (display, reporting, propagation — paired with idempotency + reconciliation) vs unsafe (money decisions), and how copies **converge** (and how conflicts are resolved when concurrent writes diverge).

**How it actually works.** In an eventually-consistent setup, a write is accepted by *some* node and propagated to others *asynchronously* (M10's binlog→replica flow, 10.2). Between the write and full propagation, different nodes hold *different* versions — a read may hit a node that hasn't received the update yet (**stale read**, 10.5/10.6). Once propagation completes (and if no new conflicting writes arrive), all nodes hold the *same* state — **convergence**. If *concurrent* writes happened on different nodes (possible in multi-primary/AP systems, or after an AP partition heals, 12.2), the copies *diverged* and must be **reconciled** via a **conflict-resolution** rule: **last-write-wins** (by timestamp — simple, but can lose data), **merge/CRDTs** (commutative structures that converge automatically — e.g., a counter that sums all increments), or **application-level** resolution (domain logic decides). For money, *convergence is not enough* — you also need to know the converged value is *correct* (no lost/duplicated write), which is why eventual consistency for money is *always* paired with **idempotency** (12.9 — so retries/duplicates don't corrupt) and **reconciliation** (12.14 — to detect/repair drift).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Eventual consistency exists to buy **availability and low latency** (AP, EL) for data that doesn't need instant agreement — most data. It solves the cost problem of strong consistency: not every read needs the latest write *right now*, so for those, you skip the expensive coordination (quorum/consensus) and let copies catch up asynchronously — getting faster writes, lower latency, higher availability, and easy scaling (M10's read replicas, 10.1). It's the foundation of read-scaling (offload stale-tolerant reads to async replicas) and of resilient, available systems (serve from any node, converge later). It exists *because* demanding strong consistency everywhere would make systems slow and brittle — eventual consistency is the pragmatic default for everything that can tolerate it, freeing strong consistency for the operations that truly need it.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Eventual consistency trades **recency/ordering guarantees** for **availability, latency, and scale**: you get fast, available, scalable reads/writes, but reads can be stale or out-of-order until convergence, and concurrent writes need conflict resolution (with its own pitfalls — last-write-wins can silently lose updates). The alternatives are stronger levels (12.4): **causal** (preserve cause→effect — avoids some anomalies, more cost), **read-your-writes** (session freshness — avoids the user-confusion case, M10/10.6), **strong** (no staleness — but slow/CP). For money, eventual consistency is acceptable *only* with guardrails: **idempotency** (12.9 — duplicates/retries during convergence don't double-apply), **reconciliation** (12.14 — detect/repair any non-convergence or lost write), and **never reading eventually-consistent data for an irreversible money decision** (use strong/RYW there, 12.4). The tradeoff is fine for *propagation, integration, display, and reporting*; it's a money-loss bug for *authorization and balance decisions*.

**Generics / first-principles.** "If you stop writing, all copies eventually agree — but in the meantime reads can be stale or out-of-order, and concurrent writes must be reconciled to converge." This is the universal **eventual consistency / convergence** model (BASE — Basically Available, Soft-state, Eventually-consistent — the counterpoint to ACID; Dynamo's model; the foundation of CRDTs and anti-entropy). The transferable insight: **eventual consistency buys availability and latency by letting copies temporarily disagree and converge later — it's safe when staleness is tolerable and conflicts are resolvable, and the engineering work is in the *convergence* (propagation + conflict resolution) and in *not* reading eventually-consistent data for decisions that can't tolerate staleness.** It transfers to every AP system (Dynamo, Cassandra, DNS, Git's eventual convergence, CRDTs in collaborative apps). The deep principle for money: **convergence guarantees copies *agree*, not that they're *correct* — so eventual consistency for money must be paired with idempotency (no double-apply) and reconciliation (detect/repair), and never trusted for an irreversible decision.**

**MySQL-specific reality.** MySQL async replication (M10/10.5) *is* eventual consistency: replicas lag the primary, then converge as they apply the binlog (10.2) — a replica read is a read of a *past* (eventually-current) state. MySQL replication is *single-primary* by default (no concurrent-write conflicts to resolve — the primary is the single writer, so convergence is just "replicas catch up," no conflict resolution needed); **multi-primary group replication** *can* have conflicts, handled by its **certification** (a transaction that conflicts with a concurrent one on another node is *rolled back* — optimistic conflict detection, not silent divergence). So MySQL's common eventual consistency (async replicas) is the *easy* kind (single writer → just catch up). For our domain: **reporting/reconciliation/display reads** run on async replicas (eventual — fine, staleness harmless, offloads the primary, M02/2.17); a **balance-for-authorization** never reads an async replica (it reads the primary — strong, 12.4 — because a stale balance → double-spend); cross-system propagation (CDC, 12.12) is eventual *and* made safe by **idempotent consumers** (12.13) + **reconciliation** (12.14). MySQL's eventual consistency is the read-scaling workhorse — used everywhere staleness is tolerable, guarded everywhere it isn't. The Pass C Mermaid draws diverge→propagate→converge (+ conflict resolution).
