## 11.6 · The shard key: the most important decision ★

**Mental model.** The **shard key** is the column (or columns) whose value decides which shard a row lives on — and it is **the single highest-leverage, hardest-to-reverse decision in a sharded system**. It governs four things at once: **co-location** (which rows end up together — can a transfer's two legs be one shard? 11.9), **routing** (can a query find its shard from its filter, or must it fan out to all? 11.10), **load distribution** (do writes/reads spread evenly, or does one shard melt? — the hotspot), and **cardinality** (are there enough distinct key values to split finely). Choose it well and the system feels almost single-node (most queries hit one shard, transfers stay atomic, load is even). Choose it badly and you live in scatter-gather, hotspots, and cross-shard transactions forever — and *changing the shard key on a live system is among the hardest operations in all of data engineering* (11.14). Get this one right.

**How it actually works.** Every sharded table is split on its shard key; the routing layer (11.13) computes `shard = scheme(shard_key_value)` (11.7) to place and find rows. The four properties play out as: **(1) Co-location** — rows sharing the shard-key value land on the same shard, so anything accessed/transacted *together* should *share* the key (e.g., shard the ledger by `account_id` and all of an account's entries are on one shard with it). **(2) Routing/locality** — a query that filters by the shard key routes to exactly one shard (fast, single-node); a query that *doesn't* must scatter-gather to *all* shards (11.10) — so the shard key should match your *most common/critical* query's filter. **(3) Even distribution** — the key's values must spread load uniformly; a low-cardinality or skewed key (e.g., `country` with 80% one value, or `merchant_id` where one merchant does 50% of volume) creates a **hotspot shard** that bottlenecks the whole system (the hot-account problem, M08's hot row lifted to a hot shard). **(4) Cardinality** — enough distinct values to divide across many shards and to reshard finer later. The art is finding one key that satisfies all four for *your* access pattern.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The shard key exists because sharding *must* split on *something*, and that something determines whether the sharded system is usable or a constant fight. It's the lever that decides how much of single-node life you keep: single-shard transactions (11.9/11.11), single-shard queries (11.10), even load, and feasible resharding. A good shard key makes the common path single-shard (cheap, ACID, fast) and pushes the cross-shard cases to the rare periphery; a bad one makes the common path cross-shard (expensive, distributed, slow). Because it's so hard to change (11.14 — re-keying means re-distributing every row and rewriting routing), the choice is effectively permanent, which is *why* it deserves more design effort than almost anything else in the system. It's the decision the whole rest of the architecture is built on.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The shard-key choice is a *tension* between the four properties, often forcing tradeoffs: a key that **co-locates** well (e.g., `customer_id` to keep a customer's accounts together) may **distribute** poorly (a whale customer = a hotspot); a key with perfect **even distribution** (a hash of a unique ID) may **co-locate** poorly (a transfer's two accounts hash to different shards → cross-shard, 11.9/11.11); the key that matches **query A** may not match **query B** (one of them scatter-gathers). Common candidate keys for payments and their tradeoffs (the recurring worked example): **`account_id`** (co-locates an account's entries + balance, even if accounts are numerous and uniform; but a transfer between two accounts is cross-shard unless they share a shard); **`customer_id`** (co-locates all of a customer's accounts → intra-customer transfers single-shard; but whale customers hotspot and cross-customer transfers are cross-shard); **`transaction_id`** (perfectly even, but co-locates *nothing* — every balance read scatter-gathers — almost always wrong). There's rarely a perfect key; you choose the *least-bad* for your dominant access pattern and accept the rest as cross-shard.

**Generics / first-principles.** "The partition key determines co-location, routing, and load balance — pick it so that data accessed *together* lives *together*, load spreads *evenly*, and your hottest query routes to *one* partition; it's the dominant, near-permanent design decision of any sharded system." This is universal and identically framed in every sharded store (Cassandra/Dynamo *partition key*, Mongo *shard key*, Vitess *vindex*, Kafka *partition key*): the same four properties (co-location, routing, even distribution, cardinality) and the same warning (it's the hardest thing to change). The transferable insight: **sharding's entire success or failure rides on the partition key — it's not a tuning knob, it's the foundational schema decision, chosen from the *access pattern* (what's queried and transacted together), not from the data model in isolation.** Choosing it is a *workload* decision (study the queries/transactions), not a *data* decision — the deepest principle of distributed data modeling.

**MySQL-specific reality.** In app-level sharding the shard key is whatever column your routing code hashes/ranges on; in **Vitess** it's the **Vindex** (the shard-key index that maps key → shard, with primary/secondary vindexes for routing on more than one column). MySQL itself doesn't know it's sharded — the key's enforcement (and the pain of a bad one) lives in the routing layer (11.13) and the schema design. For our domain, the canonical choice: **shard the ledger by `account_id`** (or a derived `ledger_group`/`tenant_id`) so each account's balance and entries co-locate, load spreads across accounts, and the *dominant* operation — reading/updating one account's balance and entries — is single-shard ACID. The sharp edge: a **transfer between two accounts** touches two account_ids → potentially two shards (11.9 shows how to co-locate them via the key design; 11.11 handles the genuinely-cross-shard case). The hot-account/hot-merchant problem (one popular account or merchant concentrating load on one shard) is the money-relevant distribution failure (M08's hot row → a hot shard) and shapes the key choice. This is the decision M16 (the platform) is built on; the ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the four properties and the account_id-vs-customer_id-vs-transaction_id comparison.

## 11.7 · Sharding schemes: range, hash, directory ★

**Mental model.** Given a shard key, the **scheme** is *how* its value maps to a shard — and there are three, mirroring the partitioning types (11.3) but across servers. **Range sharding:** shards own ordered key ranges (accounts 0–1M → shard 1, 1M–2M → shard 2) — supports range queries and is simple, but the *newest* range is a write hotspot (sequential keys all land on the last shard) and ranges can grow uneven. **Hash sharding:** `shard = hash(key) mod N` — spreads keys *evenly* (no hotspot), but destroys order (range queries can't route → scatter-gather, 11.10) and naive `mod N` makes resharding catastrophic (11.8). **Directory (lookup) sharding:** a *lookup table/service* maps key → shard explicitly — maximally flexible (move any key to any shard, rebalance hotspots, place specific tenants) at the cost of an indirection (every route does a lookup) and the directory becoming a critical component. Each is the same range-vs-hash-vs-flexibility tradeoff you'll choose deliberately.

**How it actually works.**
- **Range:** maintain shard boundaries (`[lo, hi)` per shard). Route by finding the shard whose range contains the key. **Pros:** range queries route to a contiguous set of shards; resharding a range = splitting one range in two (relatively local). **Cons:** **sequential keys hotspot** (monotonic IDs/timestamps all hit the highest range → the newest shard takes all new writes, M08's insert hotspot at shard scale); uneven ranges (some ranges denser) cause unbalanced shards.
- **Hash:** `shard = hash(key) mod N`. **Pros:** **even distribution** (a good hash spreads any key, including sequential ones, uniformly → no hotspot); simple routing (compute the hash). **Cons:** **no range routing** (hashing destroys order, so a range query scatter-gathers to all shards, 11.10); and **naive `mod N` is reshard-hostile** — changing N remaps almost every key (11.8 — fixed by consistent hashing).
- **Directory:** a `key → shard` map (a lookup table, often itself replicated/cached). Route by looking up the key (or a coarser bucket of it) in the directory. **Pros:** **total flexibility** — move individual keys/buckets between shards to rebalance hotspots, place big tenants on dedicated shards, reshard incrementally. **Cons:** the **lookup indirection** (latency + the directory is a critical dependency and potential bottleneck/SPOF — must be made HA/cached); the directory grows with key cardinality (usually bucketed to keep it small).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The schemes exist to navigate **order-locality vs even-distribution vs flexibility** — the same fundamental tradeoff as partitioning (11.3), now choosing it across nodes with resharding consequences. Range solves *range queries + simple splitting* but suffers sequential hotspots. Hash solves *even load* (critical when keys are sequential or skewed) but loses range queries and (naively) reshards terribly. Directory solves *flexibility/rebalancing* (the real-world need to move hot keys and reshard incrementally) at the cost of indirection. Most large production systems converge on **hash (with consistent hashing, 11.8)** for even load + reshard-friendliness, *or* **directory/bucketed** for flexibility (Vitess's range-based vindexes over a *hashed* keyspace are effectively this) — pure range sharding on a monotonic key is usually a hotspot trap.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** **Range:** range queries + easy splits, but sequential-write hotspot + skew. **Hash:** even load + skew-proof, but no range queries + naive-mod reshard pain (use consistent hashing, 11.8). **Directory:** flexible rebalancing + incremental reshard + per-tenant placement, but indirection latency + a critical directory component. The practical resolution most systems use: **hash the key to destroy skew/sequential hotspots, then range-partition the *hash space* into shards** (so resharding splits a hash-range, and a "directory" of hash-ranges → shards stays small) — this is essentially what **Vitess** does (hash vindex → keyspace IDs → ranges). For the ledger: hashing `account_id` spreads accounts evenly (no hot newest-account shard) — but then you must *engineer co-location* of a transfer's two legs deliberately (11.9), since hashing scatters them. The scheme choice is downstream of "do I need range queries on the shard key?" (almost never for account-id access → hash) and "do I need to rebalance hot keys?" (yes → some directory/flexibility).

**Generics / first-principles.** "Map keys to nodes by *order* (range — locality, but hotspots), by *hash* (uniform, but no locality), or by *explicit lookup* (flexible, but indirection) — the same order-vs-spread tradeoff as any partition function, now with resharding stakes." Universal across distributed stores: DynamoDB/Cassandra hash-partition (even load, no range scans without a sort key), HBase/Bigtable range-partition (range scans, but region hotspots on sequential keys — the famous "salt your keys" advice is exactly avoiding the sequential-range hotspot), and many systems add a directory/metadata service (HDFS NameNode, Vitess topology) for flexible mapping. The transferable insight: **hashing buys uniformity by destroying order; ranging preserves order at the cost of uniformity; a directory buys flexibility at the cost of an indirection — and you choose based on whether your workload needs range-locality, even-spread, or dynamic rebalancing.** It's the same structural choice as 11.3 (partitioning) and even M05 (B+Tree-ordered vs hash index) — order vs uniformity, everywhere.

**MySQL-specific reality.** App-level sharding implements whichever scheme you code (a hash function + mod/ring, range boundaries, or a lookup table). **Vitess** provides **Vindexes**: a **hash vindex** (e.g., `hash`, `xxhash`) maps the shard key to a **keyspace ID**, and the keyspace is divided into **shard ranges** over those keyspace IDs — combining hash (even spread, skew-proof) *with* range-partitioned-hash-space (so resharding splits a keyspace-ID range, and Vitess can split/merge shards cleanly, 11.14). This hash-then-range-the-hash-space design is why Vitess reshards gracefully where naive `mod N` can't (11.8). For our domain: shard the ledger by a **hash of `account_id`** (even load across accounts, no hotspot on newest accounts) — and because hashing scatters a transfer's two accounts, *deliberately co-locate* the two legs (11.9) via the key design (e.g., a shared `ledger_group` or by structuring internal transfers through per-shard intermediary accounts). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws range vs hash vs directory mapping with the spread/range-query/flexibility tradeoffs.

## 11.8 · Consistent hashing & minimizing reshuffle ★

**Mental model.** The fatal flaw of naive **`hash(key) mod N`** sharding: when you change N (add or remove a shard), *almost every key* remaps to a different shard — going from 8 → 9 shards moves roughly **8/9 of all keys** (any key where `hash mod 8 ≠ hash mod 9`), which on a live system means re-copying nearly the entire dataset under load. **Consistent hashing** fixes this: place both shards and keys on a **hash ring** (a circular hash space); a key belongs to the *next shard clockwise* on the ring. Adding a shard inserts one point on the ring and steals only the **arc between it and its predecessor** — so only ~**1/N of keys move** (just those in the new shard's arc), and removing a shard only moves *its* keys to the next one. **Virtual nodes** (each physical shard placed at many ring positions) smooth out the distribution and make rebalancing even. Consistent hashing is *the* technique that makes resharding (11.14) survivable.

**How it actually works.** Hash the shard key into a large circular space (e.g., 0…2³²−1, wrapping around). Hash *each shard* (or its ID) to one or more points on the same ring. A key is owned by the **first shard encountered going clockwise** from the key's position. **Adding shard X:** hash X onto the ring; X now owns the arc from its position back to the previous shard point — only the keys in *that arc* (previously owned by the next-clockwise shard) move to X; **every other key stays put**. **Removing a shard:** its arc's keys move to the next-clockwise shard; nothing else moves. **Virtual nodes (vnodes):** because a few random points distribute unevenly (some arcs much bigger), each physical shard is hashed to *many* points (e.g., 100–200 vnodes each) — the arcs interleave finely, so load is even *and* adding/removing a shard pulls a little from *many* shards rather than dumping a whole arc on one neighbor. The result: a key's shard is stable under membership changes except for the ~1/N that genuinely must move.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** It exists to make **resharding affordable**. Resharding (changing shard count, 11.14) is already the hardest operation; if it required moving ~all the data (naive mod-N), it would be nearly impossible to do live on a large system. Consistent hashing reduces the data movement to the **minimum theoretically necessary** (~1/N per added shard) and *localizes* it (only the affected arcs), so you can grow/shrink the cluster by moving a small, bounded fraction of data while everything else keeps serving from where it already is. It solves the specific, brutal problem "how do I add capacity to a sharded system without re-copying everything" — the difference between a sharded system that *can* grow and one that's frozen at its initial shard count.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** **Consistent hashing:** minimal, localized reshuffle on membership change (great for elastic clusters), but more complex than mod-N and (without vnodes) uneven arcs; vnodes fix the evenness at the cost of more metadata. **Naive `mod N`:** trivially simple and perfectly even *for a fixed N*, but catastrophic to reshard (remaps ~all keys) — acceptable only if you *never* change N (or accept a full migration when you do). **Range + directory (Vitess style):** an alternative to consistent hashing that gets reshard-friendliness differently — hash to a keyspace ID, range-partition the keyspace, and **split/merge ranges** (Vitess) so you move only the rows in the split range; this achieves the same "move only ~1/N" goal via explicit range splits rather than a ring. The practical landscape: **DHT/NoSQL systems** (Cassandra, Dynamo, Riak) use **consistent hashing + vnodes**; **Vitess** uses **hash-into-keyspace + range-split** (functionally similar reshard cost, more explicit control). Either way, the lesson is the same: **never shard on naive `mod N` if you'll ever change N.**

**Generics / first-principles.** "To add/remove nodes while moving the *minimum* data, map keys and nodes into a shared space and have each node own a contiguous region — then membership changes move only the affected region (~1/N), not everything." This is the foundational **consistent hashing** result (Karger et al., born for distributed web caches), now ubiquitous in distributed systems: Cassandra/Dynamo/Riak token rings, memcached client distribution, CDN/cache key placement, many load balancers. The transferable insight: **the cost of changing cluster membership is determined by your key→node mapping — `mod N` couples every key to N (so changing N moves everything), while a ring/keyspace mapping couples each key only to its local region (so changing membership moves only that region).** It's a deep idea about *minimizing coupling between data placement and cluster size* — the same instinct as stable hashing in load balancing and rendezvous hashing. Vnodes add the second principle: *spread each node across many small regions for even load and gentle rebalancing.*

**MySQL-specific reality.** Naive **app-level `account_id mod N` sharding is the classic trap** — it works until you must add a shard, then requires re-routing ~all accounts (a massive, risky migration). App-level sharding that anticipates growth uses **consistent hashing** (a ring with vnodes) or a **bucketed directory** (map keys → many fixed buckets, then buckets → shards, so adding a shard just reassigns some buckets — moving only those buckets' rows). **Vitess** sidesteps naive mod-N entirely with its **hash-vindex → keyspace-ID → range-shard** model and **`Reshard`** workflow that **splits/merges keyspace ranges**, copying only the rows in the affected range and cutting over via replication (11.14) — production-grade reshard-friendliness without you hand-rolling a ring. For our domain: shard the ledger by **hash of `account_id` over a ring (or Vitess keyspace)**, *not* `account_id mod N`, so that when the platform grows from 8 → 16 shards you move only ~half-of-one-shard's worth per split rather than re-homing every account — turning resharding (11.14) from impossible into a routine, tooled operation. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the ring with vnodes and the "add a shard moves only its arc" property.

## 11.9 · Co-location: keeping related data together ★

**Mental model.** **Co-location** is the design rule that makes sharding *survivable for money*: shard so that **data accessed and transacted together lives on the same shard**. The fintech instance is non-negotiable — **a transfer's debit and credit must land on the same shard**, so the double-entry write stays a *single-node ACID transaction* (M07–M09, all guarantees intact) instead of becoming a *distributed* transaction (11.11, M12 — slow, complex, loss-prone). Co-location is achieved by choosing/structuring the **shard key** so related rows share it: shard by a key both legs share (a `ledger_group`, a `tenant_id`, a `customer_id` when both accounts are one customer's), or route money between shards through **per-shard intermediary accounts** so each *physical* transfer is local. Co-location is how you keep the *common* path single-shard and atomic, paying the cross-shard tax (11.11) only where genuinely unavoidable.

**How it actually works.** Because rows sharing the shard-key value land on the same shard (11.6), you *engineer* the shard key so things-used-together share it:
- **Shard by a co-locating key.** If most transfers are *within a tenant/customer*, shard by `tenant_id`/`customer_id` → both accounts of an intra-tenant transfer are on one shard → the debit+credit+balance updates are *one local ACID transaction*. Most operations (a tenant's accounts, entries, transfers) become single-shard.
- **Group entities explicitly.** Assign a shared `ledger_group`/`shard_id` to sets of accounts that transact together; shard on it. Rows in a group co-locate by construction.
- **Intermediary-account pattern for cross-shard money.** For transfers that *must* cross shards (different tenants/shards), don't do one cross-shard atomic write — split it into two *local* transactions via a **clearing/intermediary account per shard**: debit source + credit shard-A's clearing account (local, atomic), then credit destination + debit shard-B's clearing account (local, atomic), reconciled/settled between clearing accounts (Saga-style, M12). Each *physical* DB transaction stays single-shard; the cross-shard movement is an *application-level* protocol over local transactions.
- **Reference data replicated everywhere.** Small shared tables (currencies, account-type metadata) are *replicated to every shard* (not sharded) so single-shard transactions can join them locally without cross-shard reads.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Co-location exists because **cross-shard transactions are the thing sharding makes expensive and dangerous (11.11), and co-location is how you avoid them.** Without it, a naive shard (e.g., hash every account independently) makes *every* transfer a coin-flip to be cross-shard → most money movements become distributed transactions → the system is slow, complex, and exposed to partial-failure money loss. With it, the *dominant* money operation (a transfer) stays a single-node ACID transaction — keeping all of M07–M09's correctness and durability for free — and only the genuinely-cross-boundary cases pay the distributed-protocol cost. Co-location is the single most important *application* of the shard key (11.6); it's what turns "we sharded the ledger" from a correctness liability into a working system. For money, **keeping the debit and credit atomic is the whole game** — co-location is how sharding preserves it.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Co-location forces tradeoffs against even distribution (11.6's tension): a co-locating key like `customer_id`/`tenant_id` risks **hotspots** (a whale tenant concentrates load on one shard) where a fully-hashed key would spread evenly — so you balance "keep transfers local" against "spread load," sometimes splitting whales out via a directory (11.7) or dedicated shards. The **intermediary-account pattern** keeps every DB transaction local but adds application complexity and an *eventual*-consistency settlement step (the two local transactions aren't instantaneously atomic — there's a brief in-flight state, managed as a Saga with idempotency, M12/M16). The alternative — actually doing **cross-shard distributed transactions** (2PC/XA, 11.11/M12) — keeps strict atomicity but is slow, lock-heavy, and failure-prone (why it's avoided). The practical stance: **design the shard key to make the overwhelming majority of money movements single-shard (co-located), and handle the rare cross-shard movements with a Saga over local transactions** — accept eventual settlement there rather than fragile cross-shard atomicity.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Partition so that data touched by the same operation shares a partition — co-locate by the *transaction boundary*, so the common operations stay local (single-partition, ACID, fast) and only rare operations cross partitions." This is the universal **co-location / locality-of-reference principle** in distributed data: Cassandra's "model your partition key around your queries so each query hits one partition," DynamoDB's single-table/co-located-item-collection design, Mongo's choosing a shard key that keeps related documents together, Vitess's "keyspace + sharding so related rows share a keyspace ID." The transferable insight: **in any partitioned system, performance and correctness come from making operations *local to one partition* — you design the partition key from the *operations* (what's transacted/queried together), and you accept that cross-partition operations are a different, costlier mode you minimize.** The deeper money-specific principle: **keep an invariant's data on one node so the invariant stays a single-node transaction** — the debit=credit invariant lives or dies on co-locating the two legs.

**MySQL-specific reality.** App-level: choose the shard key (or a derived grouping column) so a transfer's two accounts share it, and structure cross-shard movement via per-shard clearing accounts + a settlement process (M12/M16). **Vitess** directly supports this: **keyspace IDs** let you co-locate related rows (rows you want together get the same keyspace ID → same shard), and Vitess routes a transaction touching one keyspace ID to one shard (single-shard, fast, atomic); it also supports (but discourages) cross-shard transactions. Vitess's **`sequences`** and **reference tables** (replicated to all shards) handle global IDs (11.12) and shared lookup data. For our domain: shard the ledger so **both legs of an internal transfer co-locate** (by `tenant_id`/`ledger_group`, or via a hashed key plus the intermediary-account pattern for cross-group transfers) → the debit+credit+balance update is a **single-shard ACID transaction** (the money-never-lies invariant, M07/7.16, preserved at scale); currencies/account-types are **reference tables replicated to every shard**; genuinely cross-shard transfers use a **Saga with idempotency keys** (M12/M16) over local transactions, with reconciliation (M02/2.17) catching drift. This is *the* fintech sharding design — the ★ SVG (Pass C) draws both legs of a transfer on one shard (local ACID) vs the cross-shard intermediary-account path.

## 11.10 · Cross-shard queries & scatter-gather

**Mental model.** A query that filters by the **shard key** routes to **one shard** (fast, single-node — the happy path). A query that *doesn't* filter by the shard key can't know which shard has the rows, so it must **scatter** to *every* shard, run there, and **gather/merge** the partial results — **scatter-gather**. It works, but it's slow (as slow as the slowest shard, × the merge cost), it loads *all* shards for *one* query (the opposite of scaling), and the merge gets genuinely hard for **aggregations** (sum/count across shards), **joins** (across shards), **sorting** (global ORDER BY over per-shard results), and **pagination** (global LIMIT/OFFSET over scattered, sorted data). Scatter-gather is the cost of any access pattern that doesn't align with the shard key — minimize it by choosing the key for your dominant query (11.6) and by keeping cross-cutting reads on a *separate* system (a search index or a replica-fed warehouse).

**How it actually works.** The routing layer (11.13) inspects the query: if it has an equality (or range, for range sharding) on the shard key → route to the owning shard(s). If not → **fan the query out to all N shards in parallel**, each returns its matching rows, and the router **merges**: for a plain `SELECT … WHERE non_shard_col = ?`, concatenate; for **aggregations** (`SUM`, `COUNT`, `AVG`), compute partial aggregates per shard and combine (sum the sums, but `AVG`/`COUNT(DISTINCT)`/median need care); for **`ORDER BY … LIMIT k`**, each shard returns its top-k, then merge-sort and take the global top-k; for **`LIMIT … OFFSET n`** deep pagination, each shard must return up to `offset+limit` rows to guarantee correctness (deep offsets are brutal — every shard ships a huge prefix); for **joins** across shards, either co-locate the joined tables (11.9), replicate the small side to every shard (reference tables), or do an expensive cross-shard join in the router/app. Latency = slowest shard + merge; load = *every* shard pays for *one* query.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Scatter-gather isn't a feature you want — it's the *unavoidable consequence* of asking a sharded system a question it isn't sharded for. It "solves" the need to occasionally query across the whole dataset (a global report, a search by a non-shard-key attribute), but at a cost that doesn't scale (every shard involved). Its existence is *why* the shard-key choice (11.6) matters so much — you pick the key so your *frequent/critical* queries are single-shard and only *rare* queries scatter — and *why* sharded systems push cross-cutting access to other tools (search indexes, analytics replicas/warehouses) rather than scatter-gathering the operational shards. Understanding scatter-gather is understanding *which queries sharding makes cheap and which it makes expensive*, which drives the whole data-access architecture.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Scatter-gather trades *generality* (you *can* query by anything) for *cost* (every shard pays, merge is hard, latency = slowest shard). The alternatives that avoid it: **(1) choose the shard key for the dominant query** (11.6) so it's single-shard; **(2) secondary indexes/lookup tables** mapping a second attribute → shard key (Vitess *secondary vindex*) so a query on that attribute routes instead of scatters; **(3) replicate/denormalize** — keep a copy of the data organized by the *other* access pattern (a second sharded table sharded differently, or a search index like Elasticsearch fed by CDC, M10/10.14, M12); **(4) offload cross-cutting reads** (global reports, analytics, "all accounts where…") to a **replica-fed data warehouse / read model** (M02/2.17, M13) instead of the operational shards. The discipline: **operational shards serve single-shard queries; everything cross-cutting goes to a purpose-built read model** — don't scatter-gather the money path. Deep pagination and cross-shard joins especially should be designed out, not optimized.

**Generics / first-principles.** "A query aligned with the partition key hits one partition (scales); a query not aligned must fan out to all and merge (doesn't scale) — so partition for your dominant query and serve cross-cutting queries from a separate read model." This is universal: Cassandra forbids/penalizes non-partition-key queries (`ALLOW FILTERING` is a footgun for exactly this reason), DynamoDB needs a **GSI** (a re-partitioned copy) to query by a non-key attribute, every sharded SQL system warns against non-shard-key and cross-shard joins, and search/analytics are universally offloaded to specialized stores. The transferable insight: **partitioning makes *aligned* access cheap and *unaligned* access fan-out-expensive — so a sharded system isn't one database but a *collection of single-partition databases*, and any query that crosses partitions is a distributed query you should avoid, route via a secondary index, or serve from a denormalized/replicated read model.** Aggregations, sorts, joins, and pagination across partitions are *distributed-query* problems (the same merge logic as MapReduce/parallel query engines) — design them out of the hot path.

**MySQL-specific reality.** App-level sharding: your code fans out to all shards and merges in the application (you implement the partial-aggregate/merge-sort/limit logic) — error-prone, which is a major reason to use Vitess. **Vitess** handles scatter-gather *transparently*: `vtgate` recognizes a non-shard-key query, fans it to all shards, and **merges results** (including pushing down aggregations and doing the cross-shard merge/sort/limit) — but it's still scatter-gather under the hood (every shard works), so Vitess exposes it and you design to avoid it; **secondary vindexes** let you route a query on a second column to the right shard instead of scattering. For our domain: a query like "**all of customer X's accounts**" is single-shard if sharded by `customer_id` (11.6) but scatter-gather if sharded by `account_id` (the accounts spread across shards) — the shard-key choice decides; "**total platform balance**" or "**all transactions over $10k today**" are inherently cross-cutting → serve them from a **replica-fed reporting warehouse** (M02/2.17, M13), *not* by scatter-gathering the live ledger shards. **Deep pagination** and **cross-shard joins** on the ledger are designed out (keep the money path single-shard; push reporting to the read model). The Pass C Mermaid draws the scatter-gather fan-out vs a single-shard hit.
