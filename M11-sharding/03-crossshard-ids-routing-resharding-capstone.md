## 11.11 · Cross-shard transactions (the hard problem) ★

**Mental model.** Within one shard, you have full single-node ACID (M07–M09): a transfer's debit and credit commit atomically and durably, or not at all. Across shards, **that guarantee evaporates** — there is no single redo log, no single commit, no single lock manager spanning two independent servers, so an operation touching two shards *cannot* be a single atomic transaction. You're forced into a **distributed transaction** (2PC/XA — slow, lock-heavy, blocking on coordinator failure) or a **Saga** (a sequence of local transactions with compensations — eventually consistent, application-complex) — both covered in M12. The crucial design conclusion: **the goal is not to do cross-shard transactions *well* — it's to *avoid* them** (via co-location, 11.9). When unavoidable, you accept either 2PC's fragility or Saga's eventual consistency. This is *the* hard problem sharding creates for money, and the bridge into M12.

**How it actually works.** A cross-shard operation (transfer from account A on shard 1 to account B on shard 2) has no native atomic path, so you choose a protocol:
- **Two-phase commit (2PC / XA):** a coordinator asks both shards to *prepare* (do the work, hold locks, promise to commit), and if *both* vote yes, tells both to *commit*; if either votes no, both *roll back*. Gives atomicity, but: holds locks across a network round-trip (long lock hold → contention, M08), and is **blocking** — if the coordinator crashes after prepare but before commit, the shards are stuck holding locks, uncertain (the classic 2PC failure). Slow and fragile; widely avoided (M12).
- **Saga (the usual choice):** break the cross-shard operation into a sequence of **local** transactions, each single-shard and atomic, with **compensating** transactions to undo earlier steps if a later one fails. The transfer becomes: local debit on shard 1 (atomic) → local credit on shard 2 (atomic); if the credit fails, run a compensating *re-credit* on shard 1. Combined with the **intermediary/clearing-account pattern** (11.9) and **idempotency keys** (M16) so steps are safely retryable. Eventually consistent (a brief in-flight state where money is "in transit"), but each DB transaction stays single-shard, and no distributed locks block.
- **The avoidance path (preferred):** design the shard key (11.9) so the operation is *single-shard* in the first place → it's just a normal ACID transaction, no distributed protocol at all.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The problem exists because **ACID is a single-node property** — atomicity and isolation come from one server's redo log, lock manager, and commit (M07–M09), and there is no free lunch that extends them across independent servers without a coordination protocol (and the coordination protocol *is* the cost: latency, locks, blocking, or eventual consistency). It "solves" the reality that *some* operations genuinely span shards (a transfer between two customers on different shards) — but it solves it expensively, which is precisely *why* co-location (11.9) and shard-key design (11.6) matter: they minimize how often you pay this cost. The cross-shard transaction is the concrete manifestation of "sharding trades single-node ACID for scale" — it's where that trade becomes a real, money-critical engineering problem, and it motivates the entire distributed-transactions chapter (M12).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** **2PC/XA:** strict atomicity, but synchronous, lock-holding, **blocking on coordinator failure**, poor throughput — avoided in high-scale fintech (the cure is worse than the disease for most money flows). **Saga:** non-blocking, single-shard local transactions, scalable — but **eventually consistent** (a visible in-flight state), requires **compensations** (which must themselves be correct and idempotent — "what does it mean to *undo* a credit?") and careful failure handling. **Co-location/avoidance (11.9):** best of all — *no* distributed transaction — but only possible when the shard key can keep the operation local (not always, e.g., genuinely cross-tenant transfers). The fintech resolution: **co-locate to make the vast majority of transfers single-shard ACID; for the unavoidable cross-shard minority, use a Saga over local transactions with idempotency + reconciliation (M02/2.17) to guarantee money is eventually correct and never lost/duplicated** — accepting eventual settlement there rather than fragile cross-shard atomicity. 2PC is reserved for the rare cases that truly need synchronous atomicity and can tolerate its cost.

**Generics / first-principles.** "ACID is a single-node guarantee; spanning nodes requires a distributed-commit protocol (2PC — atomic but blocking) or a compensation-based workflow (Saga — non-blocking but eventually consistent) — so design to keep transactions on one node." This is the universal **distributed-transaction problem**, identical everywhere data is partitioned: microservices (each service its own DB → cross-service operations are Sagas/2PC), all sharded stores, and the theory behind it (the impossibility of cheap distributed atomicity, the FLP/2PC-blocking results). The transferable insight: **the moment a transaction must span independently-committing nodes, you lose free atomicity and must choose between synchronous-blocking (2PC) and asynchronous-compensating (Saga) — both costly — so the dominant design principle is to *partition along transaction boundaries* (11.9) so transactions stay single-node.** This is *the* reason "model around your transaction boundaries" is the first rule of distributed data design — and it sets up M12 entirely (CAP/PACELC, 2PC/XA, Saga, outbox, idempotency).

**MySQL-specific reality.** MySQL supports **XA transactions** (`XA START/PREPARE/COMMIT`) for 2PC across resources, but they're **widely avoided** in production sharding (performance, the in-doubt/blocking problem on coordinator failure, historically buggy XA recovery, lock-holding) — covered critically in M12. **Vitess** can do limited cross-shard transactions (best-effort/2PC modes) but **strongly steers you to single-shard transactions** (its whole design rewards co-located, single-keyspace-ID operations). The production fintech pattern is **Saga + intermediary accounts + idempotency keys + reconciliation** (M16), keeping every DB transaction single-shard. For our domain: a transfer **within a shard** (co-located accounts, 11.9) is a normal **single-node ACID** transaction (debit + credit + balance atomic — M07/7.16, all guarantees) — the design goal for the *vast majority* of movements; a transfer **across shards** (different tenants/shards) is a **Saga** — debit source locally, credit destination locally (via clearing accounts), compensate on failure, with idempotency keys preventing double-application and reconciliation catching any drift (M02/2.17). The money-never-lies invariant is preserved by *avoiding* cross-shard atomicity (co-location) and, where unavoidable, by *eventual* correctness (Saga + reconciliation) — never by trusting fragile 2PC. This is the explicit bridge to M12; the ★ SVG (Pass C) contrasts single-shard ACID with the cross-shard distributed path.

## 11.12 · Distributed ID generation

**Mental model.** `AUTO_INCREMENT` is a *single-server* counter — on a sharded system it **breaks**: each shard would independently generate 1, 2, 3, … → the same ID on different shards → collisions, and no global ordering. You need **globally-unique IDs generated without a single bottleneck**. The options: **UUID** (random 128-bit — globally unique, no coordination, but large, unordered, and a *terrible* clustered PK because random inserts scatter across the B+Tree, M05/M09 — page splits, poor locality); **ULID/UUIDv7** (time-ordered UUIDs — unique *and* roughly sortable by creation time → good insert locality, the modern default); **Snowflake** (a 64-bit ID = timestamp + machine/shard ID + per-node sequence — compact, time-sortable, decentralized, used by Twitter/Discord); or a **central ticket/sequence server** (hand out ID ranges — coordination, but compact sequential IDs). The choice balances *uniqueness without coordination* against *sortability* and *index-friendliness*.

**How it actually works.**
- **UUID (v4, random):** 128-bit random value; collision probability negligible; generated anywhere with zero coordination. **But** as a clustered PK it's a disaster (M05/M09): random values insert into random B+Tree positions → constant page splits, poor buffer-pool locality, fragmentation, and larger secondary indexes (every secondary index stores the 16-byte PK). Store as `BINARY(16)`, not a 36-char string (M03/3.12), if used.
- **ULID / UUIDv7 (time-ordered):** high bits are a timestamp, low bits random → **monotonically increasing-ish** → inserts go to the *end* of the B+Tree (like AUTO_INCREMENT — good locality, few splits, M05/M09) *and* globally unique without coordination. The modern recommended scheme for sharded PKs.
- **Snowflake:** 64-bit = `timestamp || node_id || sequence`. Each node (shard/generator) embeds its unique `node_id` and a per-millisecond `sequence`, so IDs are unique *without coordination*, **time-sortable** (timestamp high bits), and **compact** (64-bit, half a UUID, BIGINT-friendly). Needs synchronized-ish clocks and unique node IDs (a small coordination at setup).
- **Central allocator (ticket server):** a dedicated service (or a `sequences` table) hands out **blocks** of IDs (e.g., 1000 at a time) to each shard/app → compact sequential IDs with only occasional coordination (per block, not per ID). Vitess provides this via **sequence tables**.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** It exists because **identity must be unique across the whole system, but generation must not require cross-shard coordination on every insert** (that would re-introduce a global bottleneck, defeating sharding). The schemes solve "unique IDs without a synchronous global counter" with different tradeoffs on *sortability* (matters for index locality — M05/M09 — and for time-ordered queries) and *size* (matters for PK/secondary-index footprint, M03/M05). For the ledger, IDs (entry IDs, transaction IDs) must be unique across all shards (so a transaction is globally identifiable, references resolve, idempotency keys work) *and* index-friendly (the ledger is insert-heavy; a random-UUID PK would wreck insert performance and bloat indexes). This is why time-ordered schemes (ULID/UUIDv7/Snowflake) win for sharded money systems.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** **UUIDv4:** zero coordination + universal uniqueness, but **unordered** (bad clustered PK — splits/fragmentation) and **large** (16 bytes, bloats every secondary index). **ULID/UUIDv7:** unique + time-ordered (good locality) + no coordination — the **default modern choice**; still 16 bytes. **Snowflake:** unique + time-sortable + **compact (8 bytes)** + decentralized — excellent, but needs unique node IDs + reasonable clock sync (clock skew/rewind is a real failure mode). **Central allocator:** compact sequential IDs, but a coordination point (mitigated by block allocation; the allocator must be HA). The decision: avoid random UUID as a PK in a sharded insert-heavy system; prefer **ULID/UUIDv7** (no infra) or **Snowflake** (compact, if you can manage node IDs/clocks) or a **Vitess sequence** (if on Vitess). Pair with the M03/3.12 lesson: store binary, not string; consider a separate sequential surrogate clustered PK + the UUID as a unique secondary key if you want both locality and an external-facing ID.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Generate globally-unique identifiers *locally* (no per-ID coordination) — and prefer *time-ordered* IDs so they're also sortable and index-friendly." This is the universal **distributed ID generation** problem, solved the same way everywhere: Twitter Snowflake (and its many clones — Sonyflake, Instagram's sharded ID scheme), ULID/KSUID/UUIDv7 across modern systems, and block-allocator/ticket-server designs (Flickr's, Vitess sequences). The transferable insight: **uniqueness without coordination comes from embedding enough entropy/identity locally (random bits, or node-id + timestamp + sequence); sortability and index-friendliness come from putting time in the high bits — so the best distributed IDs are *time-ordered + locally-unique*** (Snowflake/ULID), avoiding both the global-counter bottleneck and the random-insert penalty. The deep link: ID structure directly affects *storage-layer* performance (clustered-index locality, M05/M09) — identity and physical layout are coupled.

**MySQL-specific reality.** `AUTO_INCREMENT` is per-server (breaks on shards — colliding IDs). Practical MySQL sharded-ID options: **ULID/UUIDv7 stored as `BINARY(16)`** (time-ordered → good InnoDB clustered-index insert locality, M05/M09; vastly better than random `UUID()`/v4); **Snowflake-style BIGINT** (8 bytes, time-sortable, decentralized — compact PK, ideal if you manage node IDs/clocks); **Vitess `sequences`** (Vitess hands out IDs via a backing sequence table — global uniqueness with block allocation, the native Vitess answer); or **offset/interleaved AUTO_INCREMENT** (`auto_increment_increment`/`offset` so shard k generates k, k+N, k+2N… — works but rigid, breaks on resharding). Store IDs **binary, not as 36-char strings** (M03/3.12 — 16 vs 36 bytes × every index). For our domain: ledger entry/transaction IDs use **ULID/UUIDv7 (BINARY(16)) or Snowflake BIGINT** — globally unique across shards, time-ordered (insert-friendly on the append-heavy ledger, M05/M09), and usable as idempotency-key components (M16). Avoid random-UUID PKs (insert hotspots + index bloat). The Pass C Mermaid compares the ID schemes on uniqueness/sortability/size/coordination.

## 11.13 · The routing layer (Vitess & app-level) ★

**Mental model.** Something must translate "this query for account 1,234,567" into "connect to *this* shard" — the **routing layer**, the brain that knows the shard map and directs every query. Two approaches: **application-level routing** (your code computes the shard from the shard key and connects to the right server — total control, zero extra infrastructure, but the app owns *all* the complexity: routing, scatter-gather merges, cross-shard logic, resharding cutover — error-prone and invasive, spread across every service); or **a middleware/proxy** like **Vitess** (or sharding-aware ProxySQL setups) — a layer that **presents a single logical database** to the app and *transparently* shards underneath: it parses queries, routes by shard key, does scatter-gather + merges, manages shards and replicas, and — critically — **automates resharding** (11.14). The routing layer is what makes a sharded system *usable*; Vitess is the production-grade answer for MySQL.

**How it actually works.**
- **App-level:** the application holds the shard map (key-ranges or hash-ring or directory, 11.7/11.8) and a connection pool per shard. For each query it extracts the shard key, computes the shard, and routes; for cross-shard queries it fans out and merges *in app code*; for transactions it ensures single-shard (or implements Saga, 11.11); for resharding it must coordinate the cutover itself. Simple to start, but every concern (routing, merges, failover, reshard) is hand-built and duplicated across services.
- **Vitess:** a proxy tier — **`vtgate`** (stateless query router the app connects to as if it were one MySQL) parses each query, consults the **topology/VSchema** (which defines keyspaces, shards, and **vindexes** — the shard-key → shard mappings, 11.6/11.7), and routes: single-shard queries → the owning shard; non-shard-key queries → scatter-gather with automatic merge/aggregate/sort/limit; it manages **`vttablet`** (a sidecar per MySQL instance handling connection pooling, query rewriting, and the reshard data-movement). Vitess also owns **resharding** (`Reshard`/`MoveTables` workflows that split/merge keyspace ranges by copying rows + catching up via replication + atomic cutover, 11.14), **sequences** (global IDs, 11.12), and **reference tables** (replicated everywhere, 11.9). The app sees ~one MySQL; Vitess does the distribution.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The routing layer exists because **a sharded system is many databases, but applications want to talk to *one*** — someone must bridge that gap (map queries to shards, merge cross-shard results, manage the topology, and handle resharding) and doing it by hand in every application is repetitive, error-prone, and especially dangerous for the *resharding* and *cross-shard* logic. App-level routing solves it with maximal control for simple cases; **Vitess solves it as managed infrastructure** — abstracting the sharding so applications issue near-normal SQL while Vitess handles routing, scatter-gather, failover, IDs, and (the hardest part) safe live resharding. It's the difference between sharding being a constant application burden and being a mostly-transparent infrastructure capability. For a system that will *grow* (reshard), the routing layer's resharding automation is often the deciding reason to adopt Vitess.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** **App-level routing:** no extra infrastructure, full control, lowest latency (no proxy hop), but the app owns all sharding complexity (routing, merges, cross-shard transactions, *resharding* — the killer), duplicated across services, and easy to get subtly wrong (a mis-routed write is a data-correctness bug). **Vitess:** transparent sharding, automated scatter-gather + resharding + failover + global IDs, battle-tested at massive scale (YouTube, Slack, GitHub) — but it's a **significant operational system** to run (the vtgate/vttablet/topology tiers, learning curve, an extra network hop, some SQL feature limits). A lighter middle ground: **ProxySQL** can do read/write splitting and some routing but isn't a full sharding solution (no automated resharding/scatter-gather merge like Vitess). The decision: **app-level for a small, stable shard count you'll rarely change; Vitess when you need transparent sharding at scale and (especially) painless resharding** — most large MySQL shops at scale converge on Vitess precisely for the resharding and operational maturity.

**Generics / first-principles.** "A sharded datastore needs a *routing/coordination layer* that maps logical queries to physical partitions, merges cross-partition results, and manages topology/rebalancing — either embedded in the client or provided as a proxy/coordinator." This is universal: Vitess's vtgate, MongoDB's `mongos` router + config servers, Cassandra's coordinator node + token-aware drivers, Citus's coordinator, Elasticsearch's coordinating node, Kafka's partition-aware producers/consumers. The transferable insight: **every sharded system separates the *logical* interface (one dataset) from the *physical* layout (many partitions) via a routing layer that owns the shard map, query routing, cross-partition merging, and rebalancing — you either build that into every client (control, but duplicated and error-prone) or run it as shared infrastructure (a proxy/coordinator — transparent, but operationally heavy).** Where that layer also automates *rebalancing/resharding* is what separates a system that can grow from one that can't.

**MySQL-specific reality.** **Vitess** is the dominant MySQL sharding/routing solution: **`vtgate`** (the MySQL-protocol router apps connect to), **`vttablet`** (per-MySQL sidecar), **VSchema/vindexes** (the shard-key mappings), and the **`Reshard`/`MoveTables`** workflows for live resharding (11.14) — it presents a single logical MySQL while transparently sharding, scatter-gathering, generating IDs (sequences), and resharding. Alternatives: **app-level routing** (DIY — fine for small/stable shard counts), and **ProxySQL** (excellent for read/write split + connection pooling + some routing, but not a full sharding/resharding solution). For our domain: a payments platform that shards the ledger typically runs **Vitess** — apps issue near-normal SQL to vtgate, which routes single-shard transfers (co-located, 11.9) to the owning shard as ACID transactions, scatter-gathers the rare cross-cutting query (or pushes it to a reporting replica, 11.10), generates global IDs (sequences, 11.12), keeps currencies/account-types as reference tables (replicated to all shards, 11.9), and — the decisive capability — **reshards live** (8→16 shards) without losing a transfer (11.14). App-level routing is the alternative for a small, fixed shard count. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws app → vtgate (VSchema/vindex) → shards, with the single-shard vs scatter paths.

## 11.14 · Resharding: re-splitting live data ★

**Mental model.** **Resharding** — changing the number of shards (or the shard key) on a *live, in-production* system — is **one of the hardest operations in all of data engineering**, because you must redistribute data across servers *while it keeps changing and serving traffic*, without losing or duplicating a single row, and switch routing atomically so no query goes to the wrong place mid-move. The naive approach (stop, dump, re-split, reload, restart) means downtime no money system can take. The real approach is an **online migration**: **copy** the data to its new homes, **keep it in sync** as it changes (via replication/CDC catch-up, M10/10.14), **verify** the copy matches, then **cut over** routing atomically (and roll back if anything's wrong). Consistent hashing (11.8) or Vitess's keyspace-range splits minimize *how much* data moves; tooling (Vitess `Reshard`) makes the copy-sync-verify-cutover survivable. Resharding is why the shard-key/scheme choices (11.6–11.8) matter so much — they determine whether this brutal operation is *possible*.

**How it actually works.** The online resharding pattern (Vitess `Reshard` automates exactly this):
1. **Provision** the new shards (e.g., split shard covering keyspace range [0,1) into [0,0.5) and [0.5,1)).
2. **Backfill (copy):** bulk-copy the rows that belong on each new shard from the source shard(s) — a consistent snapshot (M10/M13).
3. **Catch-up (sync):** stream the source shard's **binlog/changes** (M10/10.14 — CDC/replication) to the new shards so they apply all writes that happened *during and after* the snapshot → the new shards converge to live state and stay caught up.
4. **Verify:** compare source and target (row counts, checksums) to prove the copy is complete and correct (no lost/duplicated rows) — for money, this is reconciliation-grade verification (M02/2.17).
5. **Cut over (atomic routing switch):** briefly stop writes to the affected range (or use a serialized cutover), ensure the new shards are fully caught up, then **atomically switch the routing** (the VSchema/shard map) so queries for that range now go to the new shards; resume writes. Done carefully, the write-pause is sub-second.
6. **Roll back / clean up:** keep the old shards as a fallback until verified healthy; then decommission.
Consistent hashing (11.8) / keyspace-range splits ensure only the *moving* range's rows are copied (not the whole dataset) — making steps 2–3 bounded.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Resharding exists because **capacity needs change** — you provision N shards, grow past them, and must add more (or rebalance a hotspot) *without downtime or data loss* on a live system. It solves the otherwise-fatal problem that a sharded system would be *frozen at its initial shard count* (or its initial key) if you couldn't re-split safely. It's the operation that makes sharding *elastic* rather than a one-time irreversible partitioning. Because it's so hard and so risky (a botched reshard can lose or duplicate money — M15), it's also the operation whose *difficulty* justifies all the upstream choices: consistent hashing (11.8 — bound the data moved), a good shard key (11.6 — avoid having to change the *key*, which is even worse than changing the *count*), and adopting a routing layer that automates it (Vitess, 11.13). Resharding is where "we'll figure out scaling later" either works or becomes a catastrophe.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Resharding trades *operational complexity and risk* for *elasticity*. The online copy-sync-verify-cutover approach minimizes downtime (sub-second cutover) but is intricate (many failure points; the verify + rollback steps are essential for money). **Changing the shard count** (more shards, same key) is hard but tractable (consistent hashing / range-split bounds the data moved). **Changing the shard *key*** is far worse — essentially a full re-distribution of *every* row by a new key (no consistent-hashing shortcut applies across a key change) — which is why the shard-key choice (11.6) is treated as near-permanent. Alternatives/mitigations: **over-provision shards up front** (start with more, smaller shards than needed — "virtual shards" / many keyspace ranges — so you grow by *moving whole shards to new hardware* rather than splitting, M10-style, avoiding row-level re-splitting); **consistent hashing + vnodes** (11.8 — gentle, bounded rebalancing); and **using Vitess** (11.13) so `Reshard`/`MoveTables` handle the copy-sync-verify-cutover for you. The discipline: **make resharding rare and bounded by good upfront choices, automate it with tooling, and verify (reconcile) it like a financial operation** — never hand-roll a live money reshard without checksums and rollback.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Rebalancing a live partitioned system = copy data to its new home, keep it in sync via the change log, verify equivalence, then atomically switch routing — moving the minimum data (consistent hashing / range splits) and verifying no loss." This is the universal **online data migration / rebalancing** pattern, identical across systems: Vitess `Reshard`, Cassandra's `nodetool` streaming on token changes, MongoDB's chunk migration/balancer, Kafka partition reassignment, and every "online schema/data migration" (gh-ost/pt-osc, M13 — same copy-sync-cutover shape). The transferable insight: **you move live data by building a synchronized copy via the change stream, proving it's equivalent, and flipping routing atomically — the change log (binlog/CDC, M10/10.14) is what makes "copy while it's changing" possible, and verification is what makes it safe.** This copy-via-changelog-then-cutover pattern is *the* way all live data migrations work (resharding, schema migrations M13, even datacenter moves) — and for money, the verify/reconcile step (M02/2.17) is non-negotiable.

**MySQL-specific reality.** App-level resharding is **dangerous to hand-roll** (you must orchestrate snapshot + binlog catch-up + verification + atomic cutover + rollback yourself — many ways to lose/duplicate rows). **Vitess** is the production answer: **`Reshard`** (split/merge shards within a keyspace) and **`MoveTables`** (move tables between keyspaces) implement exactly the copy → **VReplication** (Vitess's binlog-based catch-up, built on MySQL replication, M10) → verify (`VDiff` — row-level diff/checksums) → atomic `SwitchTraffic` cutover, with rollback — turning a near-impossible operation into a tooled workflow. **VDiff** (checksum the source vs target) is the money-grade verification (reconciliation, M02/2.17). For our domain: growing the ledger from **8 → 16 shards** uses Vitess `Reshard` — backfill the split ranges, **VReplication** catches up via each shard's binlog (M10/10.14) so live transfers aren't lost, **VDiff** proves no transfer was lost or duplicated (reconciliation-grade), then `SwitchTraffic` atomically re-routes — a sub-second write pause, no lost money. Alternatively, **over-provision** (start with many small keyspace ranges and move whole shards to new hardware via replication, M10 — avoiding row-level splits). Catastrophic reshard failures (partial cutover, lost/duplicated rows, split-brain on the moved range) are the M15 deep-dive. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the copy → sync (CDC) → verify → cutover flow.

## 11.15 · Choosing partition vs shard vs replica (the decision)

**Mental model.** Three tools, three different problems — and the skill is matching tool to bottleneck rather than reaching for the most complex one. **Replicas (M10):** for **read scaling + HA** (copies of the same data) — reach for these *first* after vertical scaling; they don't help writes. **Partitioning (11.2):** for **single-server manageability** (retention-by-drop, range pruning) — *not* scaling; a narrow tool for big time-series/append tables on one box. **Sharding (11.5):** for **write/storage scaling past one machine** (data divided across servers) — the most powerful and most costly, a **one-way door** of distributed complexity, reached for *last*. The decision flow: diagnose the *actual* bottleneck (read-bound? write-bound? storage-bound? just a big single table?), apply the cheapest tool that addresses it, and **shard only when genuinely write-/storage-bound past the biggest single box you can run with read-replicas** — then derive the shard key from the access pattern (11.6).

**How it actually works.** The diagnostic decision flow:
1. **Vertical scaling first** — is the box maxed? Bigger instance, more RAM (buffer pool), faster NVMe (M09). Cheapest, no distribution. Ride it far.
2. **Read-bound?** (reads saturate the box, writes are fine) → **read-replicas (M10)** + read/write routing (route reporting/reconciliation to replicas, M02/2.17). Add caching. *Don't shard* — replicas solve this.
3. **A single huge table with retention/range-query needs, but the box isn't write-bound?** → **partitioning (11.2)** (range-by-time for drop-retention + pruning) — single-server manageability, *not* a scaling step.
4. **Write-bound or storage-past-one-box** (the box is maxed on write throughput or can't hold the dataset, and vertical scaling + replicas are exhausted)? → **shard (11.5)**. Derive the **shard key** from the access pattern (11.6 — co-locate transfers, 11.9; even load; route the dominant query), pick a **scheme** (11.7 — usually hash/consistent-hashing, 11.8, or Vitess keyspace), and adopt a **routing layer** (11.13 — Vitess for growth/resharding). Each shard is *itself* replicated (M10) for HA/reads.
5. **Combine:** the real production topology is **sharded (write scale) × replicated-per-shard (read scale + HA) [× partitioned-within-shard for history retention]** — the tools compose.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The decision framework exists because the tools are constantly *confused* (people partition expecting write-scale, add replicas expecting write-scale, or shard prematurely and eat distributed complexity they didn't need) — and the cost of choosing wrong is high (wasted effort, or worse, an unnecessary one-way door into sharding). It solves "what do I actually do when one box isn't enough?" by forcing a *diagnosis* (which resource is the bottleneck) before a *prescription*, and by ordering the tools by cost (vertical → replicas/partitioning → sharding) so you apply the cheapest sufficient one. Crucially, it encodes **"shard last, shard reluctantly"** — because sharding's complexity (cross-shard transactions 11.11, scatter-gather 11.10, resharding 11.14) is permanent, you defer it until genuinely necessary and exhaust the alternatives.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The framework *is* the tradeoff map: **vertical** (simple, ceiling), **replicas** (read-scale + HA, not writes, +staleness), **partitioning** (manageability, not scale, +limits 11.4), **sharding** (write/storage-scale, +full distributed complexity). The meta-tradeoff is **simplicity vs scale**: every step right adds capacity *and* complexity, so you stop at the leftmost tool that solves your bottleneck. Anti-patterns the framework prevents: *sharding when read-bound* (replicas were the answer), *partitioning for write-scale* (it's one server), *adding replicas for write-scale* (they duplicate writes), *sharding prematurely* (eating complexity before you need it), and *picking a shard key without studying the access pattern* (11.6 — the expensive mistake). The right answer is usually "vertical-scale and read-replicate as long as you possibly can, then shard reluctantly with a carefully-chosen key when truly write-bound."

**Generics / first-principles.** "Diagnose the bottleneck (read / write / storage / manageability) and apply the cheapest tool that addresses it — replicate for reads/HA, partition for single-node manageability, shard for write/storage scale — deferring distribution complexity until forced." This is universal capacity-planning discipline: the same read-replicate-vs-shard, scale-up-before-out, partition-vs-shard reasoning applies to every datastore (Postgres, Cassandra, Mongo, distributed caches). The transferable insight: **scaling is a *diagnosis-then-prescription* discipline, not a reflex — identify the constrained resource, apply the least-complex tool that relieves it, and treat each step toward distribution as a cost paid only when necessary; the tools compose (shard × replicate × partition) but each is for a distinct bottleneck.** "Don't shard until you must" is the single most important scaling judgment — the complexity is permanent, so the bar is high.

**MySQL-specific reality.** The MySQL playbook in order: **(1)** tune + vertically scale a single InnoDB instance (huge buffer pool, fast storage, M09); **(2)** add **read-replicas** (M10) + read/write split (route reporting/reconciliation to replicas, M02/2.17) + caching for read-bound load; **(3)** **partition** big append-only/time-series tables (the ledger history, 11.2) for retention + pruning on one box; **(4)** **shard** (app-level or **Vitess**, 11.13) only when genuinely write-/storage-bound past the biggest replicated single primary — deriving the shard key from the access pattern (11.6/11.9), using hash/consistent-hashing (11.7/11.8), and adopting Vitess for transparent routing + resharding. The composed production topology: **Vitess-sharded ledger (write scale) × each shard a primary+replicas (M10, read scale + HA) × history partitioned within each shard (retention)**. For our domain: a payments platform rides vertical + read-replicas a *long* way (most fintech write volumes fit one tuned box); it partitions the ledger history for retention; and it shards by account/tenant (11.6/11.9) *only* at extreme scale — keeping transfers single-shard (11.9) so money stays ACID. This decision is the architecture M16 makes concrete. The Pass C Mermaid is the diagnosis → tool decision tree.

## 11.16 · Fintech capstone: the sharded ledger ★

**Mental model.** The full **scaled, money-safe topology**: shard the ledger by a key that **co-locates each transfer's two legs** (11.9 — by `tenant_id`/`ledger_group`, or via per-shard clearing accounts) so the *dominant* money operation — a transfer — stays a **single-shard ACID transaction** (M07–M09 guarantees intact: atomic debit+credit, durable, isolated); make **each shard itself a replicated set** (M10 — semi-sync for durability, replicas for reporting/reconciliation + HA failover); use **distributed IDs** (11.12 — ULID/Snowflake, time-ordered, index-friendly); handle the **rare cross-shard transfer** via a **Saga over local transactions** (11.11/M12 — with idempotency + reconciliation, never fragile 2PC); route via **Vitess** (11.13) which also **reshards live** (11.14 — VReplication + VDiff verification + atomic cutover, no lost transfer); and serve cross-cutting **reporting/analytics from replica-fed read models** (11.10, M02/2.17), not from scatter-gathering the money path. The result: **write-scale past one machine without ever losing or duplicating money** — sharding made safe for a ledger.

**How it actually works.** Composing the whole module + M09/M10:
- **Shard key for co-location (11.6/11.9):** shard by `tenant_id`/`ledger_group` (hashed for even load, 11.7/11.8) so both accounts of an intra-tenant transfer share a shard → **debit + credit + balance update = one single-shard ACID transaction** (M07/7.16, M08 concurrency-correct, M09 durable). The common path keeps *all* single-node guarantees.
- **Per-shard replication (M10):** each shard = primary + replicas with **semi-sync** (a committed transfer survives total loss of the shard's primary node — M10/10.4, extending M09/9.16) + replicas for reporting/reconciliation (M02/2.17) + a standby for **automated fenced failover** (M10/10.10–10.13). Sharding × replication composed.
- **Distributed IDs (11.12):** ledger entry/transaction IDs are **ULID/UUIDv7 (BINARY(16)) or Snowflake** — globally unique across shards, time-ordered (insert-friendly on the append-heavy ledger, M05/M09), and components of **idempotency keys** (M16).
- **Cross-shard transfers (11.11):** the unavoidable cross-tenant/cross-shard minority use a **Saga** — local debit (clearing account, shard A) → local credit (clearing account, shard B) → settle — each DB transaction single-shard, with **idempotency keys** (no double-application) and **reconciliation** (M02/2.17) catching any drift. Eventually consistent, never lost.
- **Routing + resharding (11.13/11.14):** **Vitess** routes single-shard transfers to their shard, scatter-gathers (or rejects) cross-cutting queries, generates IDs (sequences), keeps currencies/account-types as **reference tables** (replicated to all shards), and **reshards live** (8→16) via VReplication + **VDiff** verification + atomic cutover — growing capacity without losing a transfer.
- **Reporting/analytics (11.10):** global reports, "all transactions over $X," reconciliation runs → **replica-fed read models / warehouse** (M02/2.17, M13), *not* scatter-gather on the live shards.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** This capstone solves the final scaling problem for money: **how to scale writes/storage past one machine while keeping the money-never-lies and durability invariants that single-node InnoDB gave for free** (M07–M09). It's the synthesis of the whole track — single-node durability (M09) × replication for node-loss survival + HA + read offload (M10) × sharding for write scale (M11) — arranged so that the *common* path (a transfer) stays single-shard ACID (preserving every guarantee) and only the *rare* cross-shard path accepts eventual consistency (managed safely with Sagas + idempotency + reconciliation). It exists to prove that **scale and correctness aren't mutually exclusive for a ledger** — with the right shard key (co-location), the right per-shard durability (semi-sync replication), the right IDs, and the right handling of the cross-shard minority, a payments platform can grow horizontally without ever losing or duplicating money. It sets up M12 (the distributed-transaction/CDC/outbox patterns the cross-shard path needs) and M16 (the full platform).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** This topology accepts: **sharding complexity** (routing, resharding, the cross-shard minority) in exchange for **write/storage scale**; **eventual consistency on cross-shard transfers** (Saga, not atomic) in exchange for **never doing fragile cross-shard 2PC**; **operational weight** (Vitess + N shards × replicas) in exchange for **transparent, reshardable scale**. The alternative — *not* sharding (ride a big replicated single primary) — is **strongly preferred until genuinely necessary** (11.15): it keeps *everything* single-node ACID, no cross-shard anything, far simpler ops. So the honest capstone tradeoff is: **shard only when write/storage scale truly demands it, and when you do, design hard for co-location so the money path stays single-shard** — paying the distributed cost only on the rare cross-shard transfer, and proving correctness there with idempotency + reconciliation rather than distributed atomicity. The art is *minimizing* the distributed surface, not mastering distributed transactions.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Scale a transactional system by partitioning along its transaction boundaries (so the common transaction stays single-partition/ACID), replicating each partition (for durability/HA/reads), generating IDs without coordination, and handling the rare cross-partition operation with a compensation-based workflow + verification — never by trying to make cross-partition transactions cheap." This is the universal recipe for **scaling a correctness-critical partitioned system** (the same shape for any sharded transactional store, any microservices-with-databases architecture). The transferable insight: **horizontal scale for transactional data comes from co-locating each transaction on one node (keep ACID where it's free), composing with per-node replication (durability/HA), and treating cross-node operations as a separate, minimized, eventually-consistent-but-verified mode — the design goal is to make the distributed surface as small as possible, not to make distributed transactions fast.** For money specifically: **keep the invariant (debit=credit) on one node, replicate that node, and reconcile the rare cross-node flows** — scale without sacrificing the invariant.

**MySQL-specific reality.** The concrete stack: **Vitess** shards the ledger by `tenant_id`/`ledger_group` (hashed keyspace, 11.7/11.8) so transfers co-locate (11.9) → single-shard **InnoDB ACID** transactions (M07–M09); **each shard is a MySQL primary + replicas** with **semi-sync** + automated fenced failover (M10) for durability/HA + reporting offload (M02/2.17); IDs are **ULID/Snowflake** (11.12, BINARY(16)/BIGINT); cross-shard transfers are **Sagas** over local transactions with idempotency keys + reconciliation (M12/M16); Vitess provides **sequences** (IDs), **reference tables** (currencies/account-types replicated to all shards), **scatter-gather** (rare cross-cutting queries, pushed to reporting replicas where possible, 11.10), and **`Reshard` + VDiff** for live, verified resharding (11.14); the ledger **history is partitioned by month within each shard** (11.2) for retention. For our domain, the money-safety guarantees that survive: a committed transfer is **atomic** (single-shard ACID, M07–M09), **durable beyond node loss** (semi-sync replication, M10), **never double-applied** (idempotency keys), **never lost across shards** (Saga + reconciliation), and **the platform scales writes** (sharding) **without forking or losing the ledger**. This is the distributed realization of money-never-lies — single-node ACID (M07–M09) → node-loss-durable (M10) → write-scaled (M11) — and it sets up M12 (the cross-shard protocols), M15 (the catastrophic sharding/cross-shard failures), and M16 (the full fintech platform).
