> **Module mental model:** *Every prior module built a guarantee; this module is what happens when each guarantee* fails *— the rare, catastrophic, often-silent failures that destroy data and lose money, and exactly how to* detect, contain, recover, and prevent *them. It's the dark mirror of the whole journey: M09 made commits durable → here a crash in the* flush/sync window *loses them; M10 replicated for HA → here a* split-brain *forks the ledger; M09's checksums guard pages → here* bit rot and lying disks *corrupt them silently; M13 made backups → here* the backup won't restore *; M12 made distribution correct → here a* dual-write or half-Saga *loses money the database faithfully persisted around. The defining theme is that the worst failures are* silent *(they don't error — the data is just wrong) and* rare *(they hit at the worst moment, when you're least prepared), so the discipline is: know the failure modes* before *they happen, watch the early-warning signals (M13/13.11), have* tested *recovery (M13/13.5), and* reconcile *(M12/12.14) to detect what slipped through. Each scenario ends with the money verdict — because for fintech, "did money get lost or duplicated, and what catches it?" is the only question that matters. This is the chapter that turns "the system is correct" into "and here's what I do when it isn't."*
>
> **Threads carried in this module:**
> - **Durability** — this module is durability's *failure* catalog: the exact crash windows that lose committed data (the flush/sync combinations, M09/14.9), the corruption that destroys it (bit rot, torn pages, lying disks, M09), the PITR gaps that prevent recovery (M13), and `innodb_force_recovery` (the last-resort durability salvage).
> - **Money-never-lies** — *the entire module is this thread*: every scenario asks "did money get lost or duplicated?" and gives the verdict + the fix. Lost transactions, split-brain forked ledgers, silent corruption, half-Sagas, dual-write loss, lost updates — the catastrophic money-loss modes and their detection (reconciliation, M12/12.14) and recovery.
> - **Generics-first** — the failure modes (crash-loss windows, split-brain, silent corruption/bit-rot, the dual-write/lost-update class, untested backups, the incident-response discipline) are universal to all stateful systems; MySQL is one instance (force_recovery, GTID reconciliation, page checksums, binlog gaps).
> - **Tradeoff** — every prevention is a tradeoff (durability config vs throughput, M09; fencing vs availability, M10; synchronous replication vs latency; backup frequency vs cost) — this module shows the *cost of having chosen wrong* (the catastrophe), making the prior tradeoffs concrete.
>
> **Prereqs:** *all of M01–M14* — this module is the *catastrophic-failure* counterpart to every guarantee built earlier. Especially: M09 (durability — the crash-loss windows, torn pages, checksums, crash recovery that *here* fail/are-salvaged), M10 (replication — split-brain, the promote-before-apply window, errant transactions that *here* are the catastrophes), M11 (sharding — the botched-reshard, cross-shard partial-write losses), M12 (distributed — the dual-write/half-Saga losses + reconciliation as the detector), M13 (operations — the backup-won't-restore, PITR gaps, the operational outage modes, the early-warning signals that would've caught it), M14 (the lost-data triage tree, 14.8, is the *entry point* — M15 is its detailed handling). **Leads into:** M16 (the fintech capstone's *DR / "what zero-data-loss really costs" / failure-and-recovery* challenge draws directly on this module — designing a platform that *survives* these catastrophes).
