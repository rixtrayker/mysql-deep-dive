## 15.1 · How to think about failure (the discipline) ★

**Mental model.** The worst database failures share two traits that make them uniquely dangerous: they're **silent** (the data is *wrong*, but nothing *errors* — no exception, no alert, just incorrect money) and **rare** (they strike at the worst moment — a power loss, a partition, a bad deploy — when you're least prepared). A *correct* system (M01–M14) handles the *expected* cases; this module is the *unexpected catastrophes*. The discipline against silent+rare failures is fourfold: **(1) Know them in advance** (you can't react to a failure mode you've never heard of — this chapter is the catalog); **(2) Watch the early-warning signals** (M13/13.11 — most catastrophes build up: HLL climbs, disk fills, lag grows, semi-sync degrades); **(3) Have *tested* recovery** (M13/13.5 — backups/PITR/failover *proven* to work, because the disaster is the wrong time to discover they don't); **(4) Reconcile** (M12/12.14 — independently verify, to *detect* the silent failures that slipped through). This module turns "the system is correct" into "and here's exactly what I do when it isn't."

**How it happens (root cause).** Silent failures arise because databases optimize for the *common* case and trade away the *rare* case (durability config trades crash-safety for throughput, M09; async replication trades consistency for latency, M10) — so when the rare event hits, the trade-off *manifests* as loss. They're silent because the database faithfully does what it was told (it persisted what it had; it served what it had) — the *wrongness* is at a layer it can't see (a lost-but-acknowledged commit, a forked replica, a corrupted page it hasn't read yet, an app-level race). Rarity means they accumulate latent (an untested backup rots for a year, an errant transaction sits until the next failover) and strike when triggered.

**Why it's catastrophic.** Because *silent + rare + money* = the worst combination: silent means you don't know it happened (money is wrong, undetected); rare means you're unprepared (no practiced response); money means the cost is real and often irreversible (a lost or duplicated transfer). A loud, common failure is *handled* (errors, retries, alerts); a silent, rare one *festers* until reconciliation catches it (if you reconcile) or a customer complains (if you don't). The catastrophe is the *combination*.

**The fix / prevention.** The discipline above: *know* (this catalog), *watch* (early-warning, M13/13.11), *test* (recovery, M13/13.5), *reconcile* (M12/12.14). Plus the per-scenario fixes (the rest of this module). The meta-prevention: **assume failures will happen, design to detect/contain/recover them, and verify continuously** — don't *hope* they won't.

**Generics / MySQL reality.** Universally, the most dangerous failures are silent (data corruption, lost writes, split-brain) and rare (correlated with disasters) — every stateful system needs a failure-modes discipline (chaos engineering, game days, runbooks, reconciliation). In MySQL: the silent failures are lost commits (M09 config), forked replicas (M10), corrupt pages (M09), errant GTIDs (M10), half-Sagas/dual-writes (M12), and the loud-but-rare ones are crashes, OOM, disk-full, applier stops. The defenses are MySQL's: durability config (M09), fencing/GTID (M10), checksums/doublewrite (M09), reconciliation (M12), tested restores (M13).

**💰 Money verdict.** The *framing* verdict: for money, "did it get lost/duplicated?" must be *answerable* — which requires reconciliation (M12/12.14) as the detector and tested recovery as the remedy. A system that *can't answer* the question (no reconciliation, untested backups) has *already* failed the money-never-lies test, before any catastrophe.

## 15.2 · Lost transactions on crash (the durability window) ★

**Mental model.** A crash (power loss, kernel panic, hardware failure) at the wrong moment can lose the *last committed transactions* — and *exactly how many* depends on the durability config (M09/14.9). With `innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit=1` + `sync_binlog=1` (the money settings), a confirmed commit is *fsync'd* to disk before the client is told "committed" → **nothing is lost** (crash recovery, M09/9.14, replays the redo). With weaker settings (`=2`/`=0`), commits are acknowledged *before* the fsync → a crash loses the **window** between acknowledgment and the periodic fsync (~1 second of commits). And a *replica* promoted on failover *before applying its relay log* loses the un-applied transactions (the promote-before-apply window, M10/10.10). The catastrophe: **confirmed transfers vanish** — the customer was told "paid," but after the crash the transfer is gone.

**How it happens (root cause).** Durability is a chain (M09/9.7): app → OS page cache → disk cache → platter. A commit is only *truly* durable when it's fsync'd through to stable storage. `flush_log_at_trx_commit=1`/`sync_binlog=1` fsync *every* commit (durable before ack); `=2` writes to OS cache and fsyncs ~every second (an *OS/power* crash loses the un-fsynced ~1s — a mysqld crash is fine, the OS still has it); `=0` fsyncs ~every second regardless (even a mysqld crash loses ~1s). So a crash loses *whatever was acknowledged but not yet fsync'd* — the size of the window is the config. The replica version: async replication (M10/10.4) means a replica lags; if the primary crashes and a lagging replica is promoted, the transactions the primary committed-and-acknowledged but hadn't replicated are *lost* (they only existed on the dead primary).

**Why it's catastrophic.** A *confirmed* transfer vanishing is the purest money-never-lies violation: the system *told the customer it happened* (acknowledged the commit), then *lost it* (the crash). The money is in limbo — debited-then-un-debited, or paid-then-unpaid — and worse, it's *silent* (the database, after recovery, simply doesn't have the transaction; it doesn't *know* it lost it). Without reconciliation (M12/12.14 — comparing against external records), you might never know. For money, *any* loss window is unacceptable.

**The fix (recovery).** *After* a crash with loss: **PITR** can't help (the lost transactions weren't in the durable binlog either, if `sync_binlog≠1`); the recovery is **reconciliation** (M12/12.14 — compare against the external processor's records to find what's missing) + **re-driving** the lost transactions (re-process from the source-of-truth queue/external system, idempotently, M12/12.9 — so re-processing doesn't double-apply). If `sync_binlog=1` but `flush_log_at_trx_commit≠1`, the binlog may have it for PITR. For the replica-promotion case: choose the *freshest* replica (largest GTID set, M10/10.10) to minimize loss.

**Prevention.** **`flush_log_at_trx_commit=1` + `sync_binlog=1`** (the money settings, M09/14.9 — no committed transaction lost on crash) + **semi-sync replication** (M10/10.4 — a committed transfer is durable on a replica *immediately*, so even total primary loss loses nothing) + **automated fenced failover choosing the freshest replica** (M10/10.10). With 1/1 + semi-sync, the loss window is closed. The throughput cost is mitigated by group commit (M09/9.11).

**Generics / MySQL reality.** Universally, durability is "fsync before ack," and any config that acks-before-fsync has a crash-loss window (the same in Postgres `synchronous_commit`, every WAL database). In MySQL: the two settings (M09) + semi-sync (M10) define the window; group commit amortizes the cost; crash recovery (M09/9.14) replays the durable redo. The replica-promotion loss is the CAP/failover tradeoff (M10/10.10) made concrete.

**💰 Money verdict.** **Money LOST** (confirmed transfers vanish) — *unless* prevented by 1/1 + semi-sync (then **nothing lost**). Detection: reconciliation (M12/12.14 — against external records). The lesson: **for money, the durability window must be closed (1/1 + semi-sync)** — a weaker config silently loses confirmed money on a crash. ## 15.3 · Source/replica divergence & split-brain ★

**Mental model.** The most catastrophic *replication* failure: **two servers both accept writes** — because a network partition split the cluster (and both sides kept serving, M10/10.11) or a botched failover promoted a replica *without fencing the old source* (so both are now "primary"). They diverge **irreconcilably**: each accepts *different, conflicting* transfers, so the ledger *forks* into two incompatible versions, neither of which is "the truth." Unlike a lost transaction (recoverable), a forked ledger is *unrecoverable by merging* (you can't automatically decide which conflicting transfers are real — both have real money). The defenses are **prevention** (fencing/STONITH or quorum — ensure *exactly one* writer) — and the hard truth: **fencing beats reconciliation** (preventing the fork is the *only* good option; reconciling a fork is a manual, lossy nightmare).

**How it happens (root cause).** Two mechanisms: **(1) Partition + AP behavior** — a network partition splits the cluster; if both sides keep accepting writes (an AP choice, or a misconfiguration, M10/10.11), they diverge. **(2) Botched failover** — the primary appears dead (it's just slow/partitioned), a replica is promoted, but the old primary *isn't fenced* (stopped/isolated) — so when it "comes back" (or never left), *both* accept writes. Now transfers land on *different* nodes; the same account has different balances on each; the ledgers conflict. GTIDs diverge (each node has transactions the other lacks). The root cause is **the assumption of a single writer being violated** without enforcement.

**Why it's catastrophic.** A forked ledger is *worse than data loss* — with loss, you know what's missing and can re-drive it; with a fork, you have *two conflicting truths*, both containing real money movements, and *no automatic way to merge them* (which of two conflicting transfers on the same account is "real"? both debited real money). Resolving it requires *manual* investigation of every conflicting transaction, with inevitable lossy decisions. It's the catastrophe that *can't be cleanly recovered* — which is why prevention is everything.

**The fix (recovery).** *After* a fork (prevention failed): **stop both nodes** (no more divergence), **pick one as authoritative** (usually the one with more/critical writes, or by business rules), **extract the conflicting transactions from the other** (the ones it has that the authoritative lacks — via GTID set difference, M10/10.7), and **manually reconcile** each (re-apply legitimate ones to the authoritative node, with reconciliation against external records, M12/12.14, to decide). This is slow, manual, and lossy — *the* worst recovery in the module. "Fencing beats reconciliation" because *this* is what reconciliation-after-a-fork means.

**Prevention.** **Ensure exactly one writer, enforced**: **fencing/STONITH** (on failover, *forcibly* isolate/kill the old primary — power off, network ACL — *before* promoting, so it *can't* keep writing, M10/10.11) OR **quorum/consensus** (group replication — a partitioned minority *refuses* writes, structurally preventing a second writer, M10/10.9/10.11) + **`super_read_only`** on all replicas (M10/10.7 — they *can't* accept writes even by accident, 15.4). **Never fail over without fencing.**

**Generics / MySQL reality.** Split-brain is *the* canonical distributed-systems catastrophe (any multi-writer-without-coordination system), which is *why* consensus algorithms (Raft/Paxos) and fencing exist — they enforce single-leadership. In MySQL: fencing (orchestrator-driven STONITH) or group replication's quorum (M10/10.11); `super_read_only` prevents accidental writes; GTID set-difference identifies the divergence. The deep lesson (M10/10.11): **fence before promote, always.**

**💰 Money verdict.** **Money DUPLICATED/FORKED and effectively LOST** (two conflicting ledgers, manual lossy reconciliation) — the *worst* money catastrophe in the module, and **unrecoverable cleanly**. Prevention (fencing/quorum) is the *only* acceptable strategy. Detection: GTID divergence + reconciliation. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws dual-writes → divergence → fence/reconcile.

## 15.4 · Errant transactions & GTID drift

**Mental model.** An **errant transaction** is a write made *directly on a replica* (a debugging session, a misconfigured app, a manual fix) — creating a transaction with a **GTID the source doesn't have** (M10/10.7). It seems harmless until the *next failover*: GTID-based failover/re-pointing relies on every node's transactions being a *subset* of the source's history; an errant GTID *breaks* this (the replica has a transaction no one else does), causing failover to fail or silently diverge. It's a *latent* split-brain seed — a small local write that sabotages HA later. Prevention is `super_read_only` (M10/10.7 — replicas *can't* be written, even by accident); detection is GTID-set comparison.

**How it happens (root cause).** A replica is *supposed* to be read-only (it only applies the source's binlog). But if it's writable (no `super_read_only`), *anything* can write to it — a developer debugging, an app misrouted to a replica, a manual "quick fix." That write gets a *new GTID* (the replica's own server_uuid) that the *source* and *other replicas* don't have. Now the replica's GTID set isn't a subset of the source's — they've *drifted*. GTID auto-positioning (M10/10.7) assumes subset relationships; the errant GTID violates it → on failover (promoting this replica, or re-pointing others to it), the GTID math breaks (a node expects transactions it can't find, or has extras), causing errors or silent divergence.

**Why it's catastrophic.** It's a *latent* failure — harmless *now*, catastrophic *later* (at the next failover, the worst time). It can *break HA entirely* (failover fails when you need it most) or *seed a divergence* (the errant transaction's effects spread inconsistently). And it's *silent* until triggered — the errant transaction sits in the GTID set unnoticed. For money, a failover that breaks (no HA) or diverges (split-brain, 15.3) is a money catastrophe, seeded by a "harmless" replica write.

**The fix (recovery).** **Detect** via GTID-set comparison (the replica has GTIDs the source lacks — `gtid_executed` diff, M10/10.7). **Fix** by either *injecting empty transactions* on the source for the errant GTIDs (so the sets reconcile — the standard repair) or *rebuilding the replica* from a fresh backup (clean GTID set). Both make the GTID sets consistent again so failover works.

**Prevention.** **`super_read_only = ON`** on *every* replica (M10/10.7 — even users with `SUPER` can't write; the replica is *truly* read-only, so no errant transactions can be created) + monitoring for GTID drift (M10/10.12) + never pointing apps/debug sessions at a writable replica. `super_read_only` is the single most important prevention.

**Generics / MySQL reality.** Universally, in any log-shipping replication, a follower that accepts local writes drifts from the leader's history and breaks failover — the general lesson is *followers must be strictly read-only*. In MySQL, this is precisely `super_read_only` + GTID (M10/10.7/10.12); the repair is empty-transaction injection or replica rebuild. The Pass C Mermaid draws the errant-transaction → broken-failover path.

**💰 Money verdict.** **Money at RISK indirectly** — the errant transaction itself may be small, but it *breaks failover* (HA loss) or *seeds divergence* (split-brain, 15.3 — forked ledger). Prevention: `super_read_only`. Detection: GTID drift monitoring (M10/10.12). The lesson: **replicas must be truly read-only** — a "harmless" replica write is a latent HA/divergence catastrophe.

## 15.5 · Silent / phantom corruption (bit rot, lying disks, torn pages) ★

**Mental model.** Data can silently *corrupt* — turn *wrong* with *no error* — from several sources: **bit rot** (a stored bit flips: cosmic rays, failing/aging media, controller bugs), **lying disks** (a disk says "fsync'd" but the data was still in a volatile cache lost on power-fail, M09/9.7 — the "lying disk" problem), and **torn/partial page writes** (a crash *mid-write* of a 16KB InnoDB page leaves it half-old/half-new — corrupt, M09). The terror is *silence*: the corrupted balance or ledger entry isn't flagged — it's just *wrong*, and you read it as if it were correct. MySQL's defenses: **page checksums** (M09 — every page has a checksum; a mismatch on read *detects* corruption) and the **doublewrite buffer** (M09 — pages are written twice, so a torn write is *recoverable* from the intact copy), plus **`CHECK TABLE`** to scan for corruption.

**How it happens (root cause).** **Bit rot:** physical storage degrades — bits flip over time (media aging) or from radiation/controller errors — silently changing stored data. **Lying disks (M09/9.7):** a disk/controller with a *volatile write cache* acknowledges `fsync` *before* the data is on stable media (to look fast); a power loss then loses the "durable" data → the page is missing-or-old (corrupt relative to what was acked). **Torn pages (M09):** InnoDB pages are 16KB but the disk writes in smaller (e.g., 4KB) sectors; a crash *mid-page-write* leaves the page partially updated — half the old version, half the new — *corrupt*. All three produce *wrong data with no error at write time* — detected only later (on read, by checksum) or never (if the defenses are off).

**Why it's catastrophic.** Silent corruption of *money* is a money-never-lies nightmare: a balance silently becomes wrong, a ledger entry silently corrupts — and you *act on it as truth* (authorize against a corrupt balance, report a corrupt total). Worse, *corruption replicates* (if the corrupt write is in the binlog, M10) or *propagates into backups* (you back up the corruption — "your backup is corrupt too"). Without checksums, it's *undetectable* until reconciliation (M12/12.14 — balance ≠ Σ entries) or a customer complaint. It's the *silent* failure par excellence.

**The fix (recovery).** **Detect:** page checksums (M09 — InnoDB flags a checksum mismatch on read, refusing the corrupt page) and **`CHECK TABLE`** (scans for corruption). **Recover a torn page:** the **doublewrite buffer** (M09 — InnoDB wrote the page to the doublewrite area *first*, so crash recovery restores the intact copy — torn pages are *automatically* recovered). **Recover other corruption:** restore from a (verified-not-corrupt) backup + PITR (M13/13.3); for a corrupt-but-readable table, `CHECK TABLE` + repair, or extract via `innodb_force_recovery` (15.6) and rebuild. Reconciliation (M12/12.14) detects *logical* corruption (wrong balances).

**Prevention.** **Page checksums ON** (`innodb_checksum_algorithm`, M09 — detect corruption on read), **doublewrite buffer ON** (M09 — prevent torn-page corruption; only disable on atomic-write-capable storage), **fsync that doesn't lie** (M09/9.7 — disable volatile disk caches or use battery-backed/enterprise storage; the durability config assumes honest fsync), **reconciliation** (M12/12.14 — detect logical corruption), and **tested backups verified not-corrupt** (M13/13.5 — so you don't back up corruption). ECC RAM and checksumming filesystems (ZFS) add further protection.

**Generics / MySQL reality.** Silent corruption (bit rot, torn writes, lying hardware) is universal to all storage — which is why checksums (page/block), write-ahead double-writes (or copy-on-write filesystems), and honest-fsync requirements exist everywhere (Postgres checksums, ZFS, etc.). In MySQL: page checksums + doublewrite buffer + `CHECK TABLE` (M09) are the defenses; the lying-disk/fsync-honesty problem (M09/9.7) is the durability-chain foundation. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the corruption sources + defenses.

**💰 Money verdict.** **Money silently WRONG** (corrupt balances/entries, acted on as truth) — the *silent* catastrophe. Prevention: checksums + doublewrite + honest fsync + reconciliation. Detection: page checksums (physical) + reconciliation (logical, M12/12.14). The lesson: **corruption is silent — defend (checksums/doublewrite) and detect (reconcile), because you won't get an error.**

## 15.6 · innodb_force_recovery: the last resort (levels 1–6) ★

**Mental model.** When InnoDB is so corrupted it *won't even start* (crash recovery itself fails), **`innodb_force_recovery`** (levels 1–6) progressively *disables* recovery features to coax the server up *just enough to dump the data* — but it's a **last resort**, not a fix: each higher level disables more safety and is *more dangerous* (higher levels can *themselves* cause further corruption or data loss). The *only* correct use is: set the *lowest* level that lets the server start, **dump the data** (`mysqldump`), then **rebuild a fresh server** and reload — *never* run production on a forced-recovery server. It's the emergency salvage for "InnoDB won't start and I need to extract the ledger."

**How it happens (root cause).** Severe corruption (bit rot in critical pages, a torn write to a system tablespace, a hardware failure) can corrupt InnoDB's *own* structures (the redo log, the system tablespace, the data dictionary) so badly that **crash recovery fails** — InnoDB detects the corruption and *refuses to start* (to avoid making it worse). Now you're stuck: the server won't come up, and your data (the ledger) is inside it. `innodb_force_recovery` exists for exactly this: levels 1–6 each skip more of InnoDB's startup/recovery steps (level 1: ignore some corruption; ... level 6: don't even apply the redo log) to get the server *up* despite the corruption — accepting that the result may be inconsistent, *just to extract the data*.

**Why it's catastrophic (and dangerous).** You're here because a catastrophe *already happened* (corruption that broke recovery). `force_recovery` is dangerous because each level *removes safety*: higher levels can read inconsistent data, skip integrity checks, and *cause further corruption* if you write (which is why you go *read-only* and *dump-only*). Used wrong (running production on it, or writing to it), it *compounds* the disaster. The catastrophe is the underlying corruption; force_recovery is the *risky salvage*, and the danger is *making it worse*.

**The fix (recovery — the correct procedure).** **(1)** Set `innodb_force_recovery` to the **lowest level (1)** that lets the server start; if it still won't start, increment (2, 3, ...) — but **stop at the lowest that works** (higher = more dangerous). **(2)** Bring the server up **read-only** (don't write — `force_recovery ≥ 4` forces read-only anyway). **(3)** **`mysqldump`** the data (extract the ledger — get the money out). **(4)** **Build a fresh, clean server**, reload the dump, **reconcile** (M12/12.14 — verify the extracted ledger is correct: balance = Σ entries, against external records — because force_recovery may have extracted *inconsistent* data). **(5)** Resume on the clean server. *Never* run production on the forced server.

**Prevention.** Prevent the *corruption* (15.5 — checksums, doublewrite, honest fsync) and the *need* for force_recovery: **tested backups + PITR** (M13/13.3/13.5 — so you can *restore* a clean copy instead of salvaging a corrupt one — restoring a verified backup is *far* safer than force_recovery) + **semi-sync replicas** (M10 — failover to a healthy replica instead of salvaging the corrupt primary). force_recovery is the resort *when you have no good backup/replica* — so the real prevention is *having* those.

**Generics / MySQL reality.** Every database has an emergency "start despite corruption to extract data" mode (with the same "last resort, dump-and-rebuild, don't run production on it" discipline). In MySQL, it's `innodb_force_recovery` levels 1–6 (each disabling more: purge, insert buffer merge, undo rollback, dictionary stats, log scan, redo apply) — a *salvage* tool, and the reconciliation-after-extraction is essential (the data may be inconsistent). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the 6 levels + what each disables + its danger.

**💰 Money verdict.** **Money at RISK of being WRONG** (force_recovery may extract *inconsistent* data) — so the extracted ledger *must be reconciled* (M12/12.14) before trust. Prevention: tested backups/replicas (so you restore-clean instead of salvage). The lesson: **force_recovery is a last-resort salvage — dump, rebuild, reconcile, never run production on it; having a tested backup/replica means you never need it.**
