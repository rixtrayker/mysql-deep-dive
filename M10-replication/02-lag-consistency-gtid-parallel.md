## 10.5 · Replication lag: causes, measurement, consequences ★

**Mental model.** Because async replication (10.4) doesn't make the source wait, a replica is **always behind** the source by some amount — **replication lag** — the time (or transaction count) between when the source committed a change and when the replica has *applied* it. Lag is normally small (milliseconds) but can spike to seconds or minutes under load, a long transaction, or a slow applier. And lag has teeth: **a read off a lagging replica returns stale data** — so reading a balance from a replica that's 5 seconds behind shows the balance *before* a just-committed transfer, and acting on that stale value is a real money bug (a double-spend, a wrong decision). Lag is the concrete manifestation of "the copies aren't instantly identical," and managing it is central to using replicas safely.

**How it actually works.** Lag arises in the binlog→relay→apply pipeline (10.2): the replica *receives* the binlog promptly (the IO thread is fast — just network), but *applying* it (the SQL thread executing the changes) can fall behind. **Causes:**
- **Apply throughput < source write throughput:** a single-threaded applier (historically) can't keep up with a source committing in parallel (the source had many concurrent writers; a serial applier replays them one at a time) → lag grows. **Parallel replication** (10.8) is the main fix.
- **A long/large transaction on the source:** a huge transaction (a bulk update) takes a long time to *apply* on the replica, stalling everything behind it → a lag spike.
- **Replica hardware/load:** a weaker replica, or one serving heavy read queries, applies slower.
- **Network:** slow/distant links delay receipt (esp. cross-region).
**Measurement:** `Seconds_Behind_Source`/`Seconds_Behind_Master` (a rough estimate — the difference between the replica's clock and the timestamp of the event it's applying; *imperfect* — it can read 0 during certain stalls or be misleading), and more robustly via **GTIDs** (10.7 — compare the source's `Executed_Gtid_Set` to the replica's, the exact transaction gap) or a **heartbeat** (write a timestamp row on the source every second, read it on the replica → precise lag). **Consequences:** stale reads (10.6), and a *larger lag = a larger data-loss window on failover* (10.10 — a more-behind replica loses more if promoted).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Lag isn't a feature — it's the *inherent consequence* of asynchronous replication (10.4): the price of not making the source wait (low latency, high throughput) is that replicas trail. Understanding it is essential because *lag determines whether a replica read is safe* (can you tolerate seeing data N seconds old?) and *how much you'd lose on failover* (10.10). It's the central operational reality of replication: replicas are useful (read scaling, HA) *but they lag*, and every use must account for it. Measuring and bounding lag is what makes replicas trustworthy for the uses that can tolerate it (reporting) and flags the uses that can't (read-after-write of your own money, 10.6).

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Lag trades **freshness for the benefits of async** (low write latency, read offload). You can *reduce* lag (parallel replication 10.8, faster replica hardware, smaller transactions, M07/7.15) but not eliminate it under async. To *bound* the consequences: route reads that need freshness to the source (10.6 — gives up the read-scaling for those); use *semi-sync* (10.4) to bound the failover loss window (but not lag itself); or use *consistency mechanisms* (GTID-based "read your writes" waits, 10.6). The fundamental alternative — *synchronous* replication (group replication, 10.9) — eliminates lag-induced staleness for reads from the cluster but at the latency cost (10.4). So you either tolerate/bound lag (async + careful routing) or pay for synchrony. For money, the rule: *never read your own just-written money state from a lagging replica without a consistency guarantee* (10.6).

**Generics / first-principles.** "Asynchronous replicas trail the source by a variable lag; a read from a replica is a read of the *past*, and the staleness can break correctness." This is the universal **replica staleness / eventual consistency** reality: any async-replicated system (read replicas everywhere, CDN edge caches, async-replicated stores) serves *stale* data from followers, and the lag is a variable you must measure and reason about. The transferable insight: **a read from an asynchronously-replicated copy is eventually-consistent — it reflects some past state, and you must decide whether your operation can tolerate that staleness** (most reads can; reads that drive decisions on your own just-written data often can't). Lag is the concrete face of eventual consistency, and "how stale can this read be, and does it matter?" is the question every replica read must answer. This is the bridge to CAP/PACELC (M12).

**MySQL-specific reality.** Measure lag via `SHOW REPLICA STATUS` → `Seconds_Behind_Source` (rough, imperfect — can show 0 during stalls or be misleading; don't trust it alone), **GTID gaps** (`Executed_Gtid_Set` on source vs replica — exact), or a **heartbeat table** (the robust approach: a row updated every second on the source, its lag measured on the replica). Reduce lag with **parallel replication** (`replica_parallel_workers`, `replica_parallel_type=LOGICAL_CLOCK`, 10.8), faster replicas, and short transactions (M07/7.15 — a long source transaction stalls the replica's applier). For our domain: a reporting/reconciliation replica running seconds behind is *fine* (reconciliation tolerates slight staleness, M02/2.17) — but a balance read that drives a *spending decision* must **not** come from a lagging replica (a 5-second-stale balance could allow a double-spend). Monitor lag and alert on spikes (M13). The classic money bug: "user pays, then immediately checks balance, read goes to a lagging replica → shows the *old* (higher) balance → user thinks the payment failed, pays again → double charge" — exactly the read-after-write hazard (10.6). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws lag's causes and measurement.

## 10.6 · Read-after-write consistency & read routing

**Mental model.** **Read routing** — sending read queries to replicas instead of the source — is how you scale reads (10.1), but it creates a specific hazard: **read-after-write inconsistency.** A user writes (a transfer), then immediately reads (their balance) — but if the read goes to a *lagging* replica (10.5), the write **isn't there yet**, so they see the *old* state. For money this is dangerous: "I paid but my balance didn't change → I'll pay again." **Read-after-write (RYW) consistency** is the guarantee that *you can read your own writes* — and achieving it despite lag requires specific patterns (route critical reads to the source, or wait for the replica to catch up to your write's GTID). Read routing is a scaling win that must be designed carefully around lag.

**How it actually works.** Reads are routed (by the application or a proxy like ProxySQL, 10.13) to the source or to replicas based on the query's freshness needs:
- **Route to a replica (scale):** for reads that tolerate staleness (reports, dashboards, search, "other users' data") — scales read throughput, offloads the source. Safe when slight staleness is acceptable.
- **Route to the source (fresh):** for reads that *must* see the latest (your own just-written data, anything driving a write decision) — gives up the read-scaling for those, but guarantees freshness. The simplest RYW fix: *route the user's reads to the source for a short window after they write.*
**Read-after-write patterns** (to get RYW without always hitting the source):
- **Read-from-source after write:** after a user writes, route *their* reads to the source (for a window / for that session) → they always see their own writes. Simple, common.
- **GTID-based wait ("read your writes" via causal consistency):** after a write, the app gets the write's **GTID** (10.7); before a replica read, it tells the replica "wait until you've applied GTID X" (`WAIT_FOR_EXECUTED_GTID_SET`) → the replica catches up to the write *before* serving the read → RYW from a replica. Stronger but more complex.
- **Sticky/session routing:** pin a user's session to a replica that's caught up, or to the source, for consistency.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Read routing solves the read-scaling need (10.1), but naive routing (all reads to replicas) breaks the *most intuitive* user expectation — *seeing your own writes* (read-after-write). RYW patterns solve this: they let you scale reads (most reads to replicas) *while* guaranteeing that the reads that *need* freshness (your own money, decision-driving reads) see it. For a payments system, RYW is essential: a user who just made a payment *must* see the updated balance (or they'll panic/retry, causing double-payments). It resolves the tension between "scale reads off replicas" and "don't show users stale data they just changed."

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** **Route-to-source-after-write:** simple and robust, but sends those reads to the source (less read-offload for them — and a user who writes a lot keeps hitting the source). **GTID-wait:** scales the reads to replicas *and* guarantees RYW, but adds latency (the read waits for the replica to catch up) and complexity (track GTIDs, use the wait function). **Sticky routing:** simple but coarse (pins a session, may not perfectly handle replica lag). The general tradeoff is **read-scaling vs consistency**: the more you route to replicas, the more you scale but the more you risk staleness; RYW patterns let you have *targeted* consistency (fresh where it matters, scaled elsewhere). The blunt alternative — *all reads to the source* — is always consistent but doesn't scale reads (defeating the purpose of replicas). The skill is classifying reads by freshness need and routing accordingly.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Reading from a lagging replica means you might not see your own recent writes — restore read-your-writes by reading from a fresh node or waiting for the replica to catch up to your write." This is the universal **read-after-write / session consistency** problem in any read-replicated or eventually-consistent system: a write to the primary isn't instantly on the replicas, so a follow-up read can miss it, and you fix it with *causal consistency* mechanisms (read-from-primary, version/timestamp waits, sticky sessions). The transferable insight: **eventual consistency breaks "read your own writes" by default; restore it by routing critical reads to a fresh source or by making the read wait for the write's version to propagate** — the same pattern as a write-through cache with read-after-write handling, a CDN with cache-busting after an update, or causal-consistency tokens in distributed databases. "Can this read tolerate not seeing the latest write?" is the routing decision, everywhere.

**MySQL-specific reality.** Read routing is done by the **application** (explicit source-vs-replica connections) or a **proxy** (ProxySQL, 10.13 — routes by query rules, can split reads/writes). RYW patterns: **route-to-source-after-write** (the common, simple approach — a user's reads go to the primary for a window after they write); **GTID-wait** (`SELECT WAIT_FOR_EXECUTED_GTID_SET(gtid)` on the replica before the read, after capturing the write's GTID — causal RYW from a replica); session/sticky routing. For our domain: a user who just made a payment has *their* balance read routed to the **source** (or via GTID-wait to a caught-up replica) so they see the new balance immediately (avoiding the panic-retry double-payment); *other* users' reads, reports, and reconciliation (M02/2.17) go to **replicas** (scaled, tolerate slight staleness). The dangerous default to avoid: routing *all* reads to replicas without RYW → the "paid but balance unchanged → pay again" double-charge bug. Read-routing + RYW design is a core fintech replication concern (10.15/10.16) and a frequent system-design interview topic.

## 10.7 · GTIDs: global transaction identifiers

**Mental model.** Originally, a replica tracked its position in the source's binlog by **file name + byte position** ("I've applied up to binlog file `mysql-bin.000042`, position `91823`") — fragile, because the file/position is *specific to one source's binlog* and breaks when you fail over (the new source has *different* binlog files/positions) or re-point replicas. **GTIDs (Global Transaction Identifiers)** fix this: every transaction gets a **globally-unique ID** (`source_uuid:sequence_number`) that *follows the transaction everywhere* — so a replica tracks "I've applied this *set* of transaction IDs," independent of which server's binlog they came from. GTIDs make **failover, re-pointing replicas, and consistency checks** vastly more robust — you reason about *which transactions* a node has, not *where in a file*.

**How it actually works.** When the source commits a transaction, it assigns it a **GTID**: `server_uuid:N` (the source's unique server UUID + a monotonic sequence number). The GTID is written to the binlog *with* the transaction and *propagates* — when a replica applies the transaction, it records *that GTID* in its **`gtid_executed`** set. So every node knows *exactly which transactions* (by GTID) it has applied — a *set* like `3E11FA47-...:1-1000000`. This enables:
- **Robust failover/re-pointing (10.10):** to point a replica at a new source (after promotion), you don't compute file/position — you just say "replicate from the new source"; the replica compares its `gtid_executed` to the new source's and **automatically streams exactly the transactions it's missing** (the GTID set difference). No manual position math.
- **Consistency/lag checks:** compare `gtid_executed` sets across nodes to see *exactly* which transactions a replica is missing (precise lag, 10.5; detecting divergence).
- **Idempotent application:** a replica won't re-apply a transaction whose GTID it already has (skips duplicates) — important for safety.
The hazard GTIDs introduce: **errant transactions** (10.12) — a transaction committed *directly on a replica* (not from the source) gets a GTID the source doesn't have, which can break failover (the topology now has a GTID that "shouldn't" exist) — a critical gotcha.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** GTIDs solve the **fragility of file+position tracking**, which was the bane of pre-GTID replication: every failover required error-prone manual computation of binlog positions across servers (get it wrong → replicas skip or duplicate transactions → divergence or data loss), and re-pointing replicas was a careful, dangerous operation. By giving each transaction a *server-independent global identity*, GTIDs make "which transactions does this node have?" a simple set comparison — so failover and re-pointing become *automatic and safe* (the node figures out what it's missing). This robustness is what makes modern automated failover (10.10/10.13) practical. GTIDs are essentially *required* for safe, automated, modern MySQL replication topologies.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** GTIDs are almost pure win for robustness, but they add: a little overhead (tracking the GTID sets), some operational rules (you can't have *gaps* or *errant* GTIDs — 10.12 — without trouble), and the errant-transaction hazard (a write directly on a replica creates a GTID the topology must reconcile). The alternative — **file+position** — is simpler conceptually but *fragile* (manual position math on failover, error-prone, no automatic "what am I missing?" logic) and is essentially legacy for serious deployments. The tradeoff is settled: **use GTIDs** for any modern, automated, failover-capable topology (the robustness is essential); the cost (overhead, the errant-transaction discipline) is well worth it. There's no real reason to use file+position in a new fintech deployment.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Give each operation a globally-unique, server-independent identity, so any node can determine exactly which operations it has and which it's missing — replication becomes set reconciliation, not position arithmetic." GTIDs embody the universal **global, content-independent operation identity** principle: instead of tracking position in *one* node's log (fragile across nodes), give each operation a global ID, so synchronization becomes "compute the set difference of applied IDs" (robust, automatic). This is the same idea as content-addressed/globally-IDed operations in distributed systems (vector clocks, Lamport timestamps, globally-unique event IDs in event sourcing M01/1.17, Kafka's global offsets within a partition), and as idempotency keys (M01/1.2 — a global ID making re-application safe). The transferable insight: **server-independent global IDs turn cross-node synchronization from fragile position-matching into robust set-reconciliation** — know *what* each node has, not *where it is*. The errant-transaction hazard is the universal "a globally-IDed operation appearing where it shouldn't breaks the reconciliation invariant" problem.

**MySQL-specific reality.** Enable with `gtid_mode=ON` + `enforce_gtid_consistency=ON`. A GTID is `server_uuid:transaction_sequence`; a node's applied set is `gtid_executed` (a *set* of ranges). Failover/re-pointing uses `CHANGE REPLICATION SOURCE TO … SOURCE_AUTO_POSITION=1` (the replica auto-computes what it's missing from the GTID difference — no file/position). `WAIT_FOR_EXECUTED_GTID_SET(gtid)` enables RYW (10.6). The **errant transaction** hazard (10.12): a transaction run *directly on a replica* (e.g., a maintenance write, or `super_read_only` not set) gets a GTID the source doesn't have → breaks failover (the replica can't be cleanly re-pointed; the errant GTID must be injected or reconciled). Prevent with `super_read_only=ON` on replicas (block *all* writes, even by admins). For our domain: GTIDs are **mandatory** for a safe, automated-failover payments topology — they make promoting a replica (10.10) and re-pointing the others *automatic and correct* (each finds exactly its missing transactions), and they enable precise lag/divergence detection (10.5) and RYW (10.6). Set `super_read_only=ON` on replicas to prevent errant transactions (10.12). GTIDs are the backbone of modern, failover-safe MySQL replication and the fintech topology (10.16).

## 10.8 · Replication threads & parallel replication

**Mental model.** Replication on a replica uses **threads**: an **IO/receiver thread** (streams the binlog from the source into the relay log — fast, just network, 10.2) and **SQL/applier thread(s)** (replay the relay log against the data). The problem: a **single applier thread** replays transactions *one at a time*, but the source committed them *in parallel* (many concurrent writers, M08) — so a single-threaded replica **can't keep up** with a write-heavy source and **lags** (10.5). **Parallel replication** fixes this: multiple applier worker threads replay *independent* transactions *concurrently* (preserving the necessary ordering for *dependent* ones), so the replica's apply throughput matches the source's commit throughput. It's the main mechanism for keeping replicas from falling behind under write load.

**How it actually works.** The challenge: replays must produce the *same* result as the source, so transactions that *conflict* (touch the same rows) must be applied in the *same order* — but *independent* transactions (touching different rows) can be applied in *any* order (or concurrently) without changing the result. Parallel replication exploits this:
- **Schema-based (older):** apply transactions on *different schemas/databases* in parallel (assumed independent). Coarse.
- **LOGICAL_CLOCK (modern, the standard):** the source tags transactions that were able to **commit together in the same group commit** (9.11) — meaning they didn't conflict (they were concurrently committable on the source) — with the same "logical clock" value. The replica can then apply transactions with the same logical-clock group **in parallel** (they're known-independent), while preserving order across groups. So the more concurrency the source had (bigger group commits), the more parallelism the replica gets — apply throughput tracks commit throughput.
- **WRITESET (newer, even more parallel):** track the actual rows each transaction modified (its "writeset") and parallelize any transactions with *non-overlapping* writesets — more aggressive parallelism than logical-clock.
Multiple **applier worker threads** then execute the parallelizable transactions concurrently. Tuning: `replica_parallel_workers` (number of workers), `replica_parallel_type` (LOGICAL_CLOCK), `binlog_transaction_dependency_tracking` (WRITESET for more parallelism).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Parallel replication solves the **single-threaded-applier lag** problem: a source with high write concurrency (M08 — many parallel transfers) generates binlog faster than one serial applier can replay it, so the replica falls progressively behind (lag grows unboundedly, 10.5 — eventually the replica is useless for reads and a huge data-loss window for failover). Parallelizing the apply (safely, by only parallelizing known-independent transactions) lets the replica's throughput *match* the source's, keeping lag bounded under write load. It's essential for any write-heavy replicated system — without it, replicas can't keep up and replication's benefits (fresh-enough reads, small failover window) evaporate.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Parallel replication trades **complexity for throughput**: more workers = more apply throughput (less lag) but more coordination (tracking dependencies, ensuring conflicting transactions stay ordered) and more replica CPU/memory. The dependency-tracking method trades precision for parallelism (schema-based: coarse, limited; logical-clock: good, based on source concurrency; writeset: most parallel, more tracking overhead). Too many workers can hurt (coordination overhead, contention on the replica). The alternative — single-threaded apply — is simple and always-correct but lags under load (the problem). The tuning question is "how many workers and which dependency-tracking method for my write pattern?" — generally LOGICAL_CLOCK or WRITESET with a worker count matched to the source's concurrency. There's no avoiding parallel apply for a write-heavy replicated system.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Replay a log in parallel by identifying independent operations (non-conflicting) that can be applied in any order, while preserving order only between dependent ones — match replay throughput to the original's parallelism." This is the universal **parallel log replay / dependency-aware parallelism** problem: an ordered log can be replayed faster than serially *if* you can determine which operations are independent (commute) and parallelize those while ordering the dependent ones — the same idea as instruction-level parallelism (reorder independent instructions), parallel stream processing (partition by key for independent parallelism), and deterministic parallel replay generally. The transferable insight: **a serial log can be applied in parallel up to its inherent dependency structure — independent operations parallelize, dependent ones must stay ordered — and the original's concurrency tells you how much parallelism is safe** (the logical-clock insight: what committed together didn't conflict). Recognizing "these operations are independent (different rows/keys) so they can be reordered/parallelized" is the key to scaling any ordered-log replay.

**MySQL-specific reality.** Threads: the **IO/receiver thread** (one, streams binlog → relay log) and **SQL/applier thread(s)** (one coordinator + N workers with parallel replication). Configure: `replica_parallel_workers=N` (e.g., 4–16+), `replica_parallel_type=LOGICAL_CLOCK` (the standard), `binlog_transaction_dependency_tracking=WRITESET` (on the *source* — enables more parallelism via row-level writesets), `replica_preserve_commit_order=ON` (preserve source commit order for consistency/GTID). Monitor lag (10.5) to verify parallel apply is keeping up. For our domain: the payments primary has high write concurrency (many parallel transfers, M08), so its replicas **must** use parallel replication (LOGICAL_CLOCK or WRITESET, enough workers) to keep apply throughput matching commit throughput — otherwise reporting replicas lag badly (stale reports, 10.5) and the failover window grows (10.10). WRITESET-based dependency tracking gives the best parallelism for a write-heavy ledger (parallelize any non-row-overlapping transfers). Parallel replication is what makes replicas viable under fintech write load — without it, a write-heavy primary's replicas fall hopelessly behind. It's a core scaling/tuning concern (10.15).
