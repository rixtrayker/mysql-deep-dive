# M10 · Replication ★ — Pass A (Skeleton)

> **Pass A goal:** lock the ordered concept list, a one-line mental model per concept, and the planned diagram + bespoke-SVG inventory + worked example per concept. No prose yet — that's Pass B.
>
> **Module mental model:** *Up to now there was one server holding all the data — a single point of failure and a single machine's capacity. Replication copies the data to other servers by **shipping the binary log** (M09 — the same durable log that records every committed change) from a **source** to one or more **replicas**, which replay it to stay in sync. This buys three things: **read scaling** (send reads to replicas), **high availability** (promote a replica if the source dies), and **durability/geo-redundancy** (a second copy, possibly in another datacenter). But it introduces the defining problem of distributed data: **the copies are not instantly identical.** Async replication means a replica is always slightly behind (**replication lag**) — so a read from a replica can be stale, and a source that dies before a replica caught up loses committed transactions. Every replication decision is a point on the **consistency / availability / latency** triangle: how synchronous (durability vs latency), how to fail over (availability vs data loss), how to route reads (scale vs staleness). Replication is where "the database" stops being one box and becomes a* distributed system *— with all the subtlety that implies, and where a careless choice silently loses money.*
>
> **Threads carried in this module:**
> - **Durability** — replication is a *second durable copy*; **semi-synchronous** replication makes a commit wait for a replica's acknowledgment (replica-confirmed durability) — directly extending M09's durability posture (a committed transfer survives even total loss of the source's node). The binlog (M09) is the mechanism.
> - **Money-never-lies** — *the* fintech replication hazards: **replication lag → stale reads → money bugs** (read a balance off a lagging replica → double-spend / wrong-balance decision); a **source that dies before a replica applied a committed transfer loses it** (the promote-before-apply data-loss window); **split-brain** (two sources) corrupts the ledger. Replication is where money can be silently lost or duplicated at the distribution layer.
> - **Generics-first** — log shipping, async vs sync replication, the leader/follower (primary/replica) model, failover, and consensus are universal distributed-systems concepts (every replicated datastore, Raft/Paxos, Kafka replication); MySQL is one realization with specific mechanisms (binlog formats, GTIDs, relay logs).
> - **Tradeoff** — *the* distributed tradeoff, previewing CAP/PACELC (M12): consistency vs availability vs latency. Async (fast, available, but stale/lossy) vs semi-sync (durable, but slower) vs sync/group (consistent, but more latency/coordination). Every replication choice is a point on this surface.
>
> **Prereqs:** M09 (the **binlog** — the durable log replication ships; the durability posture semi-sync extends; group commit/2PC ensuring redo↔binlog consistency, 9.11), M07 (transactions/durability — what's being replicated and what "lost on crash" means), M08 (concurrency — parallel replication applies concurrent transactions), M02/2.17 (the normalized-ledger/derived-balance + reconciliation pattern that read-replicas serve and that detects drift), M01/1.14 (the read access patterns replicas offload). **Leads into:** M11 (Sharding — replication scales *reads*; sharding scales *writes*; together = the scaled topology), M12 (Distributed Data Concerns — CAP/PACELC, the dual-write problem, **CDC off the binlog**, the outbox pattern — replication's concepts generalized to distributed transactions), M13 (Ops — backups/PITR built on the binlog; monitoring lag), **M15 (Failure Modes & Data Loss — the catastrophic replication failures: split-brain divergence, promote-before-apply loss, GTID errant transactions, relay-log corruption)**, M16 (the platform's replicated, sharded topology).
