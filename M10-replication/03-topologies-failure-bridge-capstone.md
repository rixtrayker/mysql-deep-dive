## 10.9 · Replication topologies ★

**Mental model.** Replication can be arranged in different **shapes**, each serving a different need. **Source → replica** (the basic pair). **One source → many replicas (fan-out)** — for read scaling (many replicas share the read load) and HA (multiple failover targets). **Chained** (source → replica → replica's replica) — to reduce load on the source (replicas replicate from other replicas) or bridge regions. **Multi-source** (many sources → one replica) — to *aggregate* data from several sources (e.g., collecting from multiple shards, M11, into one reporting replica). **Group replication** (a consensus cluster where any node can take writes, with automatic conflict handling and failover) — for strong consistency + built-in HA. The topology is a design choice driven by *what you need* (read scaling, geo-distribution, aggregation, HA).

**How it actually works.**
- **Source → replica(s) / fan-out:** one source, N replicas each streaming its binlog (10.2). Reads fan out across replicas (scale, 10.6); any replica can be promoted on source failure (HA, 10.10). The workhorse topology. The source's binlog-serving and the replicas' independent lag (10.5) are the constraints.
- **Chained (relay) replication:** a replica replicates from *another replica* (which replicates from the source). Reduces the *source's* fan-out load (it serves the binlog to fewer direct replicas) and can bridge regions (one replica per region, then local replicas chain off it). Cost: *additive lag* (each hop adds lag) and a longer failure chain.
- **Multi-source:** one replica streams binlogs from *multiple* sources (into separate replication channels), merging their data. Used to **aggregate shards** (M11 — many write-shards → one combined reporting replica) or consolidate. Requires non-overlapping data (or conflict handling) to avoid collisions.
- **Group Replication (consensus cluster):** a set of nodes running a Paxos-based protocol; transactions are *certified* by a majority before commit (10.4 — sync). Supports **single-primary** (one writable, others read-only, automatic failover) or **multi-primary** (all writable, with conflict detection). The basis of **InnoDB Cluster** (10.13). Strong consistency + automatic HA, at coordination/latency cost.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Different needs require different shapes: **read scaling** → fan-out (many replicas); **reducing source load / geo-bridging** → chained; **aggregating multiple write sources** (sharded reporting, M11) → multi-source; **strong consistency + automatic HA** → group replication. Having a vocabulary of topologies lets you *compose* the right structure for your requirements — and real systems combine them (e.g., per-region fan-out clusters, chained across regions, with multi-source aggregation for global reporting). The topology *is* the high-level architecture of a replicated/scaled deployment.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Each topology trades differently: **fan-out** (simple, scales reads, but the source serves all binlog streams and is the single write point); **chained** (offloads the source, bridges regions, but adds per-hop lag and failure-chain complexity); **multi-source** (aggregates, but needs non-conflicting data and per-channel management); **group replication** (strong consistency + auto-HA, but coordination latency and operational complexity, and write throughput capped by majority agreement). The big divide: **async topologies** (fan-out/chained — fast, available, but lag + failover loss window) vs **group replication** (consistent, auto-failover, but slower). For most read-scaling + HA needs, **async fan-out with GTIDs + automated failover** (10.10/10.13) is the pragmatic choice; **group replication / InnoDB Cluster** when strong consistency + hands-off HA justify the cost. Sharding (M11) is *orthogonal* — it scales writes and is *combined* with replication (each shard is itself replicated).

**Generics / first-principles.** "Compose replicas into a topology that matches your needs — fan-out for read scale, chains for load/geo, aggregation for consolidation, consensus clusters for strong consistency + auto-failover." Replication topologies are instances of universal **distributed-system topology patterns**: leader-follower fan-out (read replicas everywhere), relay/cascade (CDN tiers, multi-level caches), aggregation (fan-in collectors), and consensus groups (Raft/Paxos clusters). The transferable insight: **the arrangement of replicas is a composable design space — pick the shape per requirement (scale/locality/aggregation/consistency), and compose shapes for complex needs**. The async-vs-consensus divide (fan-out vs group replication) is the universal "eventually-consistent-but-fast vs strongly-consistent-but-coordinated" topology choice, the same as in any distributed datastore. Topology design is distributed-systems architecture.

**MySQL-specific reality.** MySQL supports: **source→replica fan-out** (the classic, with GTIDs 10.7), **chained** (a replica with `log_replica_updates=ON` re-logs to its own binlog so *its* replicas can chain), **multi-source** (multiple replication *channels* via `FOR CHANNEL`, for aggregation), and **Group Replication** (the consensus cluster, single- or multi-primary, underlying **InnoDB Cluster**, 10.13). For our domain: a payments platform typically runs **per-region fan-out** (a primary + read-replicas + a standby in each region), possibly **chained or cross-region replicas** for DR, **multi-source aggregation** to combine sharded ledgers (M11) into a global reporting replica (M02/2.17), and optionally **InnoDB Cluster** (group replication) for strong-consistency HA on the core. The topology is the platform's replication architecture (10.16, M16) — composed from these shapes per requirement (read scale, geo-DR, sharded aggregation, HA). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the topologies.

## 10.10 · Failover & promotion (and the data-loss window) ★

**Mental model.** When the source dies, the system must **promote a replica to be the new source** (failover) — otherwise writes can't happen (total outage). But here's the danger: under **async replication** (10.4), a replica might **not have received the source's last committed transactions** when the source died — so promoting it **loses those transactions** (the **promote-before-apply data-loss window**). The committed transfers that were on the source but hadn't reached the promoted replica are *gone*. Failover trades availability (writes resume) against potential data loss (the gap), and the size of the gap = the replica's lag at failure time (10.5). **Fencing** (ensuring the old source can't come back and cause split-brain, 10.11) and **semi-sync** (10.4 — bounding the loss to received transactions) are how you manage it.

**How it actually works.** Failover sequence (automated by an orchestrator, 10.13):
1. **Detect** the source is dead (health checks fail).
2. **Fence** the old source (10.11 — ensure it's truly down / can't accept writes, to prevent split-brain).
3. **Choose** the most up-to-date replica (the one with the largest `gtid_executed` / least lag — to minimize loss).
4. **Promote** it to source (make it writable: `super_read_only=OFF`).
5. **Re-point** the other replicas at the new source (with GTIDs, 10.7, each auto-finds what it's missing — robust).
6. **Re-route** writes to the new source (via the proxy/app, 10.13).
**The data-loss window:** between steps, *any committed transactions that the source had but the promoted replica didn't receive are lost.* Under **async**, this window = the replica's lag (could be seconds of transfers). Under **semi-sync** (10.4), the source waited for ≥1 replica to *receive* each commit before acknowledging — so *if you promote that replica* (or one caught up to it), the loss is bounded to *zero* received-but-acknowledged transactions (the committed transfers were guaranteed on a replica). This is *why* semi-sync matters for money: it bounds the failover loss window. Choosing the *most-caught-up* replica (step 3) further minimizes loss in async.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Failover solves **availability**: a single source is a single point of failure, so the system must be able to continue (resume writes) when it dies — by promoting a replica. But it surfaces the *fundamental tension* of async replication: you can have **availability** (promote and continue) *or* **zero data loss** (wait for all replicas to catch up — but the source is dead, you can't), not both instantly. Failover is the mechanism that *chooses availability* (resume writes) and *manages the data-loss window* (minimize it via semi-sync + choosing the freshest replica + fencing). Understanding it is essential because *how you fail over determines whether you lose committed money* — the difference between a clean failover (no loss, semi-sync, freshest replica, fenced) and a catastrophe (lost transfers, split-brain) is design and tooling.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The core tradeoff is **availability vs data loss** (a CAP manifestation, M12): failover *fast* (promote quickly, resume writes — available, but more loss risk if you promote a behind replica) vs *safe* (wait to confirm the freshest replica, ideally with semi-sync guaranteeing no loss — but slower, and if no replica is caught up, you may have to choose loss or unavailability). **Semi-sync** (10.4) shifts the tradeoff (bounds loss to ~zero received transactions, at commit-latency cost). **Manual vs automated failover:** automated (orchestrator, 10.13) is fast and consistent but risks bad promotions (promoting a behind replica, or failing to fence → split-brain, 10.11); manual is careful but slow (downtime). **Fencing is non-negotiable** — failover *without* fencing the old source risks split-brain (10.11), worse than data loss. The fintech choice: semi-sync + GTID + automated-but-careful failover (fence, choose freshest) — minimizing both loss and split-brain risk. The catastrophic failover incidents (lost transfers, split-brain) are M15.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Failover trades availability for a data-loss window equal to how far the promoted node was behind — minimize it (synchronous acks, choose the freshest, fence the old) but you can't have instant availability *and* zero loss when the leader dies mid-replication." This is the universal **leader failover / data-loss-window** problem in any leader-follower system: when the leader dies, you promote a follower, and anything the leader had that the follower didn't is lost unless replication was synchronous — the same in Raft (a leader can lose uncommitted entries; committed-by-majority survive), Kafka (unclean leader election loses un-replicated messages), Postgres failover, every replicated store. The transferable insight: **promoting a follower after leader death loses whatever hadn't replicated to it — synchronous replication (wait for the follower before acking) bounds this to zero, async doesn't — and you must fence the old leader to avoid split-brain**. This *is* the CAP tradeoff (M12) made concrete: partition/failure forces choosing availability (promote, maybe lose) or consistency (wait, maybe unavailable).

**MySQL-specific reality.** Failover is orchestrated by tools (Orchestrator, MHA concepts, **InnoDB Cluster**'s built-in automation, 10.13): detect → fence → choose freshest replica (largest `gtid_executed`) → promote (`super_read_only=OFF`) → re-point others (GTID auto-position, 10.7) → re-route (ProxySQL, 10.13). The **data-loss window** under async = the promoted replica's lag (10.5); **semi-sync** (10.4) bounds it (the committed transactions were guaranteed received by a replica). `super_read_only=ON` on replicas (10.7) prevents errant writes and aids clean failover. For our domain: a payments platform fails over with **semi-sync** (so a committed transfer was on ≥1 replica → promoting it loses *no* confirmed transfers), **GTID** (auto-re-point, 10.7), **fencing** (prevent split-brain, 10.11), choosing the **freshest** replica, and **automated orchestration** (fast, but careful) — so a source death is a brief blip with *no lost money*, not a data-loss incident. Getting failover right (semi-sync + fence + freshest + GTID) is *the* HA correctness decision for money (10.15/10.16); getting it wrong is an M15 incident (lost transfers, split-brain). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws failover + the data-loss window + fencing.

## 10.11 · Split-brain & the danger of two sources ★

**Mental model.** The **worst** replication failure: a network partition or a botched failover leaves **two servers both accepting writes** (two "sources") — and they **diverge irreconcilably** (each accepts different, conflicting writes the other doesn't have). This is **split-brain**, and for money it's catastrophic: the ledger *forks* into two contradictory versions (different transfers on each), and there's no clean way to merge them (which fork is "right"? both have real committed money). Unlike data loss (you lose *some* transactions — bad but bounded), split-brain *corrupts* the entire dataset's consistency (two conflicting truths). **Fencing / STONITH** (ensuring only one node can ever accept writes) is the *prevention* — and prevention is the only good answer, because split-brain is barely recoverable.

**How it actually works.** Split-brain arises when the "single writable source" invariant is violated — *two* nodes accept writes simultaneously:
- **Botched failover:** the orchestrator promotes a replica (new source) but the **old source isn't actually dead** (it was just unreachable — a network blip) or comes *back* — now *both* the old and new source accept writes. (This is why **fencing the old source is non-negotiable in failover, 10.10.**)
- **Network partition (multi-primary):** in a multi-primary setup (or a partition), each side thinks the other is dead and continues accepting writes → two diverging sources.
Once two sources take conflicting writes, they **diverge**: source A has transfer X, source B has transfer Y, neither has the other's, and GTIDs (10.7) now contain *errant* transactions (each source generated GTIDs the other doesn't have, 10.12). **Reconciliation is brutal:** you can't just pick one (the other has real committed money); merging requires manual, transaction-by-transaction analysis (which transfers to keep, how to resolve conflicts) — often with real financial loss/duplication. **Prevention** is the answer: **fencing** (STONITH — "Shoot The Other Node In The Head" — forcibly kill/isolate the old source before promotion, so it *can't* take writes) and **quorum/consensus** (group replication, 10.9 — a majority must agree, so a minority partition *can't* accept writes → no split-brain by design).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** Split-brain is the failure mode the *entire failover process must prevent*. It exists because the naive "promote a replica when the source seems down" can violate the "exactly one writable source" invariant (if the old source isn't really down or comes back). Understanding it is what makes you take **fencing** seriously: failover *without* guaranteeing the old source is dead is a split-brain waiting to happen — and split-brain is *worse than the outage you were avoiding* (a corrupted, forked ledger vs a temporary inability to write). For money, split-brain is an existential threat (the books fork irreconcilably), so the whole HA design is oriented around *preventing* it (fence, or use consensus). It's the reason "just promote a replica" is dangerously incomplete.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Split-brain *prevention* trades **failover speed/simplicity for safety**: **fencing** (STONITH — kill the old source via power management / network isolation before promoting) adds a step and infrastructure (a way to reliably kill a node) but *guarantees* the old source can't take writes — essential. **Consensus/quorum** (group replication, 10.9 — only a majority can commit, so a partitioned minority *can't* accept writes) prevents split-brain *by design* (no node can write without majority agreement) — at the cost of coordination latency (10.4) and needing ≥3 nodes (for a majority). The dangerous "alternative" — automated failover *without* fencing — is the split-brain trap (fast, but risks the catastrophe). The choice: fence rigorously (for async fan-out + orchestrated failover) or use consensus (group replication, prevents it structurally). For money, *never* fail over without fencing or consensus. The catastrophic split-brain *recovery* (when prevention failed) is M15.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Two nodes both believing they're the leader and accepting writes causes irreconcilable divergence — the 'exactly one leader' invariant must be enforced by fencing the old leader or by quorum, never assumed." Split-brain is *the* canonical distributed-systems failure: the **dual-leader / partition** problem, universal to every leader-based system (Raft/Paxos prevent it via majority-quorum leader election — a node *can't* be leader without majority votes; without quorum, you need fencing). The transferable insight: **leader-based systems must guarantee at most one leader at a time — via consensus quorum (structural prevention) or fencing/STONITH (forcibly removing the old leader) — because two leaders accepting writes diverge irreconcilably**. This is why consensus algorithms exist (to safely elect *one* leader despite partitions) and why "fence before promote" is sacred in failover. The CAP connection (M12): during a partition, you *must* sacrifice availability on the minority side (refuse writes) to preserve consistency (no split-brain) — the consensus/quorum choice. Split-brain is what happens when you (wrongly) choose availability on both sides.

**MySQL-specific reality.** Split-brain prevention in MySQL: **fencing** in async-fan-out failover (the orchestrator, 10.13, must reliably isolate/kill the old source — via STONITH, network ACLs, or `super_read_only` enforcement — before promoting); **`super_read_only=ON`** on replicas (block *all* writes including admin, so a non-promoted node can't accidentally become a second source); **Group Replication / InnoDB Cluster** (10.9/10.13 — quorum-based, *structurally* prevents split-brain: a partitioned minority can't reach majority so can't commit → no divergence — the strongest prevention). **Errant transactions** (10.12) are the GTID symptom of writes on the wrong node (a split-brain precursor). For our domain: a payments platform *must* prevent split-brain — either **rigorous fencing** in orchestrated failover (guarantee the old primary is dead/isolated before promoting, + `super_read_only` on all replicas) or **InnoDB Cluster** (quorum prevents it by design). A forked ledger (two primaries with conflicting transfers) is an unrecoverable money-never-lies catastrophe — so prevention is the *only* acceptable strategy, and it's the central HA-safety requirement (10.15/10.16). The catastrophic split-brain incident/reconciliation is M15. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws split-brain divergence + fencing.

## 10.12 · Critical internals: the silent failures ★

**Mental model.** The plan-flagged "critical internals" — the **non-obvious replication failures that don't announce themselves** and silently lose or corrupt money. The dangerous ones: **(1) semi-sync silently degrading to async** — on timeout, semi-sync (10.4) *falls back to async* and the source keeps committing *without* waiting for replicas, so you *think* you have replica-confirmed durability but you *don't* (and a source crash now loses data you believed was safe). **(2) Relay-log corruption** — a corrupted relay log on a replica breaks/halts its replication (or silently applies garbage). **(3) GTID errant transactions** (10.7) — a write *directly on a replica* creates a GTID the topology shouldn't have, breaking failover. **(4) The promote-before-apply loss window** (10.10) — promoting a behind replica loses transactions. These are dangerous *precisely because they're silent*: replication *looks* healthy while the guarantee you rely on is gone.

**How it actually works (the silent-failure catalog):**
- **Semi-sync → async degradation:** semi-sync waits for a replica's ack up to `rpl_semi_sync_source_timeout` (default ~10s). If no replica acks in time (replica down, slow, network issue), the source **logs a warning and switches to async** — it *keeps committing without waiting*. It auto-switches *back* to semi-sync when a replica catches up. **The danger:** during the async window, commits are *not* replica-confirmed — so a source crash loses them, *despite your config saying semi-sync.* You *believe* you have replica durability; you don't. **Detection:** monitor `Rpl_semi_sync_source_status` (1=semi-sync active, 0=degraded to async) and *alert* on degradation — *this is the critical money gotcha.*
- **Relay-log corruption:** a replica's relay log gets corrupted (crash, disk issue) → replication halts with an error (or, worse, applies corrupted data). Recovery: with GTIDs, re-fetch the missing transactions from the source (the relay log is *re-derivable* — it's a copy of the source's binlog); without GTIDs, painful position math.
- **Errant transactions (GTID):** a transaction committed directly on a replica (not from the source) → a GTID only that replica has → on failover, the topology can't cleanly reconcile (the errant GTID "shouldn't exist"). Prevent with `super_read_only=ON` (10.7).
- **Promote-before-apply window (10.10):** covered above — async failover loses the lag's worth of transactions.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** This concept exists because **replication's most dangerous failures are silent** — the system *appears* healthy (commits succeed, replicas seem connected) while a guarantee you depend on (replica durability, no divergence, clean failover) has quietly evaporated. The *semi-sync degrades to async* case is the worst: it directly undermines the durability posture you configured for money (10.4/9.16) *without any obvious error* — you only discover it when a crash loses data you "guaranteed." Knowing these silent failures is what lets you **monitor for them** (alert on semi-sync degradation, on errant GTIDs, on relay-log errors, on lag) instead of *assuming* your config delivers what it claims. For money, this is the difference between *believing* you're durable and *verifying* it. The plan flagged these precisely because they're the gap between "configured for safety" and "actually safe."

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** These aren't tradeoffs to choose — they're hazards to *detect and prevent*. Semi-sync's degrade-to-async is a *deliberate* design (it prioritizes *availability* — the source keeps working if replicas are down — over *failing the write*); the "fix" is to **monitor and alert** (know when you're degraded), and optionally to use a stricter setup (more replicas to ack, `rpl_semi_sync_source_wait_for_replica_count`, or group replication's quorum which *can't* silently degrade — a partitioned minority *can't* commit). The alternative to monitoring is *blind trust in config* — the trap. For errant transactions: `super_read_only` prevents them. For relay-log corruption: GTIDs make recovery clean. The meta-lesson: **a configured guarantee is only real if you monitor that it's actually holding** — for money, you *verify* durability (alert on semi-sync status, lag, errant GTIDs), you don't *assume* it.

**Generics / first-principles.** "A safety mechanism that *silently degrades* (fails open) gives you false confidence — the guarantee is gone but nothing tells you — so you must *monitor that the guarantee is actually holding*, not just that it's configured." This is the universal **fail-silent / fail-open hazard**: a protection that quietly stops protecting (semi-sync → async, a circuit breaker stuck closed, a backup job silently failing, a cache silently bypassed) is *more dangerous* than one that fails loudly, because you keep relying on a guarantee that's gone. The transferable insight: **monitor that your safety guarantees are *actively holding*, not just configured — especially ones that degrade gracefully (fail open), because graceful degradation hides the loss of the guarantee.** "It's configured for X" ≠ "X is currently true." For anything critical (money durability), instrument and alert on the *actual* guarantee state. This is the same "tested backups, verified durability" discipline as M09/9.7 (a fsync that lied) and M15 — the gap between *believed* and *actual* safety.

**MySQL-specific reality.** The MySQL silent failures + detection: **semi-sync degradation** — monitor `Rpl_semi_sync_source_status` (`SHOW STATUS LIKE 'Rpl_semi_sync_source_status'`; 0 = degraded to async) and `Rpl_semi_sync_source_no_tx`, *alert on degradation* (the critical money gotcha — `rpl_semi_sync_source_timeout` governs the fallback). **Relay-log issues** — monitor `Last_IO_Error`/`Last_SQL_Error` in `SHOW REPLICA STATUS`; recover via GTID re-fetch. **Errant transactions** — detect by comparing GTID sets across the topology (Orchestrator flags them); prevent with `super_read_only=ON` on replicas (10.7). **Promote-before-apply** — bounded by semi-sync (10.10) and choosing the freshest replica. For our domain: a payments platform running semi-sync **must alert on `Rpl_semi_sync_source_status=0`** (degraded to async) — because during that window, "durable" transfers are *only* on the source and a crash loses them (undermining 9.16's posture *silently*). It must set `super_read_only=ON` (no errant transactions), monitor lag and replica errors (M13), and verify GTID consistency. The lesson: **for money, you don't assume your replication guarantees hold — you monitor that they do**, because the failures are silent. These silent failures are the bridge to M15 (the catastrophic data-loss incidents they cause). The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the silent-failure catalog.

## 10.13 · High-availability solutions (the orchestration layer)

**Mental model.** Raw replication (10.1–10.12) gives you the *mechanism*, but turning it into a *highly-available system* — one that *automatically* detects a source failure, promotes a replica, and re-routes traffic, with no human in the loop — requires an **orchestration layer**: tools that *detect* failures and *manage failover* (Orchestrator, MHA concepts, InnoDB Cluster's built-in automation) plus a *routing layer* that directs clients to the current source/replicas (ProxySQL, MySQL Router). These tools are what make replication an *HA solution* rather than just a copy mechanism — they automate the failover sequence (10.10) and the read/write routing (10.6) that would otherwise be manual and slow.

**How it actually works.** The HA stack has two roles:
- **Topology management / failover automation:** a tool monitors the cluster's health, detects a dead source, and runs the failover sequence (10.10: fence → choose freshest → promote → re-point via GTID → ). Examples: **Orchestrator** (detects, visualizes, and automates failover for async topologies; handles fencing, GTID re-pointing), **MHA** (Master High Availability — older, similar concept), and **InnoDB Cluster** (group replication, 10.9, with *built-in* automatic failover — the cluster elects a new primary via consensus, no external tool needed — the modern integrated solution).
- **Connection routing / read-write split:** a proxy sits between clients and the database, routing **writes to the current source** and **reads to replicas** (10.6), and *automatically updating* when failover changes the source. Examples: **ProxySQL** (a powerful proxy — query-rule-based read/write splitting, connection pooling M04/4.2, automatic failover-aware routing) and **MySQL Router** (the official router, integrates with InnoDB Cluster). The proxy hides the topology from the application (the app connects to the proxy; the proxy handles which server).
Together: the orchestrator handles *failover* (who's the source), the proxy handles *routing* (send traffic to the right server) — and the application sees a stable endpoint despite failures.

**Why it exists / what it solves.** This layer solves the **automation gap**: replication's mechanism (10.1–10.12) requires *operating* — detecting failures, executing failover correctly (fence, freshest, GTID re-point — easy to get wrong, 10.10/10.11), and updating routing — and doing this *manually* means slow recovery (minutes of downtime) and human error (botched failover → split-brain, 10.11). The orchestration layer *automates* this: failure → automatic, correct, fast failover + re-routing, with no human and no downtime-minutes. It's what makes a replicated MySQL deployment *actually highly available* (seconds of disruption, automatic) rather than *capable of HA if someone's awake to do it right*. For a payments platform that must be always-on, this automation is essential.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The orchestration layer trades **operational complexity for automated HA**: it adds components (the orchestrator, the proxy) that must themselves be reliable (a failed orchestrator or proxy is a new failure point — they need their own HA), and automated failover risks *bad automated decisions* (promoting a behind replica, or failing to fence → split-brain — so the tool's correctness, esp. fencing, is critical, 10.11). **InnoDB Cluster** (integrated group-replication-based HA + MySQL Router) is the modern, cohesive choice (consensus prevents split-brain structurally, 10.11; built-in failover); **Orchestrator + ProxySQL** is a popular flexible stack for async topologies (powerful, battle-tested, but you assemble it). The alternative — *manual failover* — is simpler (no extra components) but slow (downtime) and error-prone (human mistakes under pressure). For money/HA, automated orchestration is standard, with the caveat that the orchestrator *must* fence correctly (10.11). The choice is which stack (InnoDB Cluster vs Orchestrator+ProxySQL), not whether to automate.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Turning a replicated cluster into an HA system requires automated failure-detection + leader-election/promotion + traffic-rerouting — the control plane that makes failover hands-off and correct." This is the universal **control plane / orchestration** layer of any HA distributed system: detection (health checks), decision (elect/promote, with fencing/consensus for safety), and routing (service discovery / load balancing to the current leader). The transferable insight: **raw replication is the *data plane*; HA also needs a *control plane* (detect → decide → re-route) that automates failover correctly (especially fencing) and a routing layer that hides the topology from clients** — the same pattern as Kubernetes (controllers + service routing), Raft-based systems (built-in leader election + client redirection), and every managed datastore's HA. The orchestrator is the control plane; the proxy is service discovery/routing. Automating the control plane (correctly) is what makes "highly available" real.

**MySQL-specific reality.** The HA stack: **failover automation** — **Orchestrator** (async topology management, failover, fencing, GTID re-pointing — popular, powerful), **InnoDB Cluster** (the modern integrated solution: Group Replication, 10.9, for consensus-based auto-failover + split-brain prevention, 10.11, managed via MySQL Shell), MHA (legacy concept); **routing** — **ProxySQL** (query-rule read/write split, connection pooling, failover-aware routing — the flexible choice), **MySQL Router** (official, InnoDB Cluster-integrated). For our domain: a payments platform uses an HA stack — e.g., **InnoDB Cluster + MySQL Router** (consensus HA, structural split-brain prevention, integrated) *or* **semi-sync + Orchestrator + ProxySQL** (async with orchestrated, fenced failover + smart routing) — so a source failure triggers *automatic, fenced, GTID-based* failover (no lost money with semi-sync, no split-brain) and *automatic re-routing* (the app's writes follow the new primary), with seconds of disruption, not minutes of outage. This orchestration layer is what makes the platform *always-on* (10.15/10.16, M16). Choosing and operating it correctly (esp. fencing) is the operational HA decision.

## 10.14 · Replication for backups, PITR & CDC (bridge)

**Mental model.** The **binlog isn't just for replicas** — it's a general-purpose **stream of all committed changes**, and that stream powers *several* things beyond replication. **Point-in-time recovery (PITR, M13):** replay the binlog from a backup up to a chosen moment to recover to *any* point in time (e.g., just before a bad deploy). **Change data capture (CDC, M12):** stream the binlog to *other systems* (Kafka via Debezium/Maxwell) so the rest of the architecture (search indexes, caches, analytics, other services) reacts to database changes in real time. So the same binlog mechanism (10.2) that feeds replicas *also* feeds backups and the broader event-driven architecture. This concept is the **bridge**: replication's mechanism (binlog shipping) generalizes into PITR (M13) and CDC/outbox (M12) — the binlog as the system's change stream.

**How it actually works.** The binlog (M09) is a durable, ordered log of every committed change (10.2). Three consumers of that stream:
- **Replicas (this module):** stream and replay it to maintain copies (10.2).
- **PITR (M13):** take periodic full backups + *retain the binlogs since* the backup. To recover to time T: restore the last backup before T, then **replay the binlog up to T** (the binlog *is* the redo-from-backup). This recovers to *any* point (e.g., the instant before an accidental `DELETE` or a bad migration). The binlog's role: the change stream that "rolls forward" a backup to a precise moment.
- **CDC (M12):** a connector (**Debezium**, Maxwell) *reads the binlog* (acting like a replica — streaming it) and publishes each change as an event to **Kafka** (or similar). Downstream consumers (search indexers, cache invalidators, analytics pipelines, other microservices) react to these change events. This is how the database becomes a *source of truth* the rest of the event-driven architecture subscribes to — and it's the basis of the **outbox pattern** (M12, avoiding the dual-write problem: write to the DB, and CDC reliably propagates the change, instead of the app writing to both the DB *and* a message queue, which can partially fail).
So the binlog is the *one* change stream, consumed three ways: replicas (copies), PITR (recovery), CDC (integration).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** This concept reveals that **the binlog is a foundational asset, not just a replication detail** — it's the authoritative, ordered, durable stream of everything the database did, and that's enormously valuable: it enables **precise recovery** (PITR — recover to any moment, M13) and **reliable integration** (CDC — propagate changes to the whole architecture without dual-writes, M12). For a payments system, both are critical: PITR lets you recover the ledger to just before a corruption/bad-deploy (M13/M15); CDC lets the rest of the platform (notifications, analytics, fraud detection, reconciliation feeds) react to ledger changes *reliably* (the binlog *is* the change feed, so nothing is missed). It's the bridge from "replication" to "the database as the heart of an event-driven system."

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** Using the binlog for PITR requires **retaining binlogs** (storage, `binlog_expire_logs_seconds`) and `sync_binlog=1` (M09/9.10 — durable binlog, so PITR doesn't miss committed transactions). Using it for CDC requires **ROW format** (10.3 — so changes are exact/deterministic for downstream consumers) and a CDC connector (Debezium — operational complexity, but reliable). The CDC alternative — *dual-writing* (the app writes to the DB *and* publishes to Kafka itself) — is the **dual-write problem** (M12): the two writes can partially fail (DB succeeds, Kafka fails → inconsistency), which CDC/outbox *avoids* (only write to the DB; CDC propagates from the binlog reliably). So CDC-off-the-binlog is *the correct way* to integrate (vs racy dual-writes). The tradeoff is the binlog-retention/CDC-infrastructure cost vs the reliability gain (precise recovery, no dual-write inconsistency). For money, both PITR and CDC-via-outbox are standard (the reliability is worth it).

**Generics / first-principles.** "A durable, ordered change log is a reusable asset: replay it to copy state (replication), roll a backup forward to any point (PITR), or stream it to integrate with other systems (CDC) — the log is the system's source of truth for *what changed*." This is the universal **change log / event stream as integration backbone** principle: a durable ordered log of changes (the binlog, a WAL, an event log, M01/1.17) can be *replayed* for copies/recovery and *subscribed to* for integration — the same idea as Kafka-as-the-central-log, event sourcing (the event log feeds projections *and* integrations), and CDC pipelines generally. The transferable insight: **once you have a reliable ordered log of all changes, it serves replication, recovery, *and* integration — don't dual-write to a separate queue (racy); let consumers subscribe to the change log** (the outbox/CDC pattern, M12). The binlog being reused for replicas, PITR, and CDC is the "log is primary, everything derives from it" theme (M01/1.17, M08/8.2, M09/9.14) at the architecture level. This bridges to M12 (the distributed-integration generalization) and M13 (PITR).

**MySQL-specific reality.** The binlog (M09) consumed three ways: **replicas** (this module), **PITR** (M13 — full backup + binlog replay to a point: `mysqlbinlog` to apply binlog events up to a position/time; requires `sync_binlog=1` and binlog retention, `binlog_expire_logs_seconds`), and **CDC** (M12 — **Debezium**/Maxwell read the binlog as a replica and publish to **Kafka**; requires `binlog_format=ROW`, 10.3). For our domain: the payments ledger's binlog feeds (1) **reporting/standby replicas** (this module), (2) **PITR backups** (M13 — recover the ledger to just before any incident, e.g., a bad migration, M15), and (3) a **CDC stream** (M12 — to feed fraud detection, notifications, analytics, and the **outbox pattern** for reliable cross-service propagation without dual-writes — e.g., reliably notifying an external system of each settled transfer). The same binlog, three uses — the change stream at the heart of the platform. This concept bridges M10 (replication) → M12 (distributed/CDC/outbox) → M13 (PITR), all built on the binlog. ROW format (10.3) + durable binlog (`sync_binlog=1`, 9.10) make all three correct.

## 10.15 · Choosing a replication strategy (the decision)

**Mental model.** Pulling it together: choosing a replication strategy means matching the **topology + sync mode + read routing + failover** to your **requirements** — read scaling, HA, geo-distribution, durability, consistency. There's no one answer; you reason from needs to design. For **most fintech**, the answer converges: **semi-sync** (replica-confirmed durability, 10.4) + **GTID** (robust failover/re-pointing, 10.7) + **ROW format** (no divergence, 10.3) + **automated, fenced failover** (HA without split-brain, 10.10/10.11/10.13) + **careful read routing with read-after-write for money reads** (scale reads safely, 10.6) + **monitoring of lag and semi-sync status** (catch silent failures, 10.12). The decision is a *composition* of the module's choices, driven by what the system must guarantee.

**How it actually works — the decision procedure:**
1. **Read scaling needed?** → add **read replicas** (fan-out, 10.9) + **read routing** (10.6); classify reads by freshness need (RYW for money reads, replicas for reports).
2. **HA / failover needed?** (almost always for money) → **GTID** (10.7) + **automated, fenced failover** (10.10/10.11) + an **orchestration layer** (Orchestrator+ProxySQL or InnoDB Cluster, 10.13).
3. **Durability beyond one node?** (yes for money) → **semi-sync** (10.4 — a committed transfer survives node loss) *or* **group replication** (stronger, consensus); **monitor it isn't silently degraded** (10.12).
4. **Geo-distribution / DR?** → **cross-region replicas** (10.9), accepting cross-region lag (10.5).
5. **Strong consistency required?** → **group replication / InnoDB Cluster** (10.9 — no divergence, structural split-brain prevention) — at latency cost.
6. **Aggregating shards?** (M11) → **multi-source** replica for combined reporting (10.9).
7. **Integration / recovery?** → **binlog for CDC + PITR** (10.14, ROW format, durable binlog).
The output is the full replication design (10.16).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** No single replication configuration fits all systems — a read-heavy content site, a write-heavy ledger, and a globally-distributed service want different topologies, sync modes, and failover strategies. The decision procedure ensures you *reason from requirements* (what must this system guarantee — scale? availability? durability? consistency?) to the *design* (topology + sync + routing + failover), rather than copying a default. For money, it ensures the design actually *delivers* the money-critical guarantees (durability beyond node loss, no divergence, no stale-money-reads, clean failover) — which requires deliberately choosing semi-sync, GTID, ROW, fenced failover, and RYW, and *monitoring* them. It's the synthesis that turns the module's concepts into a coherent, requirement-driven design.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The decision *is* navigating the module's tradeoffs together: **read scaling** (replicas) vs **staleness** (RYW for critical reads, 10.6); **durability** (semi-sync/group) vs **latency** (10.4); **availability** (fast failover) vs **data loss** (the window, 10.10) and **split-brain risk** (fencing, 10.11); **strong consistency** (group replication) vs **throughput/latency** (10.9). The fintech bias: prioritize *correctness/durability* (semi-sync, ROW, GTID, fenced failover, RYW for money) and accept the latency/complexity cost — because losing or staling money is unacceptable. A loss-tolerant system would choose differently (async, looser routing). The meta-tradeoff is *complexity vs guarantees* — a money-safe replicated topology is more complex (semi-sync monitoring, fenced failover, RYW routing) than a simple async fan-out, but the guarantees require it. Choose the guarantees your data's value demands.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Derive the replication design from the guarantees the system must provide — scale, availability, durability, consistency — composing topology + acknowledgment mode + routing + failover to meet them, biasing toward stronger guarantees as the cost of failure rises." This is the universal **requirements-driven distributed-systems design**: you don't pick a topology/consistency level in the abstract; you enumerate the guarantees you need (and what failures you must survive) and design to meet them, accepting the inherent tradeoffs (CAP/PACELC, M12). The transferable insight: **replication (and distributed-system) design is requirements-first — what must survive (node loss? region loss?), what can be stale (reports yes, your own money no?), what latency can you afford — and the design (topology, sync mode, routing, failover) follows; for high-value data (money), bias toward stronger guarantees and *verify* they hold (monitor, 10.12).** This is the same right-size-your-guarantees discipline as M07/7.14 (isolation level) and M09/9.10 (durability matrix), now at the distribution layer.

**MySQL-specific reality.** The fintech-convergent MySQL replication strategy: **`binlog_format=ROW`** (no divergence, 10.3) + **`gtid_mode=ON`** (robust failover, 10.7) + **semi-sync** (`rpl_semi_sync` plugins, replica-confirmed durability, 10.4) *with alerting on `Rpl_semi_sync_source_status`* (catch silent degrade, 10.12) + **`super_read_only=ON`** on replicas (no errant transactions, 10.7/10.11) + **automated fenced failover** (Orchestrator or InnoDB Cluster, 10.10/10.11/10.13) + **read routing** with **read-after-write** for money reads (10.6, via ProxySQL/source-routing/GTID-wait) + **`sync_binlog=1`** (durable binlog for PITR/CDC, 9.10/10.14) + **parallel replication** (keep replicas caught up, 10.8) + **lag/error monitoring** (M13). Alternatively, **InnoDB Cluster** (group replication) for strong-consistency HA with structural split-brain prevention. For our domain: this design gives the payments platform read scaling (replicas for reporting), HA (fenced auto-failover, no split-brain), durability beyond node loss (semi-sync, monitored), no stale-money-reads (RYW), and reliable recovery/integration (PITR + CDC) — the money-safe replicated topology (10.16, M16). Choosing and *operating* this (esp. monitoring the silent failures, 10.12) is the platform's replication design decision.

## 10.16 · Fintech capstone — the replicated payments platform ★

**Mental model.** The capstone assembles the module into **the complete replicated topology for a money system** — and states *what survives node loss and what's protected from stale reads*. The design: **semi-sync** (a committed transfer is durable on ≥1 replica → survives total loss of the primary node, extending M09's posture, 9.16) + **GTID + ROW format + automated fenced failover** (HA with no divergence, no split-brain, no lost money) + **read replicas with careful read-after-write** (scale reporting/reconciliation reads off the primary, but never serve a user a stale balance they just changed) + **monitoring of lag and semi-sync status** (catch the silent failures, 10.12) + **the binlog feeding PITR and CDC** (recovery + integration, 10.14). It's the money-never-lies thread at the distribution layer — a committed payment isn't lost on node loss, isn't read stale, and the ledger never forks — and the setup for M11 (sharding the writes), M12 (distributed/CDC), and M16 (the full platform).

**How it actually works — the replicated payments topology:**
- **Primary** handles all writes (transfers, M07/M08 — the atomic transfer); **semi-sync** (10.4) makes each commit wait for ≥1 replica's receipt → a confirmed transfer is durable beyond the primary's node (survives node/disk/datacenter loss, not just a process crash, M09).
- **Read replicas** serve reporting and **reconciliation** (M02/2.17 — offloading the primary, the read-scaling win), tolerating slight lag (10.5).
- **Read-after-write routing** (10.6): a user's own balance read (after their payment) goes to the **primary** (or a GTID-caught-up replica) — so they see their new balance (no panic-retry double-charge); other reads go to replicas.
- **Standby replica** is the failover target; **GTID** (10.7) + **automated, fenced failover** (10.10/10.11) + **InnoDB Cluster or Orchestrator+ProxySQL** (10.13) → a primary failure triggers fast, fenced, GTID-based promotion (no lost transfers with semi-sync, no split-brain) + automatic re-routing.
- **ROW format** (10.3) → replicas never diverge from non-determinism.
- **Cross-region replica** for DR (survive a region outage); **multi-source aggregation** to combine sharded ledgers (M11) for global reporting.
- **Monitoring** (M13): lag, `Rpl_semi_sync_source_status` (alert on degrade-to-async, 10.12), replica errors, GTID consistency — *verifying* the guarantees hold, not assuming.
- **The binlog** (10.14) also feeds **PITR** (recover the ledger to any point, M13) and **CDC** (propagate ledger changes to fraud/notifications/analytics reliably via outbox, M12).
**What survives:** a confirmed transfer survives primary process crash (M09 redo), power loss (M09 fsync to honest storage), torn page (M09 doublewrite), *and total primary node loss* (semi-sync replica has it) → failover loses *no* confirmed money; the ledger never forks (fencing/quorum); users never see stale-money-reads (RYW).

**Why it exists / what it solves.** The capstone answers, at the distribution layer, the same question M09 answered locally: ***is a committed payment safe, even when a whole node dies?*** — and adds *is it ever read stale?* and *can the ledger fork?* With this topology: a confirmed payment survives node loss (semi-sync — beyond M09's single-node durability), is never read stale by its owner (RYW), and the ledger never diverges (fencing/quorum, no split-brain, ROW format). It extends the money-never-lies guarantee from "survives a single-node crash" (M09) to "survives total node/region loss and never forks or stales" (distributed). It's the synthesis of the whole module — and the foundation M11 (sharding scales the *writes* this replicates), M12 (CDC/distributed generalizes the binlog integration), and M16 (the platform) build on. It's where money becomes safe across the *distribution* of the system, not just one machine.

**Tradeoffs & alternatives.** The capstone embodies the module's tradeoffs, resolved for money: **semi-sync** (durability beyond node loss vs commit latency, 10.4 — money pays the latency); **fenced automated failover** (fast HA vs split-brain risk — fence rigorously, 10.11); **RYW routing** (read scaling vs your-own-money freshness — fresh for money reads, 10.6); **ROW format** (no divergence vs binlog size — money chooses safety, 10.3); **monitoring** (operational cost vs *verifying* guarantees hold, 10.12 — money verifies). The failure alternatives are the money-never-lies catastrophes: async failover losing confirmed transfers (10.10), split-brain forking the ledger (10.11), semi-sync silently degraded so "durable" transfers are lost (10.12), stale-replica reads causing double-charges (10.6). The design biases toward *guarantees* (durability, consistency, no divergence) at latency/complexity cost — because for money, the cost of the failure modes is unacceptable. Sharding (M11) is the next layer (scale the *writes* this topology can't), and CDC (M12) the integration layer.

**Generics / first-principles.** "Make the data safe across the distribution of the system: durable beyond any single node (synchronous acks), never forked (one leader, enforced by fencing/quorum), never read stale where it matters (read-your-writes), and *verify* these guarantees hold (monitor) — so high-value data survives node/region loss without loss, duplication, or divergence." The capstone synthesizes the module's threads — durability (semi-sync extends node-local durability to node-loss survival), money-never-lies (no lost/forked/stale money across the distribution), generics-first (log shipping, leader-election, RYW, fencing/quorum are universal), tradeoff (the CAP/PACELC choices resolved for money). The transferable instinct for *any* high-value distributed data: **durable beyond one node (sync replication), exactly one leader (fencing/consensus, no split-brain), read-your-writes where it matters, and monitored (verify, don't assume) — the cost of these guarantees is justified by the cost of their failure.** This is the distributed-systems realization of money-never-lies, building toward the full platform (M11 sharding, M12 distributed, M16).

**MySQL-specific reality.** The concrete money-safe MySQL replication topology: **`binlog_format=ROW`** + **`gtid_mode=ON`** + **semi-sync** (monitored via `Rpl_semi_sync_source_status`, alert on degrade, 10.12) + **`super_read_only=ON`** on replicas + **`sync_binlog=1`** (9.10, durable binlog) + **automated fenced failover** (InnoDB Cluster *or* Orchestrator + ProxySQL, 10.13) + **parallel replication** (10.8, replicas keep up) + **read-after-write routing** for money reads (10.6) + **cross-region replica** (DR) + **multi-source aggregation** for sharded reporting (M11) + **binlog → PITR + CDC** (10.14, M12/M13) + **lag/error/GTID monitoring** (M13). With this, a confirmed transfer survives primary crash, power loss, torn page (M09), *and* total primary node/region loss (semi-sync replica + DR replica); failover loses no confirmed money (semi-sync + fence + GTID + freshest); the ledger never forks (fence/quorum + ROW); users never read stale balances (RYW); and the platform recovers (PITR) and integrates (CDC) off the binlog. This is the replicated payments platform (M16) — money-never-lies across the distribution. M11 shards the writes this topology replicates; M12 generalizes the binlog integration (CDC/outbox); M15 covers the catastrophic failures when these protections fail. Getting this topology right is *the* distribution-layer correctness foundation of the fintech system. The ★ SVG (Pass C) draws the full annotated topology.
