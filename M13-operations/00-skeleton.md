# M13 · Operations, Backups & Observability — Pass A (Skeleton)

> **Pass A goal:** lock the ordered concept list, a one-line mental model per concept, and the planned diagram + bespoke-SVG inventory + worked example per concept. No prose yet — that's Pass B.
>
> **Module mental model:** *Everything so far made the database* correct *(modeling, ACID, durability) and* scalable *(replication, sharding, distribution). This module makes it* operable *— the unglamorous, mission-critical discipline of running a database in production so it stays available, recoverable, observable, secure, and changeable without downtime. Four pillars: **Backups & recovery** (the* only *thing standing between you and permanent data loss — and a backup you haven't tested restoring is not a backup; **point-in-time recovery** off the binlog (M10) lets you rewind to any moment, e.g. just before a bad deploy). **Schema migrations at scale** (a naive `ALTER TABLE` locks a huge table for hours — **online schema change** tools (gh-ost, pt-online-schema-change) rebuild it in the background and cut over atomically, the* same *copy-sync-cutover pattern as resharding M11/11.14). **Observability** (you can't operate what you can't see — the slow query log, `performance_schema`/`sys`, key metrics, and the early-warning signals that predict trouble before it's an outage: replication lag M10, history-list length M08/M09, checkpoint age M09, connection saturation). **Security & config** (least-privilege auth, TLS in transit, encryption at rest, audit — and the handful of config knobs that actually matter, most already met: buffer pool M09, `innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`/`sync_binlog` M09, the sync mode M10). For fintech, operations is where* money-never-lies *meets reality: a backup that won't restore, a migration that locks the ledger, an unmonitored replica silently degrading, or a leaked credential are how a* correct *system still fails in production. This is the module that keeps the correct, scalable system* alive and recoverable.*
>
> **Threads carried in this module:**
> - **Durability** — operations is where durability is *verified and recoverable*: backups (the durable copy of last resort), PITR (rewind via the binlog, M10), and the discipline that a backup is only real if a *restore* has been tested. Durability config (M09's `flush_log_at_trx_commit`/`sync_binlog`, M10's sync mode) is operated and monitored here.
> - **Money-never-lies** — *the* operational money hazards: a backup that won't restore (the data is gone *and* the backup is too), a migration that locks or corrupts the ledger, an unmonitored semi-sync silently degraded (M10/10.12) so you've lost replica durability without knowing, a leaked credential enabling theft. Operations is where a correct system is kept correct *in production* — and where reconciliation (M02/2.17, M12/12.14) and monitoring are the watchdogs.
> - **Generics-first** — backups (logical/physical/snapshot/incremental), PITR, online schema migration (copy-sync-cutover), observability (metrics/logs/the four golden signals), least-privilege, encryption, and config tuning are universal operational concepts (every database, every production system); MySQL is one instance (XtraBackup, gh-ost, performance_schema, the specific knobs).
> - **Tradeoff** — every operational choice is a tradeoff: backup type (speed vs granularity vs restore time — RPO/RTO), online DDL (zero-downtime but slower/heavier vs fast-but-locking), durability config (throughput vs data-at-risk, M09), monitoring (overhead vs visibility), security (friction vs protection). Operations is applied tradeoff-thinking under real constraints.
>
> **Prereqs:** M09 (durability config — `innodb_flush_log_at_trx_commit`/`sync_binlog`, the buffer pool — operated/tuned here; crash recovery — what a restore complements), M10 (the **binlog** — the basis of PITR and of monitoring replication lag; semi-sync status to monitor, 10.12), M11 (sharding — backups/migrations/monitoring *per shard*, online DDL = the same copy-sync-cutover as resharding 11.14), M12 (reconciliation 12.14 + CDC 12.12 as operational watchdogs/pipelines; the distributed monitoring story), M08 (locking — why naive DDL is catastrophic, MDL; history-list-length to monitor), M02/2.17 (reconciliation — the operational correctness check), M03 (encryption-at-rest of sensitive columns). **Leads into:** **M15 (Failure Modes & Data Loss — the catastrophic side of *every* operational topic: the backup that won't restore, PITR gaps, a migration gone wrong, `innodb_force_recovery`, the unmonitored degradation that became an incident — M13 is "operate it right," M15 is "when operations fail")**, M14 (the cheat-sheet/triage flowcharts distill this module's runbooks), M16 (the platform's operational posture — backup/DR/RPO-RTO, zero-downtime migrations, monitoring, security).

## Concept list (ordered)

| 13.1 | **Why operations matters (the production reality)** | A correct, scalable database still fails in production without operational discipline — backups, recovery, observability, safe change, and security are what keep it *alive and recoverable*. | The four pillars (backup/recovery · safe change · observability · security) | The ways a "correct" payments DB still dies: lost data, locked migration, blind degradation, breach |
| 13.2 | **Backup types: logical, physical, snapshot** ★ | Three ways to copy the database — *logical* (dump the SQL/data — portable, slow restore), *physical* (copy the files — fast restore, less portable), *snapshot* (storage-level point-in-time) — each a different speed/portability/restore tradeoff. ★ | ★ logical vs physical vs snapshot (speed × portability × restore time) | Choosing a backup strategy for the ledger: nightly physical + binlog, vs logical for portability |
| 13.3 | **Point-in-time recovery (PITR) via the binlog** ★ | A full backup restores you to *backup time*; replaying the **binlog** (M10) from there forward (or to a chosen moment) recovers you to *any point* — e.g., the instant *before* a bad deploy dropped a table. ★ | ★ PITR: full backup + binlog replay → any moment (stop-before-the-disaster) | Recovering the ledger to 14:29:59 — one second before a `DELETE` without `WHERE` ran at 14:30 |
| 13.4 | **RPO & RTO: the recovery objectives** | Two numbers define your recovery: **RPO** (how much data you can afford to *lose* — drives backup/replication frequency) and **RTO** (how long you can afford to be *down* — drives backup type + automation). | RPO (data loss tolerance) vs RTO (downtime tolerance) → strategy | Setting RPO≈0 / RTO≈minutes for a payments ledger, and what that demands (semi-sync, fast restore, standby) |
| 13.5 | **The backup you can't restore (tested restores)** ★ | A backup is worthless until a *restore* has been tested — untested backups silently rot (corrupt, incomplete, un-decryptable, missing the binlog) and you discover it *during* the disaster. ★ | The untested-backup trap → restore drills → verified RPO/RTO | The backup that "ran successfully" every night for a year but couldn't restore when needed (M15) |
| 13.6 | **Online schema migrations (the locking problem)** ★ | A naive `ALTER TABLE` on a huge table locks it (or blocks via metadata locks, M08) for hours — **online schema change** rebuilds a shadow copy in the background and cuts over atomically, with near-zero downtime. ★ | ★ naive ALTER (locks) vs online DDL (shadow copy + backfill + cutover) | Adding a column to a billion-row ledger table without locking out transfers — naive vs gh-ost |
| 13.7 | **gh-ost & pt-online-schema-change** | The two online-DDL tools: **pt-osc** (triggers keep a shadow copy in sync) and **gh-ost** (reads the binlog, M10 — no triggers) — both copy → backfill → cut over, the *same* pattern as resharding (M11/11.14). | pt-osc (triggers) vs gh-ost (binlog) — copy/sync/cutover | Migrating the ledger schema with gh-ost: binlog-driven shadow table, throttled, atomic cutover |
| 13.8 | **MySQL's own online DDL (and its limits)** | Modern InnoDB does *some* `ALTER`s online/in-place (`ALGORITHM=INPLACE/INSTANT`) — instant for some changes, in-place for others — but many still copy/lock, so you must *know which* and reach for gh-ost when needed. | InnoDB DDL algorithms: INSTANT / INPLACE / COPY (which locks) | Which ledger schema changes are INSTANT (add column at end) vs need gh-ost (change type, add index on huge table) |
| 13.9 | **Observability: metrics, logs, the golden signals** ★ | You can't operate what you can't see — **metrics** (rates/saturation/errors), **logs** (slow query log, error log), and tracing, organized by the *golden signals* (latency, traffic, errors, saturation) so problems surface *before* they're outages. ★ | ★ the observability stack + the four golden signals | What to watch on a payments DB so a problem is caught early, not as a 3am outage |
| 13.10 | **performance_schema, sys & the slow query log** | MySQL's built-in instrumentation: **`performance_schema`** (low-level instruments), the **`sys`** schema (human-friendly views over it), and the **slow query log** — the tools to find *what's actually slow* and *why*. | P_S → sys views → slow log: finding the slow/contended queries | Using sys/slow-log to find the query causing replica lag and the table causing lock waits |
| 13.11 | **Early-warning signals (predict, don't react)** ★ | The specific metrics that *predict* trouble before it's an outage: replication lag (M10), history-list length (M08/M09), checkpoint age (M09), connection saturation, semi-sync status (M10/10.12), disk space — watch these and you prevent incidents. ★ | ★ the early-warning dashboard (each signal → what it predicts) | The signals that would have caught: a long transaction (HLL), a degrading replica (lag), a filling disk |
| 13.12 | **Connection management & pooling** | Each connection costs memory + a thread; too many exhaust the server (and `max_connections`), too few starve the app — a **connection pool** (app-side or ProxySQL) right-sizes and reuses connections. | Connection pool: app ↔ pool ↔ MySQL (right-size, reuse, queue) | Why a connection storm during a traffic spike took down the payments DB, and how pooling prevents it |
| 13.13 | **Config tuning that actually matters** | A handful of settings dominate (buffer pool size M09, `flush_log_at_trx_commit`/`sync_binlog` M09, sync mode M10, `max_connections`, log sizes) — most defaults are fine; tune the few that matter, measure, don't cargo-cult. | The few knobs that matter (vs the hundreds that don't) | Tuning a payments primary: buffer pool, durability (the money settings), connections — and what to leave alone |
| 13.14 | **Security: auth, TLS, encryption, least privilege, audit** ★ | Defense in depth — **authentication** (strong, per-service accounts), **TLS** (encrypt in transit), **encryption at rest** (data files + backups), **least privilege** (grant only what's needed), and **audit** (who did what) — non-negotiable for money. ★ | ★ the security layers (authn · TLS · at-rest · least-priv · audit) | Securing a payments DB: per-service least-privilege accounts, TLS, encrypted backups, audit trail |
| 13.15 | **Choosing the operational posture (the decision)** | Match backup/DR, migration approach, monitoring, and security to the system's RPO/RTO, scale, and risk — fintech wants near-zero RPO, fast RTO, tested restores, online DDL, deep monitoring, and strong security. | Decision: requirements (RPO/RTO/risk) → operational posture | Designing the full operational posture for a payments platform from its RPO/RTO/compliance needs |
| 13.16 | **Fintech capstone: the operable payments platform** ★ | The complete operational posture — tested physical backups + binlog PITR (RPO≈0), online schema change, deep monitoring with early-warning signals, reconciliation watchdog (M12/12.14), least-privilege + TLS + encryption + audit — so the correct, scaled platform stays alive, recoverable, and secure. ★ | ★ the operable payments platform (backup/PITR · monitoring · migration · security) | End-to-end: how the platform is backed up, monitored, migrated, and secured (sets up M15/M16) |
